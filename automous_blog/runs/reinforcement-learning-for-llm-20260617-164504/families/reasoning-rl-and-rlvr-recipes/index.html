Reasoning RL with Verifiable Rewards

Reasoning RL asks a narrow but important question: if you can automatically check whether an answer is right, can reinforcement learning teach a language model to reason better? That is the appeal of RLVR, or reinforcement learning from verifiable rewards. Instead of collecting step-by-step human labels, you give the model problems with answers that can be checked, reward successful trajectories, and hope the policy learns better ways to think.

The difficulty is that correctness alone is a thin signal. A final answer can be right or wrong while hiding everything that happened in the middle. That makes this subtopic less about one shared algorithm and more about a shared interface problem: how to turn sparse verification into useful training pressure without wasting rollouts, biasing the optimizer, collapsing exploration, or teaching the model to overthink.

The Recurring Training Loop

Across these recipes, the recurring shape is:

That pipeline matters because each stage compensates for a weakness in the next one.

  • Prompt curation gives RL tasks that can actually be verified.
  • A long chain-of-thought warmup gives the model room to explore, recover from mistakes, and expose reasoning that reward can reinforce later.
  • RL optimization shifts probability mass toward trajectories that end in correct, verifiable answers.
  • Deployment and compression turn expensive long-trace behavior into something cheaper and more stable for real use.

Some recipes keep this loop close to the standard post-training stack. DeepSeek-R1 uses a staged path from RL to cleanup and distillation. Others stretch the loop. Kimi k1.5 pushes long-context RL directly, while Absolute Zero Reasoner expands the front of the pipeline by generating its own verifiable tasks before optimization even starts.

Why Sparse Verification Is Hard

The verifier usually sees only the end state: the answer passes or fails. That creates several bottlenecks at once.

First, rollouts are expensive. Reasoning traces are long, and long traces are exactly where RL is supposed to help. But if every attempt is costly, training can spend huge amounts of compute on trajectories that are redundant, low quality, or impossible to reuse.

Second, the reward is sparse. Final-answer correctness says little about whether the model found a robust path, guessed correctly, or learned a brittle formatting trick. Sparse rewards can improve pass@1 while leaving the model’s actual reasoning boundary mostly unchanged.

Third, the optimizer can quietly distort the lesson. If the loss overweights easy questions, long responses, or template artifacts, the training loop may look like reasoning improvement while mostly learning verbosity or answer style.

Fourth, exploration is fragile. Too little exploration and RL just sharpens familiar paths. Too much exploration and training becomes noisy, wasteful, or incoherent. Even when the model already contains a short correct path, naive decoding may keep it talking long after it should stop.

The Design Axes That Matter

Because there is no single shared mechanism here, it helps to look at the main axes these recipes tune.

How Much Reasoning Context To Keep

One axis is context length. Long-context recipes treat reasoning as a trajectory worth preserving rather than compressing immediately. Kimi k1.5 illustrates the logic: if the model has enough room to backtrack, try alternate paths, and recover within one trajectory, RL can reward the whole attempt instead of only the shortest clean solution. The cost is obvious: long-context training is expensive, and overthinking needs explicit control.

This is also where compression enters. Long traces may be useful during learning even if they are too expensive for deployment. That is why long-to-short transfer and later distillation show up as a recurring recipe choice rather than an afterthought.

What The Reward Actually Teaches

Another axis is reward design. In this family, the default reward is verifiable final-answer correctness. That is attractive because it scales, but it does not automatically produce readable or well-structured reasoning.

DeepSeek-R1 is a clear example. Its lesson is not just that RL can work with rule-based rewards, but that sparse correctness often needs help from a staged pipeline. A small cold-start supervised seed can make learned traces more readable, rejection-sampled traces can clean up the intermediate data, and distillation can package the behavior into smaller dense models. The broader point is that verifiable reward can discover reasoning behavior, but auxiliary stages often make that behavior usable.

1-Shot RLVR pushes the same point from another angle. If the model already has latent reasoning ability, even one carefully chosen verifiable example can redirect search productively. That does not mean data no longer matters. It means reward usefulness depends heavily on example leverage, not just on dataset size.

How The Optimizer Weighs Trajectories

The update rule is another major axis. Many reasoning recipes avoid a learned critic and instead use group-relative or regularized policy updates, but the details matter because small normalizations can change what the model learns.

Dr. GRPO is valuable here because it makes the hidden failure mode explicit. If the objective divides by response length or by per-question variance in the wrong way, the optimizer can reward verbosity or distort difficulty weighting. In that case, longer wrong answers may receive favorable treatment, and apparent gains may come from formatting artifacts rather than better reasoning. The conceptual lesson is simple: when reward is already sparse, optimizer bias is especially dangerous because there is so little signal available to correct it.

One useful mental model is that RL for reasoning is not only choosing answers. It is choosing which trajectories deserve extra probability mass. Biased weighting changes that allocation.

Whether RL Expands Capacity Or Just Sharpens Sampling

An uncomfortable axis in RLVR is the difference between sampling efficiency and true capability growth. Better pass@1 is not the same thing as a wider reasoning boundary.

The pass@k boundary analysis argues that current RLVR often improves low-sample success by concentrating probability on paths the base model already knew, while reducing coverage at large k. That reframes a lot of optimism around reasoning RL. Sparse binary rewards are good at reweighting familiar successful traces, but weaker at discovering genuinely new solution modes.

This is why distillation appears in this family as more than compression. If a stronger teacher transfers broader capability into a smaller model, distillation can move the boundary in a way that plain RLVR often does not.

How To Keep Exploration Alive Without Wasting Tokens

Exploration is where inference and training start to blur together. If reasoning RL depends on sampled trajectories, then decoding policy is part of the learning system, not just a deployment detail.

SAGE shows one response to overthinking. Instead of using a fixed length budget, it explores multiple reasoning paths and asks whether the model is already confident enough to stop. The core lesson is that efficient reasoning may already exist inside the model, but ordinary decoding can hide it under unnecessary tokens. Search can surface shorter, high-confidence chains, and mixed RL rollouts can teach the policy to prefer them.

IntroLLM makes the same issue more explicit by turning temperature into a learned control policy. Rather than fixing exploration by hand, it lets the model raise entropy at uncertain pivots and lower it during precise execution. That is a useful design shift: in reasoning RL, exploration control can itself be trained from reward.

When The Data Pipeline Becomes The Real Bottleneck

RLVR sounds data-light because it replaces rich annotation with verifiability. In practice, the bottleneck often moves rather than disappears.

One version is narrow data scarcity: 1-Shot RLVR shows that example selection can matter more than dataset size when the model already contains the relevant skill. Another version is curated task scarcity: Absolute Zero Reasoner asks what happens when the model helps generate its own training distribution inside a grounded executor. In both cases, the lesson is that the reward signal is only as useful as the task distribution feeding it.

That is why prompt curation remains in the pipeline even for methods that want to minimize human supervision. Someone, or something, still has to decide what problems are worth practicing.

What The Evidence Supports

The compact input supports a few clear takeaways.

  • Long-context RL can produce strong reasoning behavior and later compress it. Kimi k1.5 reports a long-CoT model at 77.5 on AIME 2024 and 96.2 on MATH-500, while its short-CoT transfer keeps 94.6 on MATH-500.
  • A staged RL recipe can turn sparse rule-based rewards into large gains. DeepSeek-R1-Zero is reported to improve AIME 2024 pass@1 from 15.6% to 71.0%, and DeepSeek-R1 reaches 97.3% on MATH-500.
  • Better sampling and optimization matter, not just more rollout volume. The input reports that curriculum sampling improves over uniform sampling, and that negative gradients improve sample complexity versus ReST.

That evidence is enough to support the tutorial point: reasoning RL works when the pipeline compensates for sparse reward, but the improvement usually comes from recipe design around the reward, not from binary verification alone.

What To Remember

Reasoning RL is best understood as infrastructure for making sparse correctness signals usable. The common theme is not a single algorithm. It is a shared interface problem between verifier, rollout, optimizer, and decoder.

If you remember one thing, remember this: verifiable rewards are powerful because they scale, but they are weak teachers by themselves. The successful recipes add context, reuse, staging, exploration control, and compression so that a thin final-answer signal can shape useful reasoning behavior.
