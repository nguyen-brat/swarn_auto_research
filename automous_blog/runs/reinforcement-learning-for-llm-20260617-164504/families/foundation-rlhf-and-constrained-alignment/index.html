How RLHF Turns Base Models into Constrained Assistants

A pretrained language model is good at continuing text, but that is not the same as being a reliable assistant. It may ignore the user’s intent, answer harmful requests too eagerly, sound convincing when it is wrong, or learn to please a reward model instead of a person. The central problem in this subtopic is how to take a broad base model and reshape it into something more helpful, safer, and more faithful without breaking the capabilities that made the model useful in the first place.

The common theme is not a single algorithm. It is a shared interface problem. All of these methods work around the same post-training interface: humans can judge outputs more easily than they can write a full objective, so alignment becomes a loop for collecting judgments, learning from them, and then constraining optimization so the model does not drift into strange behavior.

Reward modeling turns human rankings into a trainable signal. Policy optimization pushes the model toward responses that score better under that signal. Evaluation checks whether the model became more useful in practice or merely better at exploiting the proxy.

This is the intuition behind the RLHF / InstructGPT pipeline: first show the model examples of good responses, then learn a scorer from ranked outputs, then optimize against that scorer while staying near the supervised model. A compact way to express that leash is:

Where Alignment Gets Hard

The first bottleneck is misaligned instruction following. Next-token pretraining does not teach the model which part of a prompt is the real task, when to refuse, or how to weigh truthfulness against fluency. A model can sound polished while still missing user intent.

The second bottleneck is reward misalignment. Once a learned reward model stands in for human judgment, optimization starts chasing a proxy. That proxy may work well near the data it saw during training and then become brittle as the policy moves into new regions.

The third bottleneck is coarse supervision. Labels like “better answer” or “worse answer” are often too blunt. They do not explain whether a response failed because it was unsupported, unsafe, off-topic, or evasive.

The fourth bottleneck is reward hacking and instability. Text generation is a long sequence of decisions, so small optimization mistakes compound. Without strong regularization, a policy can raise reward while lowering fluency, faithfulness, or general usefulness.

There is also a practical pressure point after alignment: the alignment tax. A model can become more preferred in chat and worse at other capabilities. That means post-training is not just about behavior control. It is also about retaining broad competence.

The Knobs That Shape RLHF

This family is easiest to understand through its design axes rather than through method names.

One axis is the optimizer. PPO is the common workhorse because it gives an online update rule for improving policy behavior from reward-model scores. But the real lesson is not “use PPO.” It is that post-training needs an optimizer that can push behavior while tolerating noisy rewards.

Another axis is regularization. KL-to-SFT is the standard stabilizer because it keeps the aligned policy close to a known-good starting point. That is why later theory-oriented work such as GSHF still centers KL-regularized preference alignment: the hard problem is controlled movement, not unconstrained maximization.

A third axis is the signal itself. Human rankings are richer than hard labels, but they still hide multiple goals inside one preference. That is why the setup keeps running into helpfulness-versus-harmlessness conflicts, proxy drift, and disagreement across users.

A fourth axis is stability. Some systems improve behavior only to lose general capability. Others become unstable because the reward is sparse or easy to exploit. This is why choices such as pretraining mix during PPO, online refresh, token-level constraints, and post-RLHF merging show up as structural decisions, not implementation trivia.

When One Reward Is Too Crude

Early RLHF showed that broad human rankings can dramatically improve instruction following, but it also exposed an uncomfortable fact: a single reward number often mixes together usefulness, politeness, safety, truthfulness, and style. That makes it hard to tell what the model is actually learning.

The helpfulness-harmlessness setup in Preference modeling + PPO RLHF makes that tradeoff visible. The tutorial lesson is that alignment does not fail only because the optimizer is weak. It also fails when the target is under-specified. If the model is rewarded for what raters usually prefer, it may still become fragile when pushed toward very high reward.

That motivates a more explicit constrained view. Safe RLHF separates reward and safety into different learned signals, then treats safety like a cost instead of burying it inside one scalar objective. This changes the teaching story from “find the best answer” to “find the best answer that stays within a safety budget.”

The same idea appears in a more diagnostic form in Sparrow. Instead of asking for one overall judgment, it uses rule-conditioned supervision and evidence-aware behavior to expose why a response failed. That is useful when supervision is too coarse: breaking the target into human-readable rules makes failure modes easier to measure and harder to hide.

Keeping the Model Useful While You Optimize It

Once alignment is framed as optimization under constraints, a new question appears: how far can you push before the model starts gaming the objective or forgetting useful skills?

RL4LMs + NLPO is a good example of the instability side of the problem. Its value is conceptual: language generation is a token-level control problem, so reward must be balanced against fluency and prompt faithfulness at every step. Warm starts, KL control, and restricted exploration matter because unconstrained RL can produce high-reward nonsense.

On the retention side, Heterogeneous Model Averaging treats alignment as something that can be partly repaired after RLHF. Instead of assuming the post-training checkpoint is final, it interpolates pre- and post-RLHF weights, with different ratios across model parts.

That figure is useful because it highlights a broader design axis: alignment is not only about the reward loop itself, but also about where in the model behavior changes are stored. If early and late layers play different roles, post-training may need layer-specific repair rather than one global merge.

What the Evidence Supports

The compact input gives strongest direct evidence for the basic RLHF loop and its main failure controls.

InstructGPT is the cleanest anchor result: its 175B outputs were preferred to 175B GPT-3 outputs 85 +/- 3% of the time. That supports the core claim that preference-based post-training can reliably turn a base model into a better assistant.

The same line of work reports that PPO models were about twice as often truthful and informative as GPT-3 on TruthfulQA. That matters because it shows RLHF is not only teaching politeness or formatting; it can shift answer quality on a truthfulness-sensitive evaluation.

The helpful-harmless line adds a second anchor: the online model was preferred over professional writers about 57% of the time. The important lesson is less the exact number than the fact that iterative refresh can improve behavior beyond a one-shot offline preference pass.

Finally, the pretraining-mix result is a key structural piece of evidence: mixing pretraining updates during PPO reduced regressions on public NLP tasks. That is the clearest support here for the claim that stability and capability retention are first-class alignment concerns, not cleanup details.

For some later ideas in this family, the compact input gives thinner direct evidence than it gives for the original RLHF foundations. Even so, the direction is consistent: separate the objectives when one score hides a conflict, add more diagnostic supervision when preferences are too coarse, and keep optimization close to a trusted reference when rewards are imperfect.

What to Remember

RLHF is best understood as constrained post-training for behavior, not as a magic reward trick. The model starts from a strong generative prior, learns what humans prefer through demonstrations and rankings, and then gets optimized under a leash so it does not wander too far from fluent, capable behavior.

The enduring challenge is that the leash and the reward are both imperfect. If supervision is too coarse, the system cannot explain its own failures. If the reward is too narrow, optimization exploits it. If the constraint is too weak, the model drifts. If the constraint is too strong, it stops improving. The most useful methods in this family each make one part of that tension more explicit.
