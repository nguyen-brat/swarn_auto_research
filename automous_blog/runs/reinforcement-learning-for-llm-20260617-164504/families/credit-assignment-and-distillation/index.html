How RLVR Learns Which Steps Matter

Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards looks deceptively simple: sample an answer, score it, and push the policy toward better outputs. The trouble is that most long reasoning traces are not uniformly important. Some tokens only keep the sentence flowing. Some steps commit the model to the right branch. Some failures are visible only after many turns. Some environments return rich feedback that a single scalar reward throws away. The real question in this subtopic is not “how do we optimize RLVR?” but “how do we tell the optimizer where the useful signal actually lives?”

That is why the common theme here is not a single algorithm. It is a shared interface problem. RLVR has to connect rollout collection to policy update without losing the structure inside a trajectory. Different methods attack different breakpoints in that interface: step credit assignment, wasted gradient budget, scalar reward bottlenecks, and noisy teacher supervision.

The Common Loop

Most of these methods start from the same basic training loop:

The loop is short on paper, but each arrow hides a hard decision. During rollout collection, the model produces a long chain of states, actions, and tokens. During advantage estimation, the trainer must decide whether to assign credit at the whole-trajectory level, at the step level, or at the token level. During the policy update, it must decide whether the signal should come from a verifier reward, a teacher distribution, feedback-conditioned self-distillation, or some combination.

This is why credit assignment and distillation often appear together. Distillation is not only about imitating a teacher. In RLVR, it often serves as a way to inject denser local information into a rollout that would otherwise be judged by one delayed outcome.

Why the Signal Gets Lost

The first failure mode is delayed reward. If a model makes a bad decision halfway through a multi-step task, a final reward says the answer failed but does not say which choice caused the failure.

The second failure mode is wasted gradient budget. Long chains of thought contain many low-information tokens. Updating all of them equally can spend compute on fluent scaffolding instead of on the tokens that actually choose a reasoning path.

The third failure mode is compression. Environments often expose richer evidence than a single pass/fail scalar: compiler errors, judge feedback, tool traces, or sibling solutions. If training collapses all of that into one number, it throws away localization.

The fourth failure mode is supervision mismatch. Dense teacher signals can help, but only if they are stable on the student’s own rollouts. If the teacher’s support is too far away, or if one sampled token carries too much of the update, distillation becomes noisy or brittle.

Four Design Axes

The embedded methods vary along a few recurring axes.

How wide is the credit scope?

One choice is whether to assign credit to the whole episode, to individual steps, or to specific tokens. Episode-level credit is cheap and robust, but coarse. Step-level credit is better for agent trajectories with delayed rewards. Token-level credit is better when the important decision is buried inside a long text span.

Do we need extra rollouts?

A practical design goal is to recover finer credit without paying for many additional environment interactions. Several methods here are attractive because they reuse on-policy rollouts rather than branching into expensive counterfactual sampling.

What counts as a trustworthy local target?

Some methods trust repeated states across trajectories. Some trust high-entropy tokens as decision points. Some trust feedback-conditioned self-teachers. Some trust only a truncated teacher-approved support. The local target matters because RLVR improves only when the finer signal is more informative than the original scalar reward.

Where should privileged information enter?

A teacher can set the whole update direction, provide dense token targets, or merely reweight a reward-anchored update. This is one of the most important safety and stability choices in the family.

Recovering Credit Inside Long Rollouts

In multi-turn settings, the main issue is often not exploration but localization. GiGPO illustrates this well. Its idea is simple: keep the coarse trajectory score, but recover local credit by grouping actions that occurred from the same state across different rollouts. If multiple trajectories revisit a shared state, the trainer can compare what happened next and estimate step-level relative advantage without launching new rollouts.

One compact way to express that idea is:

The point of the equation is not the algebra. It is the design principle: do not throw away the stable whole-trajectory score, but do not stop there when repeated states reveal where the decision really happened.

This is useful only when state matching is plausible. Exact or similarity-based matching works best when trajectories revisit recognizable anchor states. If the environment is too noisy or rarely revisits the same state, the extra structure weakens and the method slides back toward ordinary group-based RL.

When Full-Sequence Updates Waste Compute

Not every token in a chain of thought deserves equal learning pressure. Forking-token DAPO makes the argument concrete: a small set of high-entropy tokens often carries most of the useful RL signal because those are the points where the model is actually choosing between branches.

This reframes credit assignment as a filtering problem. Instead of asking “which rollout was good?”, ask “which parts of the rollout were making real decisions?” Low-entropy tokens may still be necessary for readable reasoning, but they are often poor targets for expensive policy-gradient updates. Concentrating updates on high-entropy forking tokens is a way to preserve exploration while trimming wasted gradient budget.

The lesson generalizes beyond one masking rule. When long reasoning traces mix deterministic filler with uncertain branch points, sparse but targeted updates can be more useful than uniform sequence-wide learning.

Turning Rich Feedback Into Dense Supervision

A final scalar reward is often the coarsest possible summary of what happened. SDPO teaches a different move: if the environment already gives rich feedback, let the current model reread its own attempt with that feedback attached, then distill the feedback-aware next-token distribution back into the student. That turns runtime feedback into dense local supervision without requiring a separate external teacher.

This changes what “distillation” means. The goal is not to train a smaller student to copy a stronger model. The goal is to transform retrospective information into token-level credit that a pure scalar reward cannot provide. In that view, self-distillation becomes a credit-assignment device.

SRPO adds a useful refinement: do not force one supervision style onto every sample. Correct rollouts already have the right global direction and can go through reward optimization. Incorrect rollouts benefit more from dense corrective hints, but only when teacher information is available and reliable. That sample-level routing acknowledges that different failures deserve different learning signals.

Making Teacher Signals Local, Stable, and Safe

Dense teacher supervision helps only when it is well matched to the student’s actual trajectory distribution. Teacher top-K local support matching tackles one stability problem directly: a single sampled token is too fragile a carrier for the teacher signal. Its answer is to widen supervision from one sampled token to a small teacher-selected support set, then match student and teacher locally inside that set. The conceptual move is important: sequence-level distillation may be too noisy, but token-level distillation still needs enough neighborhood structure to be trustworthy.

RLSD addresses a different issue: privileged information leakage. If the teacher decides the gradient direction, the student may absorb hidden information in ways that destabilize on-policy learning. RLSD uses teacher evidence only to scale token importance, while the verifier reward still decides whether the trajectory is reinforced or penalized. That separation is a strong design pattern for RLVR: let privileged signals modulate magnitude, not outcome sign.

OPD diagnosis and recovery explains the deeper reason these choices matter. On-policy distillation works when the student can already reach the teacher’s useful support and the teacher still contains information the student lacks. A better teacher score alone is not enough. If the teacher lives in a different reasoning mode, dense supervision can become weak, misaligned, or even regressive. In that sense, the bottleneck is not just reward sparsity. It is teacher-student compatibility on visited states.

What the Evidence Supports

The compact input gives only selective direct evidence, but it is enough to anchor the core story.

GiGPO reports gains over GRPO of more than 12% on ALFWorld and more than 9% on WebShop, which supports the claim that finer step credit can matter in long-horizon agent settings. It also reports that step-state grouping adds almost no overhead, suggesting that better localization does not have to require expensive extra rollouts.

Forking-token DAPO reports that using only the top 20% high-entropy tokens can match or exceed full-token DAPO, which supports the idea that much of the sequence carries little marginal RL signal.

SDPO reports higher aggregate final accuracy than a strong GRPO baseline on science and tool-use tasks, consistent with the claim that rich feedback can break the scalar reward bottleneck when the model can retrospectively interpret its mistakes.

SRPO reports gains over both GRPO and SDPO, which supports a narrower lesson: the best supervision signal may depend on whether the current sample is already correct or still needs dense corrective guidance.

What to Remember

Credit assignment and distillation for RLVR are best understood as ways of deciding where learning should happen inside a rollout. The important divide is not “reward methods versus distillation methods.” The more useful divide is:

  • methods that localize delayed credit across steps,
  • methods that stop wasting updates on low-information tokens,
  • methods that convert rich feedback into dense learning signals,
  • methods that keep teacher guidance local enough, safe enough, and aligned enough to help.

The durable takeaway is that RLVR improves when it stops treating every token, every step, and every supervision source as equally informative. Better training comes from finding the narrow interfaces where the real decisions happen, then spending gradient budget there.
