High-Entropy Minority Tokens Drive Effective RLVR

The paper’s central idea is simple once you see it: a chain-of-thought is not equally informative at every token.

Most tokens in a reasoning trace are just carrying the sentence forward. They finish a word, complete a symbol, or keep a derivation moving along a path that is already obvious. A much smaller set of tokens are different. Those tokens sit at branch points, where the model is genuinely uncertain about which direction to take next. The paper calls them forking tokens.

The key claim is that RLVR spends too much of its gradient budget on the boring majority and not enough on the uncertain minority. If you concentrate policy-gradient updates on the high-entropy tokens instead, training can become more effective, not less.

The shape of the problem

Think of a long chain-of-thought like a trail through a forest. Some steps are just walking straight on the same path. Others are the moments where you choose left or right at a fork.

Standard RLVR updates every token in the sequence more or less uniformly. That is convenient, but it blurs together two very different kinds of tokens:

  • low-entropy tokens that mostly continue an already chosen reasoning route,
  • high-entropy tokens that decide which reasoning route to take next.

The paper argues that this is a poor credit-assignment match for reasoning models. If the real learning signal lives at the forks, then spreading updates across the full sequence wastes effort on tokens that are already deterministic.

Here is the distribution the authors observe in large CoT samples:

What to notice is the skew. The histogram is not just “a bit” uneven. It is sharply concentrated near very low entropy, with a much smaller high-entropy tail. That is the whole premise of the method: the useful decision points are rare.

What the high-entropy tokens actually look like

The paper does not stop at the histogram. It also asks what these tokens mean in the text.

High-entropy tokens often behave like connective tissue between reasoning steps: words such as “however,” “thus,” “because,” or “suppose,” and in math, tokens that introduce assumptions or pivot to a new step. Low-entropy tokens, by contrast, are often just filling in the local structure of the sentence or expression.

That distinction matters because it explains why the entropy signal is useful. The model is not uncertain about every token in the same way. It is uncertain at the points where the next logical move is not yet fixed.

So the paper gives these uncertain tokens a name: forking tokens.

The method as a pipeline

The training recipe is easiest to understand as a short pipeline:

  1. generate chain-of-thought rollouts,
  2. compute per-token entropy from the model’s output distribution,
  3. mark the top-entropy tokens as forks,
  4. mask out gradient updates on the rest,
  5. train with DAPO-style RLVR on the remaining tokens.

That is the essence of forking-token DAPO.

The important detail is that the entropy is computed from the generation distribution at each position, not from the sampled token itself. In other words, the method is asking, “how uncertain was the model at this spot?” rather than “what token happened to be sampled?”

Once that uncertainty score exists, the paper turns it into a batch-level threshold. Within each batch, only the top 20% highest-entropy tokens are allowed to contribute to the policy-gradient loss. The rest are ignored for the update.

That sounds aggressive, but it is the point. The method is deliberately saying: the low-entropy majority is not where the interesting learning signal lives.

Why the entropy matters during decoding

Before changing the training objective, the paper checks whether these high-entropy tokens really behave like decision points at inference time.

The authors vary the decoding temperature separately for forking tokens and for the rest of the sequence. The result is very revealing: changing the temperature of the high-entropy tokens matters much more than changing the temperature of the low-entropy ones.

In other words, the model’s quality is sensitive to how much freedom those fork points have. If you suppress them, performance drops. If you let them breathe a little, reasoning improves.

That is a strong hint that the entropy signal is not a cosmetic artifact. It is tracking places where exploration actually matters.

RLVR mostly preserves the same structure

The next question is whether RLVR changes the entropy landscape in a deep way or just tweaks it a little.

The paper’s answer is: it mostly preserves the base model’s pattern.

RLVR does not turn the whole sequence into a high-entropy mess. Instead, it mainly adjusts the already high-entropy positions, while the low-entropy tokens stay comparatively stable. The following figure makes that easier to see:

What to notice here is the trend across entropy percentiles. Tokens that start out more uncertain tend to change more during RLVR. The low-entropy end barely moves. So the training process is not flattening the whole distribution; it is sharpening or reshaping the places that were already important.

That observation is the bridge to the main training idea. If RLVR already concentrates its effect on high-entropy tokens, then explicitly masking low-entropy tokens may simply make that natural bias cleaner and more efficient.

The actual optimization trick

The paper builds its main objective by taking DAPO and deleting the gradient contribution of tokens below the entropy threshold.

The practical effect is straightforward:

  • keep the DAPO machinery,
  • keep clip-higher,
  • keep dynamic sampling,
  • keep token-level policy gradients,
  • drop KL and entropy-loss terms,
  • update only on the top-entropy tokens.

So the method is not a new RL algorithm from scratch. It is a targeted rewrite of where the learning signal flows.

This matters because it preserves the basic RLVR setup while changing the credit assignment rule. Instead of asking every token to carry equal weight, it asks only the tokens that are actually making branching decisions to carry the update.

That is a cleaner fit for CoT reasoning, where much of the sequence is scaffolding and only a minority of tokens changes the path.

Why the top 20% is the sweet spot

The paper’s best-performing setting is not “keep as few tokens as possible.” It is more specific: keep about the top 20% highest-entropy tokens per batch.

Why not 10%? Because you start throwing away some useful branch points.

Why not 50% or 100%? Because you start pulling low-entropy filler back into the gradient, which dilutes the exploration signal.

The paper’s ablations suggest that the method works because it balances two competing pressures:

  • enough high-entropy tokens to keep exploration alive,
  • enough masking to avoid wasting updates on deterministic text.

That balance is especially visible in the entropy curves. The top-20% setting keeps overall entropy healthier during training than broader token sets, which is one reason it can outperform vanilla DAPO.

What the main results are really saying

The headline result is not just that the method works. It is that the improvement comes from a minority of tokens.

On Qwen3-8B, using only the top 20% high-entropy tokens matches or beats full-token DAPO. On larger models, the gains become more pronounced. On Qwen3-32B, the method improves AIME’24 by 7.71 points and AIME’25 by 11.04 points over full-token DAPO, and the performance continues to scale when the response length is extended further.

The paper also shows the opposite direction: if you train only on the bottom 80% low-entropy tokens, performance drops sharply.

That contrast is the most important evidence in the paper. It says the signal is not “any subset of tokens will do.” The signal is specifically in the tokens that represent uncertainty and branching.

Why scaling seems to help

One of the more interesting patterns in the paper is that larger models benefit more from this strategy.

That makes intuitive sense if you think about what the method is doing. Bigger reasoning models are more capable of sustaining multiple candidate paths and making productive use of exploration. If high-entropy tokens are the branch points where alternate reasoning trajectories emerge, then a larger model has more room to exploit that extra flexibility.

What to notice is that the gap between top-token training and vanilla DAPO widens as model size grows. So the method is not just a clever pruning trick; it seems to line up with the model’s capacity to use exploration well.

Why this is more than a math benchmark trick

The experiments are math-heavy, but the paper does a useful extra check: it looks at out-of-domain transfer.

Even when the method is trained on math data, the top-entropy-token version still improves performance on LiveCodeBench relative to vanilla DAPO. That does not prove universal transfer, but it suggests the entropy signal is capturing something more structural than “math-only quirks.”

The paper also tries a non-Qwen setup with Llama-3.1-8B after a cold-start SFT phase. The result is less convincing overall, but the same direction still holds: forking-token training beats full-token DAPO. That is enough to say the idea is not Qwen-exclusive, while still being honest that Qwen is where the evidence is strongest.

How to remember the method

The cleanest way to remember forking-token DAPO is this:

RLVR on reasoning traces is not a uniform token game. It is a branch-point game.

Most tokens just keep the sentence or equation moving. A small minority decide which reasoning branch the model takes next. Forking-token DAPO measures that uncertainty with entropy, keeps the highest-entropy tokens, and concentrates policy-gradient updates on them.

If you want the one-line intuition, it is this:

when a sequence mixes deterministic scaffolding with true decision points, learn mostly from the decision points.

What to keep in mind

The paper’s contribution is partly diagnostic and partly algorithmic.

On the diagnostic side, it shows that token entropy in CoT reasoning is highly skewed, and that RLVR mostly acts on the high-entropy tail. On the algorithmic side, it turns that observation into a training rule: mask out low-entropy tokens and update only the forks.

The limitations are also clear. The strongest validation is on Qwen-family reasoning models, the main benchmark suite is math-centric, and the best token fraction may vary across settings. The method also assumes you can afford token-level entropy scoring.

Still, the main lesson is robust and easy to reuse:

if a reasoning trace has many fluent filler tokens and a few uncertain branch points, the branch points deserve most of the learning signal.
