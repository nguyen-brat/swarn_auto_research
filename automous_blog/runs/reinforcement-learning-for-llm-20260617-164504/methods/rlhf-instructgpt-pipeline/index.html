Training language models to follow instructions with human feedback

The main idea in InstructGPT is simple, but it changes the whole training story for a language model.

Instead of asking a pretrained model to become helpful just by scaling up next-token prediction, the paper teaches it what good answers look like, learns a scorer from human preferences, and then uses that scorer to steer the model further. In other words, the model does not only learn language. It learns which responses humans actually prefer when they ask for help.

That matters because a pretrained LM is optimized to continue text, not to cooperate with a user. A model can be fluent and still be unhelpful, evasive, untruthful, or toxic. The InstructGPT pipeline is the paper’s answer to that mismatch.

The pipeline in one picture

If you want the entire method at a glance, this is the core loop:

What to notice is the shape of the method. The first step creates a supervised starting policy from demonstrations. The second step turns human rankings into a reward model. The third step uses reinforcement learning to improve the policy against that learned reward, while keeping it anchored to the supervised model.

That “anchored” part is not decoration. It is what keeps the policy from drifting too far while it is being optimized.

Start with examples, not just a score

The first stage is supervised fine-tuning, or SFT. Human labelers write demonstrations of the desired behavior for prompts drawn from two places: prompts people submitted through the OpenAI API Playground and prompts written by labelers themselves to bootstrap the process.

This is the easiest part of the pipeline to understand. If you want a model to answer in the style you want, show it good answers first.

But SFT alone is not enough. A demonstration tells the model what one good completion looks like. It does not yet teach the model how to rank several possible completions, or how to trade off helpfulness, truthfulness, and safety when those goals compete. That is why the paper adds a second stage.

The SFT model is also the starting point for everything that follows. The paper finds that training longer than a single epoch can help the reward-model score and human preference ratings, even if validation loss starts to overfit. So the starting point is not chosen by raw loss alone; it is chosen by whether the model better matches what humans prefer.

Turn human preferences into a reward model

The second stage collects comparison data. For a prompt, labelers rank multiple model outputs from best to worst. The paper uses those rankings to train a reward model, or RM, that predicts which response humans would prefer.

This is the clever move in the pipeline. Human feedback is expensive, so you do not want to ask labelers to score every new model output forever. Instead, you train a separate model to imitate those judgments.

That reward model becomes a learned proxy for preference.

The training setup is pairwise: if one completion is preferred over another, the reward model should assign the preferred completion a higher scalar reward. Because labelers often rank 4 to 9 outputs at once, the paper turns each ranking task into many pairwise comparisons and trains on all of them together. That gives a stronger learning signal than treating each comparison as isolated noise.

The paper also makes a practical choice here: it uses a single 6B reward model across policy sizes. That keeps the system cheaper and more stable than trying to run a giant reward model for every policy. It also avoids a second source of scaling complexity when the goal is really to study the policy itself.

Use PPO to push the policy toward what humans like

Once the reward model is in place, the final stage is reinforcement learning.

The policy sees a prompt, produces a response, and gets a scalar reward from the reward model. Then PPO updates the policy so it is more likely to produce responses that score well.

There is one more piece that matters a lot: a KL penalty keeps the policy close to the SFT model. Without that constraint, the policy can exploit weaknesses in the reward model and drift into weird, over-optimized behavior. The paper explicitly treats this as a bandit-style setup, where each prompt-response pair gets a reward and the episode ends immediately.

So the real optimization target is not just “maximize reward.” It is “maximize reward while staying near the supervised policy.”

That is the balancing act at the heart of RLHF.

If the reward model says “short, direct, and polite” but the KL penalty is weak, the policy may learn to game the scorer instead of becoming genuinely better. If the KL penalty is too strong, the model barely moves and the alignment gains disappear. The method works because it sits in the middle.

Why the pretraining mix exists

The paper finds an important side effect of RLHF: it can damage capabilities on public NLP tasks. This is the alignment tax. The model becomes more preference-aligned on the API prompt distribution, but its performance can regress on tasks like SQuAD, DROP, HellaSwag, and translation.

The fix is straightforward in spirit and very important in practice: mix pretraining updates into PPO.

This PPO-ptx variant keeps training the assistant on the alignment objective, but it also keeps touching the original pretraining distribution. That helps preserve general language ability while the policy is being shaped by human feedback.

Think of it like this: RLHF teaches the model how to behave as an assistant, but pretraining mix reminds it how to remain a broad language model. The paper shows that this reduces regressions much better than simply cranking up the KL penalty.

That distinction is easy to miss, but it is one of the paper’s most useful technical lessons. If you only tighten the leash with KL, you may keep the model close to the base policy, but you do not necessarily recover the lost competence. The pretraining mix gives the model direct pressure to retain that competence.

What the model is actually optimized on

The data flow through the pipeline is worth keeping in your head:

  1. Prompts come from API usage and labeler-written bootstrap prompts.
  2. Labelers write demonstrations for SFT.
  3. Labelers rank multiple completions for RM training.
  4. PPO uses the reward model as the training signal.
  5. Pretraining mix keeps the result from drifting too far away from the original model.

That sequence is the method. It is also the paper’s core claim about how to align a general language model with user intent.

One small but important detail is that the paper’s evaluation is not just on public benchmarks. The main evidence comes from human preferences on held-out prompts from the same kind of distribution the model is supposed to serve. That is the right place to test whether the pipeline actually produced a more useful assistant.

On that measure, the results are strong. The 175B InstructGPT outputs are preferred over 175B GPT-3 outputs 85 +/- 3% of the time. Even the 1.3B InstructGPT model beats the 175B GPT-3 baseline in human preference, which is a good reminder that “better aligned” is not the same thing as “bigger.”

The paper also reports improved truthfulness on TruthfulQA and fewer regressions when pretraining mix is used during PPO.

Where the method still falls short

The paper is honest about the limitations, and they are not minor.

The model is aligned to a specific reference group, not to all users. The preferences come from a small contractor pool and from the researchers’ labeling instructions. That means the result is shaped by who provided feedback, what prompts they saw, and how they were told to judge responses.

The model also still makes simple mistakes. It can hedge too much, fail on false-premise prompts, and produce wrong or overcautious answers when a direct answer would be better.

And there is a more serious limitation: if the user asks for harmful behavior, the model may still follow the instruction. The training recipe improves helpfulness and some safety-related properties, but it is not a complete refusal policy.

That is the right way to read the paper. It is not “human feedback solved alignment.” It is “human feedback gives us a practical post-training recipe that makes a big difference, but the resulting assistant still reflects the feedback regime we built for it.”

The part to remember

If you only remember one thing, remember this loop:

show the model good answers, learn a preference scorer from human rankings, then optimize the model against that scorer while keeping it close to the supervised starting point.

That is the InstructGPT pipeline.

It is useful because it turns a general pretrained model into a better assistant without needing a brand-new architecture. It is also important because it makes the tradeoff visible: alignment gains are real, but they come from a specific human feedback process, and that process has its own narrowness, assumptions, and failure modes.

The paper’s larger lesson is not just that RLHF works. It is that post-training can reshape a language model’s behavior in a very targeted way, and that preserving general capability may require mixing the alignment objective with the original pretraining distribution rather than replacing it entirely.
