Safe Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback

Safe RLHF starts from a simple observation that turns out to be hard to handle in practice: a language model can be useful and safe at the same time, but those two goals do not always point in the same direction.

If a model refuses a dangerous request, that is good for safety, but it may also look unhelpful from a user-assistance point of view. If it answers too directly, it may be helpful in style while crossing a safety line. The paper’s main idea is to stop treating that tension as one blurry preference score. Instead, it splits the problem into two separate signals and then optimizes them with a constraint.

That is the whole method in one sentence: learn what humans think is helpful, learn what humans think is harmless, and then let safety act like a budget rather than a fixed weight.

What to notice is the separation of labor. The annotation stage does not ask crowdworkers to collapse everything into one score. The modeling stage does not train one reward head to represent every preference. And the policy-optimization stage does not freeze the helpfulness-vs-safety tradeoff ahead of time.

That separation is the paper’s real contribution. It makes the whole system easier to reason about because each part has one job:

  • the reward model learns what looks useful,
  • the cost model learns what looks unsafe,
  • the RL step balances the two dynamically.

Why a single preference score is not enough

The usual RLHF story is tempting because it is compact: ask humans which answer they prefer, train a reward model, and optimize the policy against that score.

Safe RLHF argues that this is too crude when safety is part of the task. A single preference label mixes two different judgments:

  • Is the response actually good and helpful?
  • Is the response harmless enough to allow?

Those are related, but they are not identical. A refusal can be safe but low on helpfulness. A detailed answer can be helpful but unsafe. If annotators are forced to compress both into one ranking, the supervision becomes ambiguous, and the optimizer later inherits that ambiguity.

So the paper changes the annotation problem first. That is the important move. Once the labels are cleaner, the downstream optimization becomes much more stable.

Split the supervision before you train anything

The annotation pipeline uses the same prompt and multiple candidate responses, but the judgments are decoupled.

One set of rankings is about helpfulness: which response best addresses the prompt?

Another set is about harmlessness: which response is safer, or which response is more harmful?

The paper also attaches explicit safety labels to the responses. A response is marked safe only if it avoids risk across the paper’s 14 harm categories. That gives the safety model more structure than a plain pairwise ranking alone.

This is easiest to picture as a two-axis map:

  • the helpfulness axis asks, “Does this answer actually do the job?”
  • the harmlessness axis asks, “Does this answer avoid harm?”

The model is not asked to solve a single mushy ranking problem. It is asked to learn two separate geometries.

Reward and cost are not the same thing

The second stage trains two independent preference models.

The reward model is trained on the helpfulness data and acts like a normal pairwise preference model. If humans prefer response A over response B for a prompt, the reward model should assign A the higher score.

The cost model is trained on the harmlessness data, but it does something slightly richer than a plain classifier. It uses the pairwise harmlessness preference and the binary safety labels together.

What to notice here is that the paper wants the cost model to separate safe and unsafe responses into different regions of score space. In other words, the cost score is not just “how much do humans like this answer?” It is a safety-oriented score with a boundary.

The paper makes this idea concrete with a virtual reference point, a hypothetical response that sits on the safe/unsafe boundary at score zero. Helpful responses are not the point here. The cost model is trying to learn whether a response lies on the safe side or the unsafe side of that boundary.

That is why the cost model has two pieces of supervision:

  • pairwise ranking, so it still knows which of two responses is safer,
  • label-based classification, so it also knows where the safe/unsafe boundary lies.

This combination matters. A classifier alone tells you whether something is safe, but it does not tell you how to compare two responses that are both somewhat problematic or both mostly fine. A ranker alone tells you relative order, but not the explicit boundary. Safe RLHF uses both because the downstream optimizer needs both kinds of information.

From safe preference modeling to constrained optimization

Once the two models are trained, the policy is optimized with a constrained RL objective.

The reward model gives the policy a helpfulness signal:

maximize expected reward

The cost model gives it a safety signal:

keep expected cost below a threshold

That is the whole idea behind the constrained formulation. Instead of saying “mix reward and cost with one fixed coefficient,” the paper says:

maximize helpfulness, subject to a harmlessness constraint.

That framing is more than a cosmetic rewrite. It changes how the tradeoff is controlled. Safety is no longer baked into a manually chosen reward weight. It is now an optimization constraint whose strength can move as the model changes.

The paper rewrites the hard per-response safety requirement into an expectation-style constraint with a threshold term. Then it solves the result with a Lagrangian:

reward - lambda * cost

The multiplier lambda is the crucial knob. If the model starts drifting toward harmful behavior, the cost goes up and the multiplier pushes harder on safety. If the model is safely under the threshold, the multiplier relaxes and the optimizer can spend more capacity on helpfulness.

That dynamic adjustment is what static reward shaping cannot do.

Why the multiplier matters

Reward shaping is the obvious baseline: choose a fixed weight and optimize

R - nu * C

This looks reasonable, but it is brittle. If the weight is too small, the model chases helpfulness and ignores harm. If the weight is too large, the model becomes overly cautious or over-refuses. Even decent middle values can be wrong once the policy changes during training.

Safe RLHF avoids that trap by updating the multiplier during training. The paper alternates between updating the policy and updating lambda, using the moving average of the cost model outputs as the signal for whether the current policy is still inside the safe region.

Conceptually, this is easier to remember if you think of safety like a budget:

  • the reward model tells the optimizer what good behavior looks like,
  • the cost model tells it what unsafe behavior costs,
  • the multiplier decides how hard to spend safety budget at the current moment.

That budget is not fixed once at the beginning. It adapts as the policy moves.

The implementation keeps the PPO-style stabilization pieces too:

  • a KL penalty keeps the policy close to the reference model,
  • a PTX loss keeps some pressure from the original SFT distribution,
  • PPO handles the actual policy update.

So the system is not “pure constrained optimization” in the abstract. It is constrained optimization wrapped inside the standard RLHF machinery that keeps language models from drifting too far.

What the training loop actually looks like

Each Safe RLHF round follows the same rhythm:

  1. Start from the current model.
  2. Generate responses to prompts.
  3. Re-annotate those responses for helpfulness and harmlessness.
  4. Retrain the reward model and cost model.
  5. Run PPO-style fine-tuning with the Lagrangian objective.
  6. Update the multiplier from the observed cost.

The paper runs this loop three times.

That repeated loop is important because the prompt set changes. After the first round, the authors add red-teaming prompts in later rounds so they can probe weaknesses that were not visible in the original data. In other words, the model does not just get safer because the training recipe is nicer. It gets safer because the data distribution is actively expanded to include failure cases.

This is a good reminder that alignment is partly an optimization problem and partly a data-collection problem. Safe RLHF addresses both.

What the cost model buys you in practice

The cost model is more than a side classifier. It is the piece that makes the safety constraint operational during RL.

The paper compares this design with using a separate safety classifier’s logits as the cost signal. That weaker alternative helps less. It also shows that if you remove the classification capability from the cost model and stop updating the multiplier, the method collapses back toward static reward shaping.

So the cost model is doing two jobs at once:

  • it encodes the safety geometry learned from annotation,
  • it gives the optimizer a reliable signal for adjusting the helpfulness-safety balance.

That is why the paper treats reward and cost as separate learned objects, not as one reward model with a safety suffix attached.

What the paper shows after training

The paper’s headline result is not just that the model gets safer. It gets safer while also improving helpfulness.

The clearest sign is the large drop in harmful responses and the corresponding jump in GPT-4 and human preference ratings. Beaver-v3, the final round, reduces harmful responses on the evaluation set from 53.08% to 2.45%, while also improving both helpfulness and harmlessness scores.

What to notice in this result is not the exact ranking number. The important point is the shape of the outcome: the model did not simply become more refusal-heavy. It improved on both axes. That is exactly what the constrained setup is trying to accomplish.

The paper also shows that static reward shaping cannot match this behavior reliably. Fixed weights tend to over-optimize one side or the other. The dynamic multiplier is what keeps the method from getting stuck in that tradeoff.

How to keep the idea in your head

The easiest way to remember Safe RLHF is this:

  1. Do not ask one label to represent two different goals.
  2. Train a reward model for helpfulness.
  3. Train a cost model for harmlessness.
  4. Optimize the policy against reward, but enforce safety with a learned constraint.
  5. Let the constraint weight move during training instead of freezing it by hand.

That is the whole design.

If conventional RLHF is “teach the model what humans like,” Safe RLHF is “teach the model what humans like, teach it what humans will not accept, and let safety constrain the final policy instead of hiding inside a fixed scalar mix.”

Where it is still limited

The paper is explicit about the limits.

It is a single-turn setup, so the method is not yet a full answer for long multi-turn conversations. It also depends on costly crowd annotation, red-teaming, and RL training. And because the system relies on explicit safety labels, it is only as good as the safety taxonomy and the prompt coverage behind those labels.

Those are not small caveats. They matter if you want to move the method into a broader deployment setting.

Still, the core lesson is durable: when helpfulness and safety are in tension, it is often better to separate the supervision and optimize one side as a constraint. That gives you a cleaner learning signal and a more adaptive tradeoff than a single fixed reward weight.
