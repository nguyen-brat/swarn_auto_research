Mitigating the Alignment Tax of RLHF with Model Averaging

RLHF creates a familiar tension in language models: the aligned checkpoint is usually better at following human preferences, but it often gives up some of the broad NLP ability the base model already had. That regression is the alignment tax. This paper’s central move is surprisingly simple: instead of trying to fight forgetting with another training loop, it merges the checkpoint before RLHF and the checkpoint after RLHF.

The interesting part is not the merge itself. It is the claim that where you merge matters just as much as how much you merge. The paper shows that different Transformer layers behave differently under averaging, and that low-level layers are especially valuable for preserving both alignment reward and general language ability.

You can think of the method as a controlled blend between two specialists:

  • the pre-RLHF model still remembers the broad skill set learned during pretraining and instruction tuning,
  • the post-RLHF model knows how to satisfy human preference more reliably.

Heterogeneous Model Averaging, or HMA, tries to keep the best parts of both by mixing the layers unevenly instead of treating the whole network as one block.

The basic idea in one picture

The paper starts from a very useful observation: a uniform average is already strong, but a layer-aware average is stronger.

What to notice here is that the model is split into a small number of Transformer parts, and each part gets its own interpolation ratio. That means HMA is not asking, “Should we trust the old checkpoint or the new one?” It is asking a more precise question: “Which parts of the model still benefit from the old representation, and which parts should stay closer to the aligned checkpoint?”

That distinction is the whole method.

Why plain model averaging is already useful

Before introducing HMA, the paper first establishes that ordinary model averaging is unusually strong for this problem. The merge is simple:

θavg=(1−α)θ0+αθ\theta_{\text{avg}} = (1 - \alpha)\theta_0 + \alpha \thetaθavg​=(1−α)θ0​+αθ

Here, \theta_0 is the model before RLHF and \theta is the aligned model after RLHF. The ratio \alpha controls how much of the aligned checkpoint survives the merge.

This already helps because the two checkpoints are not totally unrelated. They share a lot of basic linguistic structure, but specialize in different directions. The pre-RLHF checkpoint is better at many general tasks, while the post-RLHF checkpoint is better aligned to preference reward. Averaging lets the shared parts survive instead of forcing the model to pick one checkpoint and discard the other.

The paper’s broader result is that, among several forgetting-mitigation baselines like early stopping, regularization, LoRA, and distillation, model averaging gives the strongest alignment-versus-forgetting tradeoff. That is the setup HMA builds on.

Why one global ratio is too blunt

The reason uniform averaging leaves performance on the table is that the Transformer is not uniform. Early layers, middle layers, and output layers do not store the same kind of information.

The paper argues that low-level layers are especially important because they encode more shared features. If two tasks overlap in the feature space, averaging can preserve diversity instead of destroying it. That is exactly the kind of situation where model averaging helps: you keep multiple versions of a useful representation rather than collapsing everything into one.

The paper’s proof-of-concept experiment makes this concrete by averaging only one Transformer region at a time.

The key thing to notice is not the exact layer boundaries. It is the pattern. When the paper averages the input layers, the model often improves both the alignment reward and downstream NLP ability at the same time. Averaging middle or output layers does not give the same effect. That suggests the useful overlap lives mostly in the low-level representation space, not uniformly throughout the network.

In plain terms: early layers still speak a language both checkpoints understand. Later layers have diverged more.

From one ratio to many

HMA turns that observation into a method. Instead of using one shared \alpha for the whole model, it gives each Transformer part its own ratio:

θ[k](K):=αkθ[k]+(1−αk)θ0[k],∀k∈1,…,K.\theta^{[k]}(K):=\alpha_k\theta^{[k]}+(1-\alpha_k)\theta_0^{[k]},\forall k\in 1,\ldots,K.θ[k](K):=αk​θ[k]+(1−αk​)θ0[k]​,∀k∈1,…,K.

This is the compact version of the idea. Each layer group k is merged separately. If a part is more useful when it stays close to the pre-RLHF checkpoint, its \alpha_k should be smaller. If a part should inherit more of the aligned behavior, its \alpha_k should be larger.

There is one important constraint: the paper keeps the mean averaging ratio fixed. That makes the comparison fair against vanilla model averaging. Otherwise, HMA could look better simply because it used more of the aligned checkpoint overall. By holding the average of the \alpha_k values constant, the paper isolates the effect of redistributing averaging power across layers.

That is why the objective is not “maximize reward at any cost.” It is “find the best layerwise redistribution while staying on the same overall averaging budget.”

How HMA is optimized

The optimization is deliberately lightweight. The paper does not retrain the whole RLHF stack. Instead, it searches only a few layer-group weights after training.

The implementation uses a proxy distillation objective: sample responses from the aligned policy, treat them as a high-reward proxy dataset, and choose layerwise ratios that maximize the likelihood of those sampled responses under the merged model. In other words, HMA borrows the reward signal from the aligned checkpoint without reopening the whole RLHF loop.

That matters because it keeps HMA practical. You are not tuning millions or billions of parameters. You are tuning only a small vector of layer ratios, often with K = 3 by default. The method is post-training, cheap to apply, and easy to sweep across a few candidate averaging budgets.

The paper also notes that a bounded parameterization is used in implementation to make the search stable, but the conceptual picture stays the same: few parameters, fixed mean, layer-specific control.

What the search is really buying you

The paper’s proof-of-concept experiments show that some layerwise patterns consistently beat the equal-ratio baseline, even when the mean averaging budget is unchanged. That is the signature result here.

HMA is not discovering a new architecture. It is discovering that alignment tax is unevenly distributed across the network, so the fix should be uneven too.

That also explains why the method works best when the two checkpoints still share a lot of structure. If the tasks are too dissimilar, averaging has less to exploit. The merge can still help, but the gains shrink because there is less shared representation to preserve. The paper is explicit about this limitation: HMA reduces the tax, but it does not erase it.

The empirical pattern to keep in mind

The results support a very simple story:

  1. ordinary averaging already gives a strong Pareto front between reward and forgetting,
  2. low-level layers are the most profitable place to average,
  3. redistributing the averaging ratio across layers improves the frontier further.

The paper reports that this pattern holds across RLHF variants, including RSF, DPO, and PPO, and it also transfers to larger open models. On Zephyr-7B-Gemma, for example, HMA slightly improves the GPT-4 judged win rate and also nudges the NLP task scores upward: reading comprehension, commonsense QA, and translation all move in the right direction.

What to notice in this figure is the consistency. The exact scores are not the main lesson. The lesson is that the same low-layer bias keeps showing up, and HMA keeps pushing the vanilla averaging curve in a better direction.

Why this is a useful mental model

The easiest way to remember HMA is this:

  • RLHF creates two checkpoints with partially overlapping strengths.
  • Plain averaging already recovers some of that overlap.
  • Layerwise averaging works better because the overlap is not evenly spread across the network.
  • Early layers are where the shared representation is most reusable, so they are the best place to preserve pre-RLHF knowledge.

That makes HMA feel less like a patch and more like a design principle. If two checkpoints diverge in different parts of the model, treat those parts differently.

The limits

The paper is careful about what HMA does not solve.

It reduces the alignment tax, but it does not eliminate it. The best ratios still need tuning. And the method is strongest when the tasks have enough shared low-level structure for averaging to exploit.

So the right takeaway is not “checkpoint merging solves RLHF forgetting.” The right takeaway is that checkpoint merging becomes much more effective once you stop pretending all layers deserve the same treatment.

What to remember

If you want one sentence, remember this: HMA takes a pretrained checkpoint and its RLHF-aligned successor, averages them layer by layer, and uses uneven ratios to preserve the shared low-level features that uniform merging would blur away.

If you want the deeper intuition, remember the shape of the tradeoff. The aligned model carries useful preference behavior, but the base model still holds general skill. HMA keeps both by spending more of the merge budget where the two checkpoints still agree and less where they have already specialized apart.
