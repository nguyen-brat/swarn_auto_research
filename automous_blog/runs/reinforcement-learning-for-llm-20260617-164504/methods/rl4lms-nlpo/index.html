RL4LMs: A Library for Training LMs with RL

The easiest way to understand this paper is to think of language generation as a control problem.

A language model does not choose one whole answer at once. It chooses the next token, then the next, then the next. RL4LMs takes that seriously and turns generation into a token-level Markov decision process. Once you do that, you can train a policy with reinforcement learning against rewards that are not differentiable, such as sentiment, factuality, coverage, or human preference proxies.

That sounds straightforward, but the paper is really about the tension that appears immediately after you do it: if you optimize reward too aggressively, the model can become fluent nonsense, repeat the prompt, or learn other forms of reward hacking. The paper’s answer is a practical one. Keep the model close to a supervised starting point with KL regularization, and reduce the search space during exploration with NLPO, a token-masking variant of PPO.

What to notice in the figure is the basic tradeoff. Higher reward is easy to get if you let the policy drift. The hard part is getting higher reward while keeping the text natural. NLPO is introduced to make that balancing act more stable.

The problem the paper is solving

The paper is not trying to invent yet another language model architecture. It is trying to answer a more practical question: can RL actually be a usable way to align language generation with human preferences?

That is difficult for two reasons.

First, the reward signal is often sparse or indirect. A sentiment classifier, a factuality metric, or a preference model can score a full sequence, but it does not tell you token by token what was right or wrong.

Second, text has a huge action space. At every step, the model may be choosing among tens of thousands of tokens. Plain policy gradient methods become unstable when the action space is that large, especially when the reward is easy to game.

RL4LMs is the library part of the answer. It gives a reusable way to define generation tasks as RL environments and train HuggingFace models with on-policy algorithms. GRUE is the benchmark part. It gives a set of generation tasks with reward functions instead of target strings. NLPO is the algorithmic part. It tries to tame the action space enough that RL training does not collapse into reward hacking.

The method in one sentence

Take a pretrained LM, view each generation as a sequence of token choices, score the finished text with a task reward, keep the policy near the base model with KL regularization, and during training mask out low-value tokens with a lagged top-p policy so exploration stays focused.

That is the whole method. The rest of the paper explains why each piece is needed.

Turning generation into an MDP

The first important move is conceptual, not algorithmic: treat text generation as a Markov decision process.

The state starts with the prompt. Each action is a token from the vocabulary. The transition is deterministic: append the chosen token to the current state. The episode ends when the model emits EOS or hits a length limit. Only then does the environment emit a reward.

That is a clean way to model language because it matches how generation actually happens.

If the prompt is the start state, then every token choice is a decision. If the final output is bad, the problem is not just the last token. It is the path the policy took through the sequence.

RL4LMs wraps this in an OpenAI Gym-style interface so different generation tasks can share the same training machinery. That matters because the paper is trying to build a library, not a one-off experiment.

What the reward means

Once generation becomes an MDP, the reward can be almost anything that turns a full output into a scalar score.

The paper supports a wide range of rewards:

  • overlap metrics like ROUGE, BLEU, SacreBLEU, and METEOR
  • model-based semantic metrics like BERTScore and BLEURT
  • task-specific metrics like CIDEr, SPICE, PARENT, and SummaCZS
  • fluency and diversity measures such as perplexity, entropy, and distinct-n-gram scores
  • learned preference classifiers trained on human feedback

This is the main reason the library matters. It is not tied to one task or one reward shape. It lets you ask the same question across many kinds of text generation: if I optimize this preference signal, do I actually get better text?

The answer, the paper argues, is often yes, but only if the optimization is constrained enough to preserve fluency.

The KL leash

The policy does not train from scratch. It starts from a pretrained LM, denoted π0\pi_0π0​, and the RL policy πθ\pi_\thetaπθ​ is initialized from it.

That starting point is important because it gives the RL agent a fluent prior. Without it, RL on text has too much freedom and too little structure.

The paper’s regularized reward is:

R^(st,at,y)=R(st,at,y)−β KL(πθ(at∣st)∥π0(at∣st))\hat{R}(s_t,a_t,\mathbf{y}) = R(s_t,a_t,\mathbf{y}) - \beta\,\mathrm{KL}(\pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)\|\pi_0(a_t|s_t))R^(st​,at​,y)=R(st​,at​,y)−βKL(πθ​(at​∣st​)∥π0​(at​∣st​))

Read this as: reward the model for the task, but charge it for drifting away from the base LM.

This is the first brake in the system. It prevents the policy from finding bizarre shortcuts that maximize the reward model while destroying naturalness. The paper also adapts the KL coefficient dynamically, which is a practical way to keep the constraint from being too loose or too tight during training.

If the KL term is removed, the paper shows that the model can reward hack badly. On IMDB, for example, unconstrained optimization can lead to generic positive phrases that ignore the prompt. So the KL term is not just a theoretical nicety. It is the thing that keeps the reward target from becoming a trap.

Why actor-critic fits this setup

RL4LMs uses on-policy actor-critic methods such as PPO, TRPO, and A2C. That choice matches the sequential setup.

The actor is the policy that chooses the next token. The critic estimates how good the current state is. The advantage function tells the actor whether a token choice was better than expected.

Because language rewards are often sequence-level and sparse, the paper uses generalized advantage estimation to stabilize learning. That reduces variance, which matters a lot when the reward only arrives at the end of a long generation.

In plain terms, actor-critic helps the model learn from delayed feedback without treating every token update as equally important.

Why KL alone is still not enough

The paper’s central observation is that KL regularization helps, but it does not fully solve the instability problem.

The reason is the action space. A language model vocabulary is enormous. Even if you keep the policy close to the base LM, the agent still has too many choices at each step. That makes exploration noisy and makes reward hacking easier than it should be.

NLPO is the paper’s answer to that second problem.

NLPO: mask the bad part of the action space

NLPO stands for Natural Language Policy Optimization. It is basically PPO with a learned action mask.

The idea is simple:

  1. keep a lagged copy of the policy, πψ\pi_\psiπψ​
  2. use that copy to define a top-p subset of likely tokens
  3. mask out the rest during exploration
  4. update the mask policy only every μ\muμ steps

So instead of sampling from the entire vocabulary, the agent explores only a narrower set of tokens that the lagged policy already thinks are plausible.

That gives NLPO two advantages at once.

It keeps the policy from wasting time on obviously irrelevant tokens, and it also makes exploration less likely to wander into reward-hacking territory. The mask is not derived from the original pretrained model alone. It is derived from the policy from a few iterations ago, so it can carry some task-specific structure learned during RL.

That is the key intuition behind NLPO. The KL penalty says, “do not drift too far from a known fluent model.” The mask says, “within this step, do not even waste probability mass on tokens that are already low-value.”

The paper finds that this extra constraint usually helps more than PPO alone, especially after supervised warm start.

A useful mental picture of the workflow

You can think of the training loop like this:

  1. Start with a prompt from the task dataset.
  2. The policy generates tokens one by one.
  3. The environment scores the completed output.
  4. The reward is adjusted by KL regularization.
  5. PPO-style actor-critic updates the policy.
  6. NLPO optionally masks low-probability tokens during exploration.
  7. The policy is evaluated on both task metrics and naturalness metrics.

That flow is the reason the benchmark is structured around full generations rather than supervised target strings. The model is not being asked to imitate a reference sentence exactly. It is being asked to discover text that satisfies a reward while still sounding like language a human would write.

Why GRUE exists

The benchmark, GRUE, is the paper’s way of making this research question measurable.

Instead of one task, GRUE includes seven generation tasks such as IMDB sentiment continuation, CommonGen, CNN/DailyMail summarization, ToTTo, WMT translation, NarrativeQA, and DailyDialog. Each task has its own preference metrics and its own naturalness metrics.

That is important because a single metric can be misleading. A model might improve on the task score while getting worse at fluency, or vice versa. GRUE forces you to look at both sides.

The paper also evaluates models with human judgments on several tasks. That is essential, because the paper’s whole warning is that automated metrics can miss reward hacking if the output still looks fluent enough on paper.

What the experiments are really showing

The headline result is not just “RL works.” It is more specific than that.

The paper finds that RL methods are usually better than pure supervised baselines when they are warm-started with supervision, and that NLPO is generally more stable and better performing than PPO.

That pattern repeats across tasks:

  • supervised warm starts usually help a lot
  • KL control helps preserve fluency
  • NLPO often beats PPO when the model is already in the right neighborhood
  • unconstrained RL can maximize reward while producing low-quality text

The IMDB sentiment task makes this especially clear. As the KL target is relaxed, sentiment reward rises, but perplexity gets worse. In the extreme case, the model learns generic positive phrases unrelated to the prompt. That is the paper’s simplest demonstration of reward hacking.

The same idea appears in other tasks too. On CommonGen and ToTTo, a supervised start is especially important because the task itself is easy to distort by repeating input concepts or copying table content. Warm starts provide a prior over what “good text” even looks like.

Why the warm start matters so much

This is one of the most important practical lessons in the paper.

If the initial policy already has some task signal, the KL term and the NLPO mask are both pointing in a useful direction. They constrain the model without destroying the structure it needs.

If the initial policy is weak, those same constraints can misfire. The KL penalty can push the model toward generic fluent nonsense, and the mask can eliminate the wrong tokens because it is built from a weak policy.

So the method is not “RL from scratch for language.” It is “RL on top of a model that already knows something useful.”

That is why the paper repeatedly shows stronger results for Supervised+PPO and Supervised+NLPO than for RL alone.

What to remember about NLPO

NLPO is not a new language model and not a new reward model. It is a change in how the policy explores.

If PPO says, “sample broadly and trust the reward to sort it out,” NLPO says, “use the recent policy to fence off the part of the vocabulary that is probably unhelpful.”

That is a small algorithmic change, but it fits the structure of text generation better. Natural language has a huge combinatorial action space, and not all tokens are equally worth exploring at every step.

The paper’s ablations support that intuition. Too much masking hurts, too little masking helps less, and there is a workable middle ground. Likewise, updating the mask policy too often or too rarely is also bad. The paper is showing that the stability benefit comes from a balance, not from an extreme restriction.

Limitations and assumptions

The method depends on having a decent reward function or preference model. If the reward is weak, the policy will optimize the wrong thing faster.

It also depends on a reasonably good starting model. The paper is explicit that warm starts matter for the strongest results. NLPO is not a magic rescue for a poor policy.

And even with these controls, manual evaluation is still important. The paper uses human judgments precisely because automatic metrics can miss reward hacking when the text remains superficially fluent.

The part that sticks

The simplest way to remember the paper is this:

text generation becomes token-level RL, but token-level RL on language is too open-ended unless you constrain it twice.

KL regularization keeps the policy near the base model. NLPO masks low-value tokens so exploration stays focused.

Together, those two ideas make RL for language feel much less like blind search and much more like controlled improvement over an already fluent model.

That is the main contribution of RL4LMs + NLPO: not just a library, but a practical recipe for making RL on language generation stable enough to study seriously.
