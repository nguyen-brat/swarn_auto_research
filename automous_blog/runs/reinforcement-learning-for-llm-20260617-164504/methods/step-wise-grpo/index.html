Step-wise Group Relative Policy Optimization for Multimodal Large Language Models

When people train multimodal language models to reason, the hardest part is often not whether the final answer is right. It is whether the model took a sensible path to get there.

If the reward only arrives at the end, most of the reasoning trace is invisible to learning. A rollout can be almost right, mostly wrong, or structurally messy, and the model only learns a single late verdict. StepGRPO is built around a simple fix: keep the online RL setup, but make the reward dense enough to judge intermediate reasoning as well as the final answer.

The method starts with a warm-up stage on chain-of-thought data, then moves into online reinforcement learning with two rule-based step rewards:

  • StepRAR, which gives partial credit when the rollout contains the key intermediate reasoning steps.
  • StepRVR, which rewards a reasoning path that is complete and logically ordered.

The result is a group-relative RL loop that can learn from better and worse rollouts within the same batch, without training a separate process reward model.

Figure 1 shows the problem the paper is trying to solve. With outcome-only reward, only a small fraction of reasoning traces get a strong learning signal. That makes exploration fragile: the model can wander through many bad or neutral trajectories before it gets enough useful feedback.

StepGRPO changes that picture by rewarding more than the final answer. A rollout can still matter if it contains the right intermediate moves or if it follows a coherent reasoning structure. That matters especially for smaller MLLMs, where long-step reasoning is still uneven and sparse final-answer rewards leave too little signal to learn from.

The whole pipeline in one pass

The easiest way to think about StepGRPO is as a two-stage teaching loop.

The first stage is warm-up. The model is fine-tuned on multimodal chain-of-thought data so it already knows how to produce step-by-step reasoning before RL starts. Without that warm-up, the online phase would be trying to reward a model that has not yet learned the basic shape of a reasoning trace.

The second stage is the actual online optimization loop:

  1. sample several reasoning rollouts for the same question,
  2. score each rollout with StepRAR and StepRVR,
  3. combine the step-wise rewards into one trajectory reward,
  4. normalize those rewards within the group,
  5. update the policy with KL regularization against a frozen reference model.

That is the core idea. StepGRPO does not replace GRPO-style optimization. It refines it so that the reward landscape is less sparse and more informative.

Why the warm-up comes first

The warm-up phase is not just a convenience. It gives the model a basic reasoning habit before the reward function starts judging it.

In the paper’s formulation, the warm-up is standard supervised fine-tuning on multimodal CoT data. The model learns from question-and-reasoning pairs, so it sees what a step-by-step answer looks like in the target domain. This is important because step-wise RL only works well if the model can already emit recognizable reasoning steps.

Think of it like coaching a student. Before you grade the student on the quality of their reasoning, you make sure they know how to write a reasoning trace at all. Otherwise the RL phase spends too much effort pushing the model into the right format instead of improving the reasoning itself.

That is also why the paper’s gains are not just “RL from scratch.” The method depends on a warm-start policy that can already produce usable CoT-style outputs.

StepRAR: giving credit for the right intermediate moves

The first new reward is Step-wise Reasoning Accuracy Reward, or StepRAR. This reward asks a narrower question than final-answer scoring: does the rollout contain the key intermediate steps needed to reach the correct solution?

The paper builds this reward around a set of extracted key steps for each training question. The important detail is not the extraction pipeline itself, but the shape of the signal it produces. A rollout is rewarded when it matches more of those key steps.

That is why the method uses soft matching rather than brittle exact matching. In math and visual reasoning, the same step can be written in several equivalent forms. A strict string match would miss valid reasoning just because the notation changed. Soft matching lets the reward recognize equivalence across formatting variants such as symbolic notation, plain arithmetic, or verbal paraphrase.

Here, k is the fraction of key steps that were matched, and \alpha controls how much the intermediate-step matches matter. The intuition is straightforward: a wrong final answer is not equally bad if the reasoning path still got most of the essential steps right.

That is a big shift from outcome-only reward. Instead of treating every wrong trajectory as equally useless, StepRAR preserves learning signal from the rollouts that are partly right. This is exactly what sparse-reward reasoning needs.

StepRVR: rewarding a reasoning trace that actually hangs together

StepRAR only checks whether the rollout contains important content. It does not ask whether the rollout is well-formed. That is the role of StepRVR, the step-wise reasoning validity reward.

This second reward looks for two things:

  • completeness: the response should include the background analysis, the step-by-step reasoning, and the final answer,
  • logic: those parts should appear in the right order, with the background first, the solution steps next, and the final answer only after the reasoning is finished.

In other words, StepRVR is less about correctness of the local math and more about whether the reasoning path is structurally sound.

That distinction matters because a rollout can be detailed but still be a mess. It can jump to the answer too early, skip the context needed to ground the visual problem, or mix conclusion with derivation. StepRVR gives the model a separate reason to keep the reasoning trace organized.

You can think of StepRVR as a gate:

The paper’s goal here is not to punish style for its own sake. It is to make the reasoning process more interpretable and more reliable. If the model learns that clean structure is rewarded, it has a stronger incentive to produce traces that are easier to follow and less likely to collapse into answer-only shortcuts.

How the rewards become an optimization signal

Once the model has generated a group of rollouts for the same question, StepGRPO combines the two rewards into one trajectory score.

At that point, the crucial move is group-relative normalization. Instead of using an absolute reward threshold, the method compares each rollout against the average reward of the whole sampled group:

A^i=ri−mean(r1,…,rM)std(r1,…,rM)\hat{A}^{i} = \frac{r^{i} - \text{mean}(r^{1}, \dots, r^{M})}{\text{std}(r^{1}, \dots, r^{M})}A^i=std(r1,…,rM)ri−mean(r1,…,rM)​

This is where the “group relative” part of the method earns its name. The model is not told that a rollout must be perfect to matter. It is told whether one rollout was better or worse than the other candidates it produced for the same question.

That baseline choice stabilizes training in two ways.

First, it makes the update local to the group. The model learns from relative differences among its own sampled trajectories rather than from a brittle global cutoff.

Second, the standard deviation scaling keeps the advantage comparable across questions. If a set of rollouts is tightly clustered, the update stays cautious. If the rollouts vary more, the model gets a clearer signal about which trajectory was better.

This is also why the paper samples multiple rollouts per question. A single rollout cannot tell you much about relative quality. A small group gives the optimizer something to compare.

The final policy update then uses that advantage together with KL regularization against a frozen reference model. The KL term matters because it keeps the policy from drifting too far while it is exploring better reasoning traces. So the optimization is not a free-for-all. It is a controlled self-improvement loop.

A concrete way to remember the method

Imagine a multimodal question where the model has to inspect an image, reason about some quantities, and then answer.

Outcome-only RL would wait until the very end and ask, “Did the answer match?”

StepGRPO asks a richer set of questions while the answer is being built:

  • Did the model identify the important intermediate step?
  • Did it carry the reasoning forward in a sensible order?
  • Did it present the answer only after the reasoning was done?
  • Compared with the other sampled rollouts, which path was actually better?

That is the heart of the method. It does not abandon final-answer correctness. It just stops pretending that the final answer is the only part worth learning from.

Why this is different from plain SFT or outcome-level RL

The paper’s experiments make the distinction clear. Compared with warm-up alone, adding step-wise reward improves performance. Compared with warm-up plus outcome-level reward, the step-wise setup does better still.

On MathVista, the paper reports 61.7 for warm-up only, 62.3 for warm-up plus outcome-level reward, and 63.5 for warm-up plus step-wise reward. That gap is not huge, but it is the right kind of improvement: the model is learning from denser supervision instead of waiting for sparse final verdicts.

What to notice in Figure 3 is that StepGRPO improves more reliably than supervised fine-tuning under the same budget. That is the key behavioral difference between passive imitation and online self-improvement. SFT can teach the model what a good trace looks like, but StepGRPO also teaches it how to prefer better traces over worse ones.

The larger benchmark picture is consistent with that story too. The paper’s stronger R1-VL variant reaches 74.3 on MathVista, ahead of the 68.2 baseline it is compared against in the extract. So the method is not just a nicer training story. It produces a better reasoning model.

What the method assumes

StepGRPO is effective, but it is not free of assumptions.

It depends on warm-up CoT data, because the online phase needs a model that can already produce meaningful step-by-step outputs. It also depends on extracted key steps and on the quality of the hand-designed reward rules. If the key steps miss something important, good reasoning can be under-rewarded. If the validity rules are too rigid, a correct but stylistically different reasoning trace can be penalized.

There is also a compute cost. Multiple rollouts per question are what make the group-relative baseline and dense step scoring work, but they also increase training cost.

So the method trades simplicity of reward design for a richer training signal. The paper’s case is that this is worth it for MLLM reasoning, where outcome-only signals are often too sparse.

What to remember

StepGRPO is easiest to remember as “GRPO with step-wise reasoning feedback.”

The model first learns the basic CoT format, then explores several rollouts per question, then receives two kinds of dense rule-based reward:

  • StepRAR for key intermediate-step accuracy,
  • StepRVR for reasoning completeness and logic.

Those rewards are normalized within the sampled group, so the policy update compares trajectories against one another rather than waiting for a brittle all-or-nothing verdict. That gives the model a stronger learning signal, avoids the need for a separate process reward model, and makes online RL more practical for multimodal reasoning.

If you want one sentence to keep in your head, use this:

StepGRPO teaches the model not only to get the answer right, but to earn that answer through a reasoning path that is recognizable, complete, and logically ordered.
