Introspective LLM: Learning to Control Sampling Temperature in RLVR

The idea behind IntroLLM is simple once you strip away the formalism: instead of treating sampling temperature as a fixed knob that a human chooses ahead of time, the model learns to turn that knob for itself while it is generating. When the model feels uncertain or reaches a reasoning pivot, it can open up the distribution and explore. When it is deep in a calculation or locking in the final answer, it can cool down and become more decisive.

That turns temperature from a hand-tuned hyperparameter into part of the policy.

The paper is written in the RLVR setting, where rewards are verifiable. That matters because the training signal is not a vague preference score. The model gets rewarded for actually solving the task. Once that is true, exploration stops being a static inference trick and becomes something worth learning directly from reward.

What to notice here is the two-step loop. The model does not jump straight from hidden state to token. It first decides how much randomness it wants, then it samples the token under that temperature. That is the whole method in miniature.

Why a learned temperature policy is worth caring about

RLVR lives or dies by the quality of the trajectories it samples. If every rollout is too deterministic, the model gets stuck recycling the same answer patterns. If every rollout is too noisy, the reward signal becomes chaotic and training wastes effort on garbage generations. So temperature is not a cosmetic setting in this regime. It is the main dial that controls exploration.

The problem is that static settings are blunt instruments. One prompt may need broad exploration at the start and tight precision at the end. Another may need a different temperature altogether. Even within one solution, the right amount of randomness changes as the model moves from setting up the problem to checking arithmetic to writing the final response.

IntroLLM treats that changing need as something the model can infer from its own hidden state. In other words, the model is not guessing blindly. It is reading the internal representation that already summarizes what it knows, how confident it is, and where it is in the reasoning process.

This is the paper’s central move: exploration control becomes a learnable policy, not a fixed schedule.

The method as a control loop

The easiest way to remember IntroLLM is as a small controller wrapped around a standard autoregressive model.

At every decoding step:

  1. the decoder computes the hidden state,
  2. a temperature head reads that hidden state,
  3. the controller decides whether to change temperature,
  4. if it does change, it samples a new temperature value,
  5. the token policy generates the next token using that temperature,
  6. the whole trajectory is scored by a verifiable reward.

The key design choice is that the temperature policy is not global. It acts at token level. That makes it much more expressive than a single prompt-level temperature and much more faithful to how reasoning actually unfolds.

The paper also makes a deliberate choice to keep the control policy lightweight. The temperature head is a small MLP attached to the final decoder layer. That keeps the overhead low enough that the method behaves like an add-on to the base model rather than a separate system.

What the controller is actually deciding

IntroLLM uses a mixed action space for temperature, and that is the detail that keeps the method stable.

The first decision is binary: should temperature change at this token or should it stay where it is?

That sounds modest, but it solves an important problem. If the controller were forced to resample temperature every single step, it could introduce noise for no benefit. Sometimes the current temperature is already right. The Bernoulli update gate lets the model keep it.

If the gate says “update,” the second decision is continuous: choose a new temperature value within the allowed range.

The paper uses a bounded continuous distribution for this part so the output stays in a safe interval instead of drifting to extreme values. In practical terms, that means the controller can explore a smoother range of temperatures without becoming erratic. The final temperature is then mapped into the target interval used in training.

This split matters because it lets the controller learn two different things at once:

  • when exploration should change,
  • how large that change should be.

That is much better than forcing one of those decisions to carry all the burden.

How the token model and the temperature model fit together

The token policy is ordinary in structure but unusual in conditioning. It still produces logits over the vocabulary, but those logits are divided by the selected temperature before sampling.

This figure is useful because it shows what the controller learns to do in practice. The global trend looks like a gentle annealing curve: early exploration, later exploitation. But the interesting part is the local wiggle. On a single problem, the model does not just lower temperature forever. It raises it at pivots, drops it during computation, and raises it again when it needs to move to the next reasoning step.

That is the intuition behind the method. The model learns a rhythm.

You can think of it like a speaker who occasionally slows down and thinks out loud when a sentence gets tricky, then speeds up again once the structure is clear. The point is not constant caution. The point is responsive caution.

This temperature-conditioned token policy is what makes the exploration control useful for reasoning. It changes the token distribution exactly where the model believes the next step is fragile.

Why the training needs coordinate ascent

Jointly learning the token policy and the temperature policy sounds natural, but it creates a training problem. If both policies change at the same time, each one is chasing a moving target. The token model sees a different exploration regime every update. The temperature policy sees a different generator every update. That kind of feedback loop can get noisy fast.

IntroLLM handles this with coordinate ascent.

The practical meaning is straightforward: alternate between updating the token policy while holding temperature fixed, and updating the temperature policy while holding the sampled tokens fixed. Both are still trained from the same reward, but they are not being optimized in a single tangled update.

Why is that helpful?

  • It reduces non-stationarity.
  • It makes credit assignment easier.
  • It lets each policy specialize in its own job.

The token policy learns how to generate good content under a chosen exploration regime. The temperature policy learns which exploration regime tends to produce good content in the first place.

That division is the reason the paper can make the controller learnable without turning training into a variance problem.

What reward is doing here

The reward setup stays close to standard RLVR. The model gets a binary signal after the full answer is checked. If the final answer is correct, the trajectory gets rewarded. If not, it does not.

That may sound sparse, but it is exactly the kind of signal this method needs. IntroLLM is not trying to learn from dense supervision about every reasoning step. It is trying to learn how much exploration to use so that the right reasoning paths are more likely to appear in the first place.

Because the reward is shared by both the token policy and the temperature policy, the controller gets direct feedback on whether its chosen exploration pattern helped. If a trajectory succeeds, both the content decisions and the temperature decisions that produced it are reinforced. If it fails, both are weakened.

That shared reward is what makes the temperature policy more than a heuristic scheduler. It is part of the same optimization loop as the model that generates the answer.

Why the learned temperatures look sensible

One of the nicest parts of the paper is that the learned behavior is interpretable instead of opaque.

The controller tends to give higher temperatures to harder problems and lower temperatures to easier ones. That is visible in the difficulty analysis, where temperature rises as the benchmark level increases. In other words, the model notices that difficult tasks need more exploration budget.

It also assigns spikes to reasoning-critical words and transitions. Tokens like “assume,” “consider,” and “finding” show up among the highest-temperature positions. That is not a coincidence. Those are the places where the model is often shifting from one subproblem to another, and that is exactly where broader sampling can help.

What to notice in this image is that the policy is not raising temperature everywhere. It is focusing on semantically meaningful moments. That is the strongest sign that the controller has learned a useful internal notion of uncertainty, not just a crude preference for randomness.

The training dynamics tell a similar story. Early in training, IntroLLM keeps entropy higher than fixed-temperature GRPO, which helps it explore more broadly instead of collapsing too quickly into a narrow answer style.

The important thing here is not just the entropy curve itself. It is the fact that higher entropy is being maintained without destroying training. The controller is learning to preserve exploration long enough to discover better solutions, rather than collapsing immediately into premature certainty.

What changed compared with the baselines

The paper compares IntroLLM against fixed-temperature GRPO, a hand-designed annealing method, and a sequence-level learned temperature method. The interesting pattern is that the token-level learned controller wins.

That tells you something important about the problem structure. A single global temperature is too coarse. A schedule that only knows time is still too blind. And a sequence-level controller is better, but it still cannot react inside the reasoning trace.

IntroLLM’s advantage comes from token-level control plus selective updates. Those two pieces work together:

  • token-level control gives the method fine-grained adaptation,
  • selective updates prevent it from thrashing by changing temperature too often.

That second point is important. The paper shows that always changing temperature is not a free win. Too much control can add variance and destabilize training. The update gate is what makes the method practical.

A good mental model for remembering the method

The best way to remember IntroLLM is this:

the model looks at its own hidden state, decides whether it needs more or less exploration, and then uses reward to learn when that instinct was useful.

That is why the paper uses the word “introspective.” The model is not introspecting in a philosophical sense. It is using its internal state as a signal for exploration control.

If you want a simpler analogy, imagine a problem solver with a hand on the volume knob:

  • turn it up when the next move is uncertain,
  • turn it down when the solution is basically in hand.

IntroLLM learns that policy from reward.

Where the method works well, and where it does not

The paper’s strengths are clear.

  • It improves reasoning accuracy and solution diversity at the same time.
  • It adds only a small parameter and compute overhead.
  • It produces a controller whose behavior is easy to interpret.

But the method is not universal. It relies on verifiable rewards, so it is most natural in domains like math and code where correctness can be checked automatically. It is also trained and evaluated mainly on math-heavy reasoning tasks, so transfer to open-ended generation is still an open question.

There is another practical cost: the system is more complex than ordinary GRPO because it adds a separate control head, a selective gate, and a coordinate-ascent training procedure. None of that is exotic, but it is still more moving parts than a plain fixed-temperature setup.

What to remember

IntroLLM’s main idea is that temperature should be learned, not chosen once and forgotten.

The method turns exploration into a policy conditioned on hidden state, then trains that policy together with the token generator using the same verifiable reward. A small gate decides whether temperature changes at all, and a bounded continuous controller decides how much it should change when it does.

That design gives the model a useful kind of self-regulation: more randomness at uncertain reasoning pivots, less randomness during precise computation. The result is better accuracy, better diversity, and more stable RLVR training without a large overhead.
