Rubric-ARM: Alternating RL for Rubric Generation and Judging

Rubric-ARM starts from a problem that shows up everywhere in reward modeling for open-ended tasks: a single scalar score is often too thin to capture what actually makes one answer better than another. In creative writing, instruction following, or other non-verifiable settings, quality is multi-dimensional. A response can be fluent but miss the point, detailed but sloppy, or correct in spirit but weak in structure. Static rubrics and frozen judges help a little, but they still leave the hardest part unsolved: who gets to decide what the rubric should look like in the first place?

This paper’s answer is to stop treating the rubric as a fixed artifact. Instead, it treats rubrics as latent actions and learns them together with the judge that applies them. The generator proposes a rubric for the prompt, the judge uses that rubric to compare two candidate answers, and alternating reinforcement learning makes each module improve the other under the same correctness objective.

The loop before the details

The cleanest way to understand Rubric-ARM is to look at the whole loop first.

What to notice is that the system is not just “generate rubric, then judge once.” It is a feedback loop. A rubric generator creates prompt-specific criteria. A judge consumes those criteria together with the prompt and the two candidate responses. Then the preference signal from that judgment is used to improve the very module that produced the rubric, as well as the module that made the judgment.

That is the core move: the checklist and the evaluator are trained together instead of being frozen separately.

Why a learned rubric is useful

If the judge only sees a scalar score, it has to compress everything into one opaque decision. That works when the task is simple, but it becomes brittle when quality has several overlapping dimensions. Rubrics give the model a way to unpack the decision into smaller checks, such as correctness, tone, structure, or coverage.

The catch is that hand-written rubrics do not scale, and prompting a frozen LLM to invent rubrics leaves the generator itself unchanged. Rubric-ARM tries to close that gap. It learns to produce rubrics that are not just plausible language, but actually useful for recovering the preference label.

You can think of it like this:

  • the rubric generator learns what questions to ask,
  • the judge learns how to answer those questions and still make the right final choice,
  • both are rewarded when the final preference matches the dataset label.

That shared objective is important. The generator is not optimized to write pretty rubrics. It is optimized to write rubrics that help the judge be correct.

Start with a warm start, not a blank slate

The paper does not begin the alternating loop from scratch. First it does a supervised warmup for both modules.

This matters because alternating RL is unstable if both sides are weak at the same time. So the paper first fine-tunes the rubric generator and the judge on synthetic rubrics and judge traces, using open-source preference and instruction-following data. The result is a pair of models that already know the basic shape of the job before RL starts.

This warmup is not the main idea, but it is a practical necessity. It gives the judge enough structure to produce valid rubric-following traces, and it gives the rubric generator enough prior knowledge to propose something other than random criteria.

One side is fixed while the other learns

After warmup, training switches into alternating reinforcement learning. The key design choice is simple: do not update both modules at once.

Rubric-ARM alternates between two phases:

  1. fix the rubric generator and train the judge,
  2. fix the judge and train the rubric generator.

That order matters. When the judge is trained first, it becomes the more stable target. Then the rubric generator can learn against a stronger evaluator instead of chasing a moving one. The paper finds that flipping the order hurts performance, which fits the intuition: if the rubric generator starts changing too early, the judge is trained against unstable criteria and the whole loop becomes noisy.

When the judge is being trained

In the first RL phase, the rubric generator is frozen. For each prompt, it samples a rubric once, and that rubric is cached and reused for multiple judge updates. Caching reduces sampling noise and keeps the judge from seeing a different rubric every time it updates.

That is more than an efficiency trick. It also stabilizes the learning signal. The judge is trying to recover the known preference label from a fixed rubric-conditioned input, so it gets a much cleaner target.

The reward here is not only whether the final preference is correct. The paper also adds a format reward that forces the judge to follow the expected structure: address the rubric criteria, provide a rubric-conditioned justification, and then make a final decision. This helps prevent degenerate traces that skip over the rubric and jump straight to a label.

When the rubric generator is being trained

Then the roles swap. The judge is frozen, and the rubric generator gets rewarded when the current judge arrives at the correct preference. This is the more interesting step conceptually, because the rubric generator is now learning to output criteria that are actually discriminative for the prompt.

In other words, the generator is not inventing generic checklists. It is learning to produce the criteria that make the judge most likely to recover the right answer pairwise preference.

The paper uses a very lightweight reward here: if the judge’s final preference is correct, the rubric gets credit; if not, it does not. That keeps the signal tied tightly to the end goal instead of overfitting to rubric style.

Why alternating training is better than simultaneous co-training

The paper spends a lot of effort explaining why the alternating schedule is not just an implementation detail. The short version is that simultaneous updates create a non-stationary target. If both modules move at once, the judge is chasing rubrics that keep changing, and the rubric generator is chasing a judge that keeps changing back.

That is a noisy game. Alternation reduces the noise by giving each side a short period where the other side is fixed.

The theoretical section frames this as a variance problem. The generator’s updates are especially unstable early on because exploratory rubric samples create extra gradient variance. By training the judge first and reusing rubrics during judge updates, Rubric-ARM reduces one source of noise before opening the harder loop again. The paper’s conclusion is not that variance disappears, but that the judge-first schedule makes the optimization much more stable.

If you want one intuition to remember, it is this: learn the evaluator before asking the rubric writer to improve.

The EM-style interpretation

The alternating procedure can also be read like a generalized EM algorithm. The rubrics are treated as latent variables. With the rubric generator fixed, the judge update looks like an M-step: improve the model that explains the observed preference label under the sampled rubric. With the judge fixed, the generator update acts like an amortized E-step: move probability mass toward rubrics that make the current judge more likely to recover the correct preference.

That analogy is useful because it explains why the method feels coordinated rather than ad hoc. The two modules are not optimizing separate goals. They are taking turns tightening the same loop around preference correctness.

What happens after reward-model training

Rubric-ARM is not only a reward model in isolation. The trained pair is then used as supervision for policy post-training.

For offline alignment, the system samples two candidate responses from a policy, has Rubric-ARM label which one is preferred, and then feeds those labels into DPO or iterative DPO. For online alignment, it uses the judge directly as a reward source for GRPO-style policy optimization. In both cases, the point is the same: a more reliable rubric-conditioned judge should produce a more useful learning signal for the policy.

That downstream use is part of the method’s value. Rubric-ARM is not just trying to score benchmarks better. It is trying to produce a better reward signal for the next training stage.

Why the paper thinks this works

The paper’s empirical story matches the training design.

Rubric-ARM improves over Rubric-RM, which is the natural baseline that uses rubrics but does not jointly optimize them with the judge. It also improves downstream policy training in both offline DPO and online RL settings. The gains are especially clear when the policy is iteratively optimized, which suggests that the reward signal stays stable enough to be reused across rounds instead of collapsing after one pass.

Two practical details help explain the stronger behavior:

  • judge-first updates make the learning signal less noisy,
  • the format reward keeps the judge disciplined and rubric-aware.

That combination matters because a rubric-based system can fail in a very specific way: it can sound structured while quietly ignoring the rubric. The format reward pushes against that failure mode.

The tradeoff you should keep in mind

Rubric-ARM is stronger than a frozen scalar reward model, but it is also more complex.

It needs a warm start. It needs multiple alternating rounds. It depends on preference data and shaped rewards. And at inference time it uses two modules instead of one, so it is less compact than a single reward head.

Those are real costs. The paper’s argument is that the extra machinery is worth it when the task is subjective and the reward signal is non-stationary. In those settings, learning the rubric and the judge together is a practical way to make the reward model less brittle.

What to remember

Rubric-ARM is easiest to remember as a two-player loop:

  1. the rubric generator proposes the checklist,
  2. the judge applies that checklist to two candidate responses,
  3. the correct preference trains the judge,
  4. the same preference trains the rubric generator,
  5. the loop alternates until both modules become better at the shared task.

The big idea is not just “use rubrics.” It is “learn the rubric itself as part of the reward model.” That is what makes Rubric-ARM feel different from static rubric systems: the criteria, the judge, and the final preference signal co-evolve instead of staying fixed.
