Sample-Routed Policy Optimization

SRPO is built around a simple but useful observation: not every training sample should be treated the same way.

If a rollout is already correct, the model usually does not need dense teacher-style correction. It already landed in the right region of behavior, so a reward-aligned update is enough. But if a rollout is wrong, and there is a reliable sibling response available, then that failure benefits from a much denser signal than a single scalar reward. SRPO turns that observation into an on-policy training loop that routes each sample to the supervision signal that fits it best.

What this figure is telling you is the whole paper in miniature. SDPO is fast at first, but it becomes unstable later. SRPO fixes that by asking a more precise question before every update: is this sample already good enough for reward learning, or does it need corrective distillation?

Why the paper needs a new recipe

The two ingredients SRPO combines already exist.

GRPO gives a stable RLVR update. It compares rollouts at the sequence level and pushes up the ones with better reward. That is dependable, but coarse. Every token in a rollout gets the same advantage signal, so if a trajectory is wrong, GRPO knows the whole sequence was bad without knowing where the mistake actually happened.

SDPO goes in the opposite direction. It uses a feedback-conditioned self-teacher to provide dense logit-level supervision, which is exactly what you want when the model needs help correcting a specific bad trajectory. The problem is that this signal can become unreliable late in training. The paper argues that two things happen at once: correct samples are forced through unnecessary self-distillation, which creates ambiguity, and the self-teacher itself becomes noisier as its entropy rises.

SRPO’s answer is not to blend both methods everywhere. It is to route samples to the method that fits their state.

The black box view

You can think of SRPO as a three-step pipeline:

  1. Generate a group of on-policy rollouts for each prompt.
  2. Check which rollouts are correct, and whether teacher information is available.
  3. Send each rollout to the branch that makes sense for it.

Correct rollouts go to GRPO. Incorrect rollouts with teacher information go to SDPO. If teacher information is missing, SRPO falls back to GRPO.

That last fallback matters. The method is not trying to force distillation to happen at all costs. It only uses the SDPO branch when dense correction is both useful and feasible.

The image makes the control flow easy to remember. The policy samples trajectories once. After that, the method splits the batch into two branches instead of forcing one universal objective to handle everything.

How the routing decision works

The routing rule is intentionally blunt.

For each rollout, SRPO checks two bits of information:

  • whether the rollout is correct,
  • whether teacher information exists for that rollout.

If a rollout is correct, it is routed to GRPO. If it is incorrect and teacher information exists, it is routed to SDPO. If it is incorrect but no teacher signal can be built, it also falls back to GRPO.

This is a clean design choice because it avoids the central weakness of advantage mixing. In a blended objective, every sample gets both signals, whether or not both signals are appropriate. SRPO refuses to do that. It keeps the reward-aligned update for trajectories that are already on track, and reserves distillation for the cases where extra guidance is actually needed.

The intuition is easy to remember:

  • correct trajectory: reward learning is enough,
  • incorrect trajectory with a good sibling: use dense correction,
  • incorrect trajectory without a teacher: stay with GRPO.

That is why the paper frames SRPO as sample routing rather than advantage mixing. It is not just mixing two losses with a hidden ratio. It is choosing the supervision signal per sample.

What SDPO becomes inside SRPO

SRPO does not use SDPO unchanged. It adds a confidence control inside the distillation branch.

The paper notices that the self-teacher’s uncertainty grows during training. When the teacher distribution becomes high entropy, its token-level targets are less trustworthy. So SRPO weights SDPO tokens by teacher confidence, using entropy as the signal.

The idea is straightforward:

  • low-entropy teacher predictions are more decisive,
  • high-entropy predictions are more ambiguous,
  • ambiguous tokens should count less in the update.

That gives Dynamic-Weighted SDPO, or DW-SDPO. It is still the same distillation objective, but each token is scaled by an entropy-based weight. Confident teacher tokens get emphasized. Uncertain ones get suppressed.

You can think of this as a soft filter. SRPO does not discard the whole distillation branch when the teacher gets noisier. It just trusts the reliable parts more than the shaky ones.

Why entropy weighting matters

This is the part that keeps SRPO from inheriting SDPO’s late-stage instability.

Late in training, the model and self-teacher get closer to each other. That sounds good, but it also means the teacher is often less informative. The paper uses entropy as a proxy for that unreliability. High entropy means the teacher is less certain about the next token, so its guidance should contribute less to the update.

That makes the SDPO branch more selective without changing its basic structure. SRPO still gets the benefit of dense supervision on failed samples, but it avoids letting noisy teacher logits dominate the gradient.

If you want a mental picture, imagine a tutor who still explains your mistakes, but speaks more softly when they are unsure. SRPO is doing that at the token level.

The final objective

Once the routing decision is made, the training objective is just a normalized sum of the two branches.

GRPO contributes token losses for the routed correct samples. DW-SDPO contributes weighted token losses for the routed incorrect samples. Then SRPO divides by the total number of routed tokens, so the final update stays on a consistent scale.

That normalization is a nice detail because it removes the need for a separate mixing hyperparameter. The balance between GRPO and SDPO is not chosen by hand. It changes automatically as the sample composition changes over training.

This is one of the method’s most elegant properties. Early in training, when failures are common, more tokens go through the SDPO branch, so dense correction has more influence. Later, as the policy improves and more rollouts become correct, the GRPO branch naturally takes over. The objective adapts to the model’s state instead of relying on a fixed schedule.

What the training dynamics look like

The paper’s training curves and routing statistics show that this design is not just conceptually neat, it also matches how learning actually evolves.

Early on, SRPO behaves a lot like SDPO: it corrects errors quickly and climbs fast. But because correct samples are no longer forced through self-distillation, it avoids the ambiguity that hurts SDPO later. At the same time, the entropy-aware weighting keeps unreliable teacher targets from taking over.

That is why SRPO can keep improving after SDPO has already flattened or collapsed. The GRPO branch acts like an anchor. The SDPO branch acts like a corrective tool. As the training mix shifts over time, the method gradually leans more on the stable branch.

This figure is useful because it shows the side effects of routing. SRPO does not just improve accuracy. It also keeps response length in a middle range and becomes cheaper over longer horizons because fewer samples need the SDPO branch as training progresses.

Why sample routing beats advantage mixing

The paper compares SRPO against an advantage-level mix and finds that routing is the better long-run strategy.

That result makes sense once you separate the two ideas.

Advantage mixing says: combine the GRPO and SDPO signals for every sample. Sample routing says: decide which signal should own this sample.

The first approach can work early, when teacher guidance is strong. But later, it spreads teacher noise into places where it no longer helps. The second approach avoids that. It keeps reward learning for correct samples and uses distillation only for failures that still have useful sibling information.

So the real lesson is not “always use both.” It is “use the right signal at the right granularity.”

What SRPO is really optimizing

The cleanest way to remember SRPO is to see it as a credit-assignment method.

GRPO answers the question: did this rollout deserve reinforcement? SDPO answers the question: where did this bad rollout go wrong, and what should it have done instead? SRPO chooses between those questions sample by sample.

That is why the paper calls it a unified on-policy framework. It stays on-policy throughout, but it changes the form of supervision based on sample status. Correct trajectories keep their reward-aligned training. Incorrect trajectories get denser correction. Unreliable teacher logits get downweighted instead of trusted blindly.

The result is a method that tries to keep the best part of each baseline:

  • SDPO’s fast early correction,
  • GRPO’s long-horizon stability,
  • and lower compute later in training because the distillation branch is used less often.

What to remember

SRPO is easiest to remember as “route the sample, then optimize.”

If the rollout is already correct, reward learning is enough, so send it to GRPO. If the rollout is wrong and a good teacher signal exists, send it to SDPO. If the teacher signal looks uncertain, trust it less by weighting tokens with entropy.

The method matters because it avoids forcing one training signal to do every job. That is the core idea behind the paper: when reward and distillation have different strengths, pick the signal per sample instead of blending them everywhere.
