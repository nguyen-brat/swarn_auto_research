Inference-Time Scaling for Generalist Reward Modeling

Why this paper matters

Most reward models are trained to behave like a single judge with a single habit. They either output a scalar score, or they choose one response over another, or they rely on some fixed critique template that is largely decided before inference ever begins. That works if the judgment problem is narrow and stable. It breaks down when the input format changes, when the response set grows from two candidates to many, or when the model needs to express why one response is better in a way that can be sampled, filtered, and aggregated later.

This paper is trying to loosen that bottleneck. Its central move is simple to say but unusual in practice: let the reward model generate the principles it uses to judge, not just the final score. Then spend more inference compute to sample several principle-and-critique bundles, filter the weak ones, and vote over the rest. In other words, the model is not only a scorer. It is also a generator of judgment criteria, and those criteria become the thing we scale.

That is what Self-Principled Critique Tuning, or SPCT, is really about. It turns reward generation into a learned, flexible language task, then uses inference-time sampling to widen the range of judgments the model can express. The result is a reward model that can rate single responses, paired responses, or larger candidate sets without being locked into a rigid pairwise or scalar format.

The key idea: the model should write its own rubric

The paper starts from a problem that shows up everywhere in reward modeling. Scalar heads are compact, but they are not very expressive. Pairwise models can pick a winner, but they struggle to rate one response on its own or to score many candidates in a uniform way. Generative reward models are more flexible because they can produce text, but without the right training recipe they may still behave like a brittle one-shot judge.

SPCT changes the role of the generated text. Instead of treating principles as a hand-written preamble or a fixed preprocessing step, it makes principles part of the model’s output. The model sees the query and the candidate responses, writes down the criteria it thinks matter, and then critiques the responses using those criteria. The paper’s Figure 2 is the cleanest way to visualize this shift.

What to notice here is the axis of flexibility. Scalar and pairwise systems compress judgment into a narrow interface. The generative pointwise setup keeps the whole response set in view and lets the model assign a separate reward to each response while still explaining the basis of that reward. That matters because once the reward is textual and pointwise, it can support one response, two responses, or many responses without changing the core interface.

So the paper is not just asking for more verbose output. It is asking the reward model to expose the criteria it is using, because those criteria can then be sampled, filtered, and aggregated like any other latent reasoning path.

How SPCT is trained

The pipeline has two stages: rejective fine-tuning and rule-based online RL. The first stage gives the model a cold start. The second stage teaches it to generate principles and critiques in a way that actually matches the correctness of the reward.

The reason for the cold start is practical. A reward model that has never been trained to talk in this format is unlikely to reliably produce useful principles on demand. So the paper first fine-tunes a pointwise generative reward model on instruction and preference data, then samples principle-and-critique trajectories from that model. Those sampled trajectories are checked against the labeled preference signal, and only the useful ones are kept.

This is the “rejective” part. The model is not just trained on everything it says. Incorrect trajectories are thrown out, and trajectories that are too easy are also discarded because they do not teach the model much. The paper even uses a hinted sampling variant during cold start, where the best response can be appended as a hint, but it reports that non-hinted sampling matters more overall because hinted trajectories can take shortcuts, especially on reasoning-heavy data.

That detail is worth remembering because it reveals the training philosophy. SPCT does not want a model that memorizes a rubric template. It wants a model that can recover a useful rubric from the input itself.

From principles to critiques

Once the model has a better cold start, SPCT moves into rule-based online RL. Here the model is rolled out on the same kind of input it will later see at inference time: a query plus one or more responses. It generates principles, then critiques, then pointwise rewards for each response.

The reward signal is intentionally simple. If the extracted pointwise rewards rank the labeled best response above the others, the rollout gets a positive outcome reward. If not, it gets a negative one. The paper pairs that accuracy-based reward with a stronger KL penalty so the model does not drift into odd formats or collapse toward a narrow set of benchmarks.

That choice keeps the training signal honest. The model is not rewarded for sounding clever. It is rewarded for generating principles and critiques that actually separate better responses from worse ones.

Seen as a data flow, the loop is:

  1. input a query and candidate responses,
  2. generate principles,
  3. critique the responses under those principles,
  4. extract pointwise rewards,
  5. compare the ranking to the label,
  6. update the model with correctness-based reinforcement.

The important part is that the principles are no longer outside the learning loop. They are what the model is learning to generate well.

Why the training recipe is not just a fancier prompt

It would be tempting to think SPCT is just prompt engineering with extra steps. That is not quite right. The paper’s real point is that principles become a learned part of reward generation, not a fixed rule sheet. That makes the model adaptive in a way prompt-only systems are not.

If the response set changes, the model can generate different criteria. If the task changes from a single response to a bundle of candidates, the same pointwise interface still works. If the model sees a hard case, it can spend more of its output budget on the principles and critiques that matter most. That is why the paper treats principles as something to generate, not something to precompute once and reuse forever.

The architectural shift is easy to miss, but it is the main thing that makes inference-time scaling possible later. A model cannot really scale by sampling if every sample is forced through the same tiny output bottleneck. SPCT widens that bottleneck by making the criteria themselves part of the output.

What the full pipeline looks like

The paper’s Figure 3 is the best single picture of the overall method.

What you should notice is the separation between training and inference. During training, SPCT first builds a useful generative reward model with rejective fine-tuning, then sharpens it with rule-based online RL. During inference, the model is not frozen into one deterministic judgment. It is allowed to sample multiple judgments, and those samples become the raw material for better scoring.

That is the paper’s main scaling intuition. More compute does not just mean more calls to the same fixed scorer. It means more opportunities to sample different principles, different critiques, and therefore different views of the same candidate responses.

How inference-time scaling actually works

Once the model has learned to generate criteria, the paper uses parallel sampling to make reward generation richer. Each sample produces a principle-and-critique bundle and a set of pointwise rewards. The final reward for each response is then obtained by voting across the samples.

The simplest way to think about the aggregation is:

S_i^* = sum_j S_{i,j}

Each sampled critique gives response i a discrete reward S_{i,j}, and those rewards are summed across samples. Because each individual reward lives in a small range, repeated sampling expands the effective reward space and gives the model finer granularity than a single pass ever could.

That is the subtle win here. Instead of asking one sample to produce the perfect judgment, SPCT asks many samples to produce slightly different judgment perspectives and then aggregates them. If the principles are good, those perspectives approximate a richer distribution over what counts as the better response.

The paper also shuffles responses before sampling to reduce positional bias. That matters because if the first response is always seen first, the model can accidentally learn an order preference rather than a quality preference. Shuffling keeps the vote focused on content.

Why the meta reward model exists

Sampling helps only if the samples are good enough to trust. Some principle-and-critique bundles will still be noisy, biased, or just weakly reasoned. The paper’s answer is to add a meta reward model, or MetaRM, whose job is to score the quality of the generated principles and critiques themselves.

This meta model is trained as a pointwise scalar reward model with binary classification-style supervision. It learns whether the generated judgment bundle is likely to be correct. Then, during inference, the system keeps only the top-ranked sampled bundles before voting.

That is a practical filter, but it is also conceptually important. The paper is saying that not every sampled judgment deserves equal weight. If one sample gets the rubric badly wrong, it should not be allowed to influence the final reward as much as a cleaner sample. MetaRM is the mechanism that makes the sampling-based scaling stable enough to matter.

You can think of it as a judge for the judge’s explanation.

What the evidence says

The empirical story is consistent with the mechanism. The SPCT-trained DeepSeek-GRM-27B reaches 69.9 overall, which is stronger than reproduced baselines such as LLM-as-a-Judge at 67.8. When inference-time scaling is turned up, MetaRM-guided voting at 32 samples reaches 72.8 overall.

That gap is the main headline. It shows that reward models can improve from more inference compute, not only from larger training runs. In this setting, sampling is not just a robustness trick. It is part of the scaling law.

The ablation on principle generation is equally revealing. Removing principle generation drops greedy performance from 69.9 to 67.5. That is a strong hint that the learned criteria are not decorative. They are a substantial part of the reward quality.

So the paper’s evidence supports a very specific claim: once the reward model learns to generate useful principles, repeated sampling of those principles becomes a real scaling knob.

Where the method still struggles

The paper is careful about the tradeoffs.

Generative reward models are slower than scalar heads. That is the first obvious cost. SPCT makes the model more expressive, but expressiveness is not free.

The second limitation is domain difficulty. The model still lags on some verifiable tasks, where exact factual checking is harder and scalar models can sometimes exploit sharper hidden signals. SPCT is flexible, but it is not magically better at every kind of judgment.

The third limitation is the one to watch most closely: principle quality can vary, and if the principles are biased then more sampling can amplify the wrong criteria instead of correcting them. The whole method depends on the model learning good rubrics, so bad rubrics can still leak through the system.

That is why the meta reward model matters so much. It is not just a nice addition. It is part of the safety rail for the inference-time scaling story.

What to remember

The easiest way to remember SPCT is this:

The reward model learns to explain how it judges, not just what it chooses.

Then inference-time compute is spent on sampling several of those explanations, filtering the weak ones, and voting over the resulting pointwise rewards. That makes the reward model more like a distribution over judgments than a single fixed score.

Three ideas carry the method:

  • Principles are learned, not hand-fixed.
  • Sampling is a scaling mechanism, not just a robustness trick.
  • Meta filtering keeps noisy critiques from dominating the vote.

If you hold onto those three ideas, the rest of the paper becomes much easier to reconstruct. SPCT is basically an attempt to make reward modeling more like an active, explainable judgment process, and then to scale that process at inference time instead of only scaling it during training.
