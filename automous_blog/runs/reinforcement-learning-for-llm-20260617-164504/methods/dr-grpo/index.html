Understanding R1-Zero: Analysis of Base Models and Reinforcement Learning

This paper is less about inventing yet another reasoning recipe and more about asking a harder question: when an R1-Zero-style system looks better after RL, how much of that improvement is really coming from the optimizer, and how much was already hiding in the base model or the prompt template?

That framing matters. If the base model already behaves like a chat model before training, then some of the apparent RL gain is not “reasoning emerging from nowhere.” It is the model being nudged into the right format. And if the optimizer itself quietly favors longer or easier-looking trajectories, then training may reward the wrong thing even when the benchmark score goes up.

The paper’s answer is to separate the pipeline into two parts and inspect both. First, it studies what the base model can already do with different templates. Then it zooms in on GRPO and shows that its normalization choices can bias the update toward shorter correct answers and longer incorrect ones. The proposed fix, Dr. GRPO, keeps the same basic RL setup but removes those hidden weighting effects.

What to notice in this figure is the reweighting effect. The group mean is fine as a baseline, but the extra division by response length and by per-question standard deviation changes who gets the strongest update. That means two questions with the same underlying reward can still shape training very differently.

The first surprise: some “base” models are not equally base

The paper starts by treating template choice as part of the method, not just a cosmetic prompt detail. That is a useful move. If a model was pretrained on question-answer text, then a prompt template that forces it into a different mode can hide capabilities that were already there.

The authors test this with several open models and three prompt styles: the R1 template, the Qwen-Math template, and no template at all. The result is a warning sign for any R1-Zero reproduction effort. Some models become better at answering only after the right template is added. But Qwen2.5-Math behaves differently: it often does best with no template, which suggests that its pretraining already makes it behave like an answerer before RL begins.

That is why the paper is careful about causal language. If a model already scores well without a template, then RL may not be creating the capability so much as exposing it. In the paper’s numbers, Qwen2.5-Math-7B jumps from 26.5 average accuracy with the R1 template to 38.2 with no template. That is not a small formatting effect. It is a sign that prompt choice can move the baseline a lot before any policy update happens.

The same logic explains the authors’ focus on DeepSeek-V3-Base. They find signs of self-reflection-like behavior there too, including “Aha” and “wait” style responses, before RL tuning. That does not prove the model is already reasoning at the final R1-Zero level, but it does mean that the famous “Aha moment” should not be treated as an unambiguous signature of training alone.

The RL loop in plain language

Once the paper turns to reinforcement learning, the setup becomes more familiar: sample multiple responses for each question, score them with a rule-based verifier, and push the policy toward the better trajectories.

The important design choice is that the reward is outcome-based and binary. A response is either correct or incorrect. There is no learned reward model here. That makes the setup cleaner, but it also means the optimizer has to extract learning signal from a very sparse verdict.

The authors also remove the KL penalty. In the usual RLHF setting, KL keeps the policy from drifting too far from a reference model. Here, the reward comes from a verifier rather than a learned preference model, so the distribution-shift concern is weaker. Dropping KL also simplifies training and reduces compute overhead.

So the training loop is intentionally minimal:

  1. sample several candidate solutions for a question,
  2. verify each one,
  3. compute a group-relative advantage,
  4. update the policy with PPO-style clipping.

That sounds standard until you look closely at the normalization terms.

Where GRPO quietly changes the objective

The paper’s main optimizer critique is that GRPO is not just PPO without a critic. It also adds two normalizations that shift the learning signal.

The first is response-length normalization. Dividing by the number of generated tokens means shorter correct answers get a stronger positive update, while longer incorrect answers get a weaker negative update. That sounds harmless until you realize what it encourages: brevity for right answers and leniency for wrong ones. In a reasoning model, that is a dangerous mix because the update is no longer saying “make correct trajectories more likely” in a neutral way. It is also saying “prefer shorter correct trajectories and tolerate longer wrong trajectories.”

The second is question-level standard-deviation normalization. If a question’s sampled responses have very similar rewards, the standard deviation is small, which makes the normalized advantage large. That means easy or hard questions can end up overweighted simply because their reward spread is narrow. Instead of treating each question as one training unit with comparable weight, GRPO gives some questions much louder gradients than others.

The key point is that both effects are bias, not just variance reduction. The mean baseline is fine. The extra divisions are what tilt the update.

The paper then makes this concrete by noting that the same length bias appears in popular open-source PPO implementations too. So the issue is bigger than one algorithm name. It is a common implementation habit that accidentally departs from the unbiased PPO objective.

Dr. GRPO: the small fix that restores the clean update

Dr. GRPO is deliberately boring in the best possible way. It does not add a new critic, a new reward model, or a new architectural trick. It simply removes the two normalizations that were doing the damage.

In practice, that means:

  • keep the group-mean baseline,
  • drop standard-deviation normalization,
  • drop response-length normalization,
  • and use a constant generation budget when averaging masked losses, rather than normalizing by however many tokens happened to be produced.

That last detail is easy to miss, but it matters. If your loss averages over the actual response length, then the model can still be nudged toward the wrong length preference. Averaging over a fixed budget keeps the update aligned with the PPO-style objective the authors want to recover.

The result is an unbiased policy-gradient update that behaves much closer to the textbook objective. The paper also points out that the resulting advantage is equivalent, up to a scale factor, to a leave-one-out style estimator. The scaling can be absorbed into the learning rate, so the practical dynamics are preserved.

In other words, Dr. GRPO is not a new philosophy. It is the same RL recipe with the hidden weighting removed.

What changes once the bias is gone

The payoff shows up in two places: token efficiency and evaluation behavior.

This is the figure where the story becomes visible. Both GRPO and Dr. GRPO can improve reward over time, and both can increase response length as training proceeds. But the pattern is not the same. GRPO keeps stretching responses even when the reward improvement slows, while Dr. GRPO keeps the length growth much more controlled.

That distinction is important because long responses are not automatically better reasoning. The paper’s argument is that some of the famous “long-CoT emergence” can be a training artifact: if the optimizer rewards long incorrect answers too gently, the model can drift toward verbosity without actually becoming more accurate.

Dr. GRPO reduces that drift. The paper reports that incorrect responses become much shorter under the unbiased update, which is exactly what you would want if you are trying to remove overthinking rather than amplify it.

The benchmark result is consistent with that story. The minimal recipe, using Qwen2.5-Math-7B with the Qwen-Math setup and Dr. GRPO on MATH level 3-5 questions, reaches 43.3% on AIME 2024. That is the paper’s strongest evidence that the fix is not just cleaner mathematically; it is also practically useful.

The bigger lesson about R1-Zero-style training

The most useful takeaway here is not “use Dr. GRPO” in isolation. It is that R1-Zero-style post-training has three separate levers, and they can easily be confused with one another:

  1. the base model’s pretraining,
  2. the prompt template,
  3. the RL optimizer.

If the base model is already math-savvy, if the template already elicits answer-style behavior, or if the optimizer favors the wrong trajectories, then training curves can look better than the actual reasoning story really is.

That is why the paper’s method section feels so diagnostic. It does not just propose a fix. It shows where the illusion can come from.

What to remember

Dr. GRPO is best remembered as the “remove the hidden bias” version of GRPO.

The method keeps the same basic group-relative RL loop, but it stops dividing by response length and per-question variance. That makes the update closer to an unbiased PPO-style policy gradient. The benefit is simple: less artificial verbosity, less overweighting of easy or degenerate cases, and better token efficiency without giving up reasoning accuracy.

The broader lesson is even more important. In reasoning RL, a good result can come from the base model, the template, or the optimizer. If you do not separate those pieces, it is very easy to mistake formatting and weighting artifacts for genuine reasoning gains.
