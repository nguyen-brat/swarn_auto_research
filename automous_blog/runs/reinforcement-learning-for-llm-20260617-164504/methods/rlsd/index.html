Self-Distilled RLVR

RLSD starts from a simple complaint about two familiar training styles for reasoning models.

Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards gives you a trustworthy yes-or-no signal from the environment, but that signal is coarse. Every token in a long answer gets the same sequence-level treatment, even though only a few words may have actually made the answer correct. On-policy self-distillation gives the opposite kind of signal: it can be dense and token-specific, but if the teacher’s advice depends on privileged information that the student cannot see, the model can start learning the hidden shortcut instead of the reasoning itself.

RLSD, or Self-Distilled RLVR, is the attempt to keep the good part of both worlds while removing the failure mode. The key move is not to let the teacher decide the update direction. The verifier still decides that. The teacher is used only to redistribute how much credit each token gets.

That distinction sounds small, but it is the whole method.

The idea in one sentence

Think of the verifier as the judge that decides whether an answer should be reinforced or punished, and think of the teacher as a credit scaler that decides which tokens in that answer deserved more of that reinforcement or punishment.

The paper’s own summary of the design makes this explicit: the environment reward stays the update direction, while the teacher-student gap becomes a stop-gradient weight that reshapes token credit.

Why plain self-distillation can go wrong

Before looking at RLSD, it helps to understand what it is refusing to do.

In ordinary on-policy self-distillation, the teacher is effectively telling the student what distribution to imitate along the student’s own rollouts. If the teacher conditions on privileged information such as a reference trace or a verified answer path, the student is asked to match a target that it can never fully observe. The paper argues that this mismatch is structural, not just an optimization hiccup.

The practical symptom is leakage. Instead of becoming better at solving the task from the visible input alone, the model starts encoding traces of the hidden reference into its parameters. Training may look good early on, but later it drifts or collapses because the student is learning to depend on information that will not exist at inference time.

The important lesson is not “privileged information is bad”. The lesson is more precise: privileged information is dangerous when it enters the gradient direction. If it only changes how strongly tokens are weighted, it becomes much safer.

In the method diagram, the split is the thing to notice. One branch stays anchored to the verifier. The other branch computes token-level weights from the teacher-student gap. RLSD is built around that separation.

What flows through the method

The pipeline is short enough to keep in your head:

  1. Sample a student rollout on-policy.
  2. Score the whole rollout with a verifier reward.
  3. Compute, for each token, how much the privileged teacher shifts its probability relative to the student.
  4. Turn that shift into a bounded token weight.
  5. Use the weighted advantage in a GRPO-style policy update.

The subtle part is step 3 and step 4.

For a generated token y_t, RLSD compares the teacher’s log-probability for that token with the student’s log-probability. That gap is treated as an evidence ratio: if the privileged context makes the token more plausible, the token gets more credit; if it makes it less plausible, the token gets less credit or more blame.

But the paper applies stop-gradient to that ratio. That means the teacher signal is used as a scalar for weighting, not as a path for gradient flow. The teacher can reshape the magnitude of the update, but it cannot sneak into the direction of the update.

That is the central safety property.

How the credit weighting works

RLSD first computes a per-token gain from the teacher-student log-probability gap.

The intuition is easy to remember:

  • if the privileged answer trace strongly supports a token, that token probably mattered for the correct reasoning path;
  • if it weakly supports or opposes a token, that token probably contributed less, or even pushed the rollout off track.

RLSD then turns that gain into a positive weight and modulates it by the sign of the sequence-level advantage. So the environment reward still says whether the whole trajectory should be reinforced or penalized, while the teacher decides where inside that trajectory the credit should concentrate.

This is a more precise version of the usual GRPO treatment. GRPO gives every token in a rollout the same advantage. RLSD keeps the same verifier-grounded sign, but redistributes the magnitude across tokens. In practice, that means the model can pay more attention to the crucial reasoning steps and less attention to generic filler.

You can see why this matters in long reasoning traces. A single correct final answer may depend on only one or two decisive manipulations. If the entire response gets identical credit, those decisive tokens are diluted. If the token weights are too noisy, the model chases the wrong details. RLSD tries to sit in the middle: dense enough to separate useful tokens from filler, but still anchored to the environment reward.

The paper’s diagram of the update

The easiest way to keep RLSD straight is to follow the data path:

question + student rollout -> verifier reward -> teacher-student evidence ratio -> clipped token weights -> policy update

Two parts of that path never change:

  • the student still samples on-policy;
  • the reward sign still comes from the verifier.

Everything else is about how much each token contributes to the final update.

That is why the method is best thought of as credit assignment, not distillation in the ordinary behavioral-cloning sense. RLSD does not try to reproduce the teacher’s token distribution. It uses the teacher only to answer a narrower question: “Which tokens on this rollout deserved more or less credit?”

Why the stop-gradient matters

The paper emphasizes stop-gradient because it is what prevents the teacher from becoming a new hidden objective.

Without that barrier, the teacher-student gap could itself become part of the optimization direction, which would pull the student toward the privileged context in the same way ordinary self-distillation does. With stop-gradient, the gap is treated as a fixed weighting signal for the current update. It influences magnitude, not direction.

That makes RLSD fundamentally different from the failing self-distillation setup analyzed in the paper:

  • the verifier decides whether the trajectory is good or bad;
  • the teacher decides which tokens were more informative;
  • the student’s own rollout supplies the support that receives gradients.

That three-way separation is the reason the method can use privileged information without inheriting the leakage problem.

Why clipping and lambda decay are there

RLSD does not let token weights grow without bound.

The paper clips the evidence weights, which keeps any one token from dominating the whole update. This plays the same stabilizing role as the clipping term in PPO or GRPO: it makes the optimization more conservative and prevents a single noisy token from hijacking training.

The method also uses a mixing coefficient lambda that starts by letting the teacher have some influence and then decays toward plain GRPO behavior. That schedule is a practical compromise. Early in training, when the model still needs finer steering, the teacher signal is most useful. Later, when the policy is already more capable, the model can rely more on the verifier alone.

That schedule is also one of the paper’s stated limitations: the best decay pattern may vary by task family.

What the training dynamics tell you

The training-dynamics figure is worth reading as a story rather than as a set of curves.

The main thing to notice is that RLSD climbs faster than plain GRPO and settles at a better ceiling, while avoiding the late collapse seen in self-distillation-only training. The entropy curve is also informative: RLSD keeps more diversity than uniform sequence-level reinforcement, because it is not forcing every token into the same blunt update. And the clipping activity stays modest, which is a sign that the token-level weights are being used as a bounded refinement rather than as an uncontrolled force.

That makes the method feel less like a new optimizer and more like a smarter way to spend the same reward signal.

A concrete way to remember it

If you want a one-line memory hook, use this:

RLSD uses the teacher to decide where credit goes, but the verifier to decide which direction the policy should move.

Or even shorter:

teacher = credit scaler, verifier = direction.

That is the design axis that separates RLSD from the self-distillation methods it replaces. The privileged signal is still useful, but it is no longer allowed to steer the whole gradient.

Where it helps and where to be careful

The paper’s best evidence comes from multimodal reasoning, where token-level credit assignment is especially important. The reported gains are strongest on tasks that benefit from fine-grained reasoning credit, and the paper notes that teacher logits add a small extra forward pass per response.

So the tradeoff is real:

  • you pay for one more teacher forward pass;
  • you get much better token-level credit assignment than uniform RLVR;
  • you avoid the leakage and late-stage degradation that come from using self-distillation as a distribution-matching target.

That also explains the open questions the paper leaves behind. It is still worth asking how well the same design transfers to pure text reasoning, which lambda schedule generalizes best, and whether weaker or stale teachers would preserve the same effect.

What to remember

RLSD is not “self-distillation plus RL” in the naive sense. It is a very specific compromise:

  • the environment reward keeps ownership of the update sign;
  • the teacher-student gap becomes a stop-gradient weight;
  • the weight redistributes credit across tokens instead of imitating the teacher distribution;
  • clipping and lambda decay keep the credit redistribution stable.

That is why the method can use privileged information without turning that information into a leakage channel.
