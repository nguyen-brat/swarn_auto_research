1-Shot RLVR

The most important idea in this paper is easy to state and surprisingly hard to believe at first: for RLVR on math reasoning, a carefully chosen single example can sometimes do nearly the same job as a training set with thousands of examples.

That sounds like an extreme compression trick, but the paper is not claiming that one example contains all the math the model needs. The real claim is more subtle. A base model already has a lot of latent reasoning ability. The right RLVR example can act like a lever that nudges that ability into a better search pattern. Once exploration is working, the model can improve far beyond the single prompt it was trained on.

The paper calls this setup 1-shot RLVR.

The surprise in one picture

Here is the headline result the whole method is built around:

What to notice is the gap between the tiny green setups and the much larger blue ones. On Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B, one selected example reaches essentially the same MATH500 score as the 1.2k-example DeepScaleR subset, and two examples can even edge it out. That is the core empirical shock of the paper.

The paper is not saying data never matters. It is saying that, for RLVR, the choice of example can matter more than the raw size of the dataset.

The method as a pipeline

The easiest way to understand 1-shot RLVR is as a short pipeline:

  1. run RLVR on a full training pool,
  2. score examples by how much their training accuracy fluctuates over time,
  3. keep one or a few high-leverage examples,
  4. train with GRPO or PPO using a binary correctness reward,
  5. let exploration, not dataset breadth, do the heavy lifting,
  6. check whether the gains transfer beyond the training item itself.

That sounds simple, but each step exists for a reason.

The first step is especially important. The paper does not pick a random single example and hope for the best. It first trains on the full pool and records each example’s historical training accuracy across epochs. Then it ranks examples by variance:

v_i := var(s_{i,1}, ..., s_{i,E})

where s_{i,e} is the training accuracy of example i at epoch e.

The intuition is plain: examples whose accuracy moves around more during training are often the ones that most strongly shape the policy. They seem to expose useful uncertainty in the model, which makes them good candidates for a tiny RLVR dataset.

This is a heuristic, not a proof. The paper is explicit that it is not necessarily the optimal selector. But it is a clean, cheap way to find examples that matter.

Why the chosen examples are so effective

One of the paper’s most interesting observations is that the best example is not always a hard example. In fact, the selected pi_1 example is almost already solvable by the base model.

That matters because it tells you what 1-shot RLVR is doing.

The model is not learning math from scratch. It is already close to the right answer on the training prompt. The training example mainly nudges the model toward a better reasoning trajectory, especially on the last few steps where search can go wrong.

For pi_1, the model often gets almost all the way to the answer before stumbling on the final cube root calculation. That is a perfect shape for RLVR: the model has enough signal to explore useful reasoning, but enough uncertainty that reward can still shape the policy.

The paper also shows that a more compact prompt, which keeps only the last hard step, works worse. So the benefit is not just about isolating the answer bottleneck. The surrounding reasoning context seems to matter because it gives the model more room to explore different solution paths.

What actually happens during training

Once training starts, the model is optimizing a standard verifiable-reward RL objective.

The reward is binary: if the final answer matches the ground truth, the response gets reward 1, otherwise 0. The training recipe uses GRPO by default, and the loss includes:

  • policy gradient loss, which pushes up responses that do better than the batch average,
  • KL loss, which keeps the policy from drifting too far,
  • entropy loss, which encourages more diverse token choices and therefore more exploration.

The important detail is that the reward is about the final answer, not the quality of the chain of thought. That means the model has freedom to discover whatever internal reasoning path works best, as long as the final answer is correct.

The paper also duplicates the tiny dataset when needed so the training batch requirements are satisfied. That is a practical detail, but it reveals something important: the method is not making the optimization easier by changing the batch mechanics. It is really asking whether a tiny amount of real RL signal can still drive learning.

Why test performance keeps rising after training accuracy saturates

This is the part of the paper that feels most counterintuitive at first.

With only one training example, the model’s training accuracy saturates quickly. That much is expected. What is surprising is that test performance keeps improving after that saturation point. The paper calls this post-saturation generalization.

Think about what that means. The model has already learned to answer the one training item correctly almost all the time. Yet it keeps getting better on held-out math benchmarks. So the improvement is not just memorization of one prompt. The training example is acting more like a pressure point that keeps reshaping the policy even after the example itself is mastered.

The paper shows that this is not identical to grokking. In grokking, the model often memorizes first and only later generalizes sharply. Here, the training dynamics are different. The policy gradient signal can remain active because entropy keeps the output distribution diverse enough that occasional errors still happen. Those occasional errors keep the advantage non-zero, which means the policy gradient does not fully vanish.

That is why entropy matters so much in this setup.

Exploration is the hidden ingredient

If the model becomes too deterministic too early, 1-shot RLVR loses steam.

The paper’s ablations show that policy gradient loss is the main driver of the gains, but entropy helps the model keep exploring after the single example is already mostly solved. That extra exploration seems to open up new reasoning traces and new test-time behaviors.

This also explains another empirical pattern: as training progresses, the model’s responses on the training example get longer and more varied, and the test-time responses become more reflective. The paper tracks words like “rethink”, “recheck”, and “recalculate” and sees them become more frequent later in training. So the model is not just getting more accurate. It is also becoming more willing to pause, reconsider, and search again.

In other words, the selected example does not merely teach an answer. It seems to encourage a more active reasoning style.

The gains are not limited to one category

Another useful detail is that category labels are not a reliable predictor of transfer.

You might expect a geometry example to mainly help geometry. The paper shows that this is too simple. A single example can improve performance across multiple math categories, and sometimes the biggest gains show up in categories different from the one the example came from.

That is one reason the historical variance score is interesting. It is not trying to identify a topical representative. It is trying to find examples that activate useful policy shifts. The best example is not necessarily the most obvious one on the surface.

The paper also shows that this effect crosses model families and algorithms. The same basic pattern appears with Qwen2.5-Math-7B, Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B, GRPO, and PPO. The exact gains vary, but the pattern survives.

What the ablation study says about the mechanism

The ablation section is the paper’s clearest explanation of why 1-shot RLVR works.

The takeaway is:

  • policy gradient loss does most of the work,
  • KL loss is helpful for stability but not the main source of improvement,
  • entropy loss can improve post-saturation generalization,
  • weight decay does not explain the effect the way it would in a grokking story.

That last point matters. The paper is trying to separate this phenomenon from a generic regularization story. The result is that 1-shot RLVR looks much more like a reward-driven exploration problem than a standard implicit-regularization puzzle.

The paper also tests entropy-loss-only training. Even without the outcome reward, entropy alone can improve some metrics for a few steps. That suggests the exploration pressure itself is part of the story, although it is still weaker than the full reward-based setup.

Format correction is real, but it is not the whole story

It would be easy to dismiss the gains as format fixing. The paper does not let that explanation stand on its own.

Format reward alone does improve performance, so some of the benefit is definitely about getting the answer into the right output shape. But the paper shows that outcome reward with a good selected example still does better than format reward alone. It also checks that the model is not just stuffing more answers into \boxed{} while leaving reasoning unchanged.

That distinction is important:

  • format reward helps the model present answers correctly,
  • outcome reward helps the model find better answers,
  • 1-shot RLVR gets both effects when the example is good.

The paper even compares RLVR with one-shot in-context learning. The one-example prompt helps, but RLVR still does better. So the gain is not just about showing the model an example in the prompt. The online RL updates matter.

Where the method is fragile

The method is powerful, but it is not magic.

The main limitations are straightforward:

  • the experiments are mostly math-focused,
  • the best single example depends on the model,
  • small datasets can still require many RL steps,
  • bad or incorrect labels can hurt badly if they are easy for the model to overfit,
  • entropy needs care because too much of it makes training unstable.

That last point is worth stressing. Exploration is good, but not unlimited exploration. The paper’s story only works when the model is still anchored by a correct verifiable signal.

So the practical lesson is not “any one example will work.” It is “a correct, high-leverage example plus controlled exploration can do much more than you would expect.”

What to remember

If you want the shortest memory hook for this paper, it is this:

1-shot RLVR says that the bottleneck in reasoning RL is not always dataset size. Sometimes it is whether you picked the right example to unlock exploration.

Or even shorter:

the model already has the reasoning seed, and one well-chosen verifiable example can be enough to make it grow.
