Rethinking On-Policy Distillation of Large Language Models: Phenomenology, Mechanism, and Recipe

On-policy distillation, or OPD, sounds like it should be straightforward. Let the student generate its own rollout, ask the teacher how much each token should be preferred, and update the student from that dense signal. In practice, though, the paper shows that OPD behaves less like a universal recipe and more like a compatibility test. A stronger teacher is not automatically a better distiller. Sometimes the student learns quickly. Sometimes nothing happens. Sometimes the student even regresses.

The paper’s main message is easy to remember:

OPD works when the student can already reach the teacher’s useful support region, and the teacher still has something new to teach.

That sounds abstract, but the method becomes much clearer once you follow the data flow. The student produces a trajectory. The teacher scores the student’s visited prefixes. Training then pushes the student toward the teacher’s distribution on those same states. So the real question is not just “which teacher is better?” It is “can the student and teacher meet on the same high-probability tokens, and does the teacher still know more than the student?”

The method in one picture

The paper’s diagnostics are built around a few simple signals: overlap between student and teacher top-k tokens, the advantage on the shared tokens, and the entropy gap between the two distributions. When OPD succeeds, those signals move together in a very particular way.

What to notice here is the shape of the learning process. Successful runs do not magically jump to a better policy. They gradually align on the same high-probability tokens the student already visits. Failing runs, by contrast, start with a support mismatch and never really escape it. The teacher may still look globally better on benchmarks, but that does not matter if the student cannot land on the same local support where the teacher’s signal lives.

That is the first big idea in the paper: OPD is local. It operates on the student’s own rollouts, so the teacher’s reward signal is only useful if the student already wanders into a region the teacher can meaningfully shape.

Why a better teacher can still fail

The easiest mistake is to assume that teacher quality alone determines whether distillation succeeds. The paper shows that this is not true.

The authors compare teachers that are similar in score but different in thinking pattern, and they find that the teacher with the better overlap dynamics often wins, even when its benchmark score is lower. That means the student is not just copying “correctness.” It is copying a reasoning style, or at least learning a distributional pattern that it can actually follow.

This is where the paper’s two conditions come in:

  1. the teacher and student need compatible thinking patterns,
  2. the teacher needs genuinely new knowledge, not just a larger version of the same behavior.

Those two conditions are easy to conflate, but the paper separates them carefully. A teacher can be locally alignable and still unhelpful if it teaches nothing new. A teacher can be stronger and still useless if its thinking pattern lives too far from the student’s current policy.

Reverse distillation makes this especially vivid. If you distill a post-RL student back toward its pre-RL checkpoint, the student can slide back toward its old behavior. Even a larger teacher from the same family can induce the same regression if it does not add new information on the student’s visited states. So the method is not asking for a generic “better model.” It is asking for a teacher that is both nearby and informative.

What OPD is actually optimizing

The formal objective is a reverse KL between the student and teacher, computed on the student’s own rollout prefixes. You do not need the notation to understand the effect. The reverse KL is mode-seeking, so it pressures the student to concentrate mass where the teacher already assigns high probability, rather than spreading mass widely across the teacher’s full support.

That detail is the key to the entire mechanism. OPD does not try to copy the whole teacher distribution uniformly. It tries to pull the student toward the teacher’s preferred modes on states the student itself visited.

The overlap region is where learning really happens

The most useful empirical result in the paper is that successful OPD does not just show some overlap. The overlap itself grows during training, and it carries almost all of the probability mass.

The paper reports that in successful runs the overlap ratio rises from about 72% to above 91%, while the overlapping tokens account for roughly 97% to 99% of the total probability mass. That means the shared support is not a tiny fringe of the distribution. It is the bulk of it.

The paper also tracks overlap-token advantage and entropy gap. Those two signals answer slightly different questions:

  • overlap ratio asks whether the student and teacher are looking at the same candidate tokens,
  • overlap-token advantage asks whether the student is placing the right amount of mass on those shared tokens,
  • entropy gap asks whether the two distributions have matched confidence on the same visited states.

When OPD succeeds, all three move in the same direction: overlap rises, the advantage moves toward zero, and entropy narrows. When OPD fails, they stagnate. That pattern is important because it tells you what the method is doing mechanically. It is not merely making the student “more like” the teacher in some vague sense. It is progressively aligning the student’s local token preferences with the teacher’s local support.

Why the shared tokens matter more than the rest

The paper goes one step further and asks whether the overlap region is just a symptom or the actual source of learning. The answer is surprisingly direct: optimizing only the overlap tokens is almost enough.

That is a strong clue about the geometry of OPD. The useful gradient is concentrated on the tokens both models already consider plausible. The non-overlap tokens contribute much less. In other words, OPD is not mostly about discovering brand-new modes from scratch. It is about sharpening and reweighting a shared high-probability region.

This is the most compact mental model for the method:

OPD is a progressive alignment process on the shared high-probability support of student and teacher.

Once you internalize that, the rest of the paper becomes easier to read. A run succeeds when the student can enter the teacher’s support region and then refine its mass there. A run fails when the student never really reaches that region, or when the teacher’s support does not contain anything new worth transferring.

How to rescue a bad setup

The paper does not stop at diagnosis. It also gives two practical repair strategies for failing OPD.

The first is off-policy cold start. Before OPD, you fine-tune the student on teacher-generated rollouts. This does not change the teacher or the objective. It simply moves the student closer to the teacher’s thinking pattern before the on-policy phase begins. That gives OPD a better starting point, so the overlap is higher from the first step and the later token-level signal is easier to exploit.

The second is teacher-aligned prompt selection. If the prompts better match the teacher’s post-training data, the student’s generated states become more compatible with the teacher, which sharpens the high-probability alignment. The paper also shows that prompt alignment can lower student entropy too much, so the practical version should mix aligned prompts with out-of-distribution prompts instead of using only the teacher’s own data.

Both aim at the same bottleneck: getting the student and teacher into a shared region where token-level distillation becomes usable again.

Why the teacher’s score is not enough

One of the paper’s sharpest points is that benchmark score and distillability are not the same thing. A teacher can score better and still be a worse OPD teacher.

That sounds counterintuitive until you remember that OPD is local. The student never sees the teacher’s benchmark score directly during training. It only sees teacher probabilities on the prefixes it itself generated. If the teacher’s extra ability is not present in that local region, then the score advantage never becomes a useful training signal.

So the paper’s “new knowledge” condition is not just a statement about scale. It is a statement about transferability. The teacher must contain some capability the student has not already internalized through its own training. Otherwise, reverse distillation can be surprisingly toothless, or even harmful.

The reverse-distillation experiments make that point harder to ignore. Distilling a post-RL model toward its earlier checkpoint can erase the improvement that RL had created. That is a strong sign that OPD is not passively preserving quality. It is actively pulling the student toward whatever the teacher thinks is likely on the student’s visited states.

The ceiling: dense supervision gets less reliable as trajectories grow

The promise of OPD is dense supervision. Every token gets a reward-like signal from the teacher, which is much richer than a single outcome label. But the paper also shows the cost: this dense signal gets less trustworthy as the trajectory gets longer.

What to notice in this figure is the long-horizon ceiling. Very short responses do not provide enough supervision. Moderate lengths work best. But as the trajectory gets longer, reward quality degrades, instability starts at later tokens, and the problem propagates backward through the sequence. The teacher is still globally informative in the sense that correct rollouts tend to score higher than incorrect ones, but that does not mean the signal is locally easy to exploit.

This is a subtle but important distinction:

  • a globally informative reward can still be locally flat or anisotropic,
  • a locally useful reward is what OPD actually needs.

The paper’s hypothesis is that later tokens drift farther from states the teacher naturally expects, so the teacher’s per-token guidance becomes noisier and harder to turn into a coherent update. That is why long-horizon reasoning and agentic interaction remain an open challenge for OPD.

What sampled-token and top-k supervision are really doing

The paper also uses the token-level analysis to answer a practical question: how dense does the teacher supervision need to be?

The answer is that you do not need to supervise every token in the full vocabulary to get the basic effect. Sampled-token OPD already works well, and moderate top-k variants do too. The degenerate case is top-1, which becomes too brittle because a tiny ranking change can flip the selected token and make the signal unstable.

That result reinforces the same lesson from the earlier sections. OPD does not need maximal support. It needs the right support. The useful signal comes from a small, high-probability region that is stable enough to align student and teacher, but not so narrow that training becomes mode-locked.

How to remember the method

The easiest way to remember this paper is to think of OPD as a three-part test:

  1. can the student visit the teacher’s useful region?
  2. does the teacher have something new in that region?
  3. can the overlap region grow once training starts?

If the answer to all three is yes, OPD tends to work. If not, the paper suggests two repairs: warm the student up with teacher rollouts, or give OPD prompts that better match the teacher’s post-training states.

That is what makes this paper useful. It turns OPD from a somewhat mysterious post-training trick into a diagnosis-and-repair recipe. The student is not copying the teacher wholesale. It is learning to enter and refine a shared support region where the teacher’s novel knowledge can actually be used.

If you remember only one sentence, make it this:

OPD succeeds when the student and teacher can meet on shared high-probability tokens, and the teacher still has new information waiting there.
