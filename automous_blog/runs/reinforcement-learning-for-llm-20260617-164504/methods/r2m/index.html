R2M: Real-Time Aligned Reward Model

R2M is built around a simple but important observation: in RLHF, the policy does not stay still, but the reward model often does. That mismatch is one of the main reasons reward overoptimization shows up. The policy keeps learning new habits, the reward model keeps scoring with old assumptions, and the policy eventually finds ways to exploit whatever the reward model still likes.

This paper’s answer is to make the reward model listen to the policy’s own evolving hidden states. Instead of treating the reward model as a frozen judge that only sees the surface prompt-response pair, R2M feeds in the policy’s latest last-layer states, fuses them with a lightweight cross-attention update, and then refreshes the reward head with a mixed ranking-and-entropy objective. The effect is not “retrain the whole reward model from scratch.” It is closer to “keep the judge synchronized with the model it is judging.”

The core idea in one picture

What to notice here is the loop. The policy generates trajectories, those trajectories carry hidden states, the reward model absorbs that policy feedback, and the updated reward model is then used in the next RL step. That is the whole method in one glance.

R2M is not trying to replace RLHF. It is trying to repair one weak part of the RLHF stack: the reward model going stale while the policy changes.

Why a frozen reward model starts to fail

In standard RLHF, the reward model is usually trained once from preference data and then reused while the policy keeps moving. That is fine at the beginning, when the policy is still close to the data distribution the reward model saw. But later, the policy drifts. It starts producing response styles, lengths, phrasing tricks, and failure modes that were not well represented in the reward model’s training snapshot.

That is the opening for reward overoptimization. If the reward model still likes a superficial cue, the policy can learn to amplify that cue without becoming genuinely better. The paper’s motivation is that this is not just a data problem. It is also a synchronization problem.

So the question becomes: what is the cheapest useful signal that tells the reward model how the policy is changing right now?

R2M’s answer is the policy’s own hidden states.

The workflow from policy to reward and back

The method follows a tight iterative loop:

  1. sample trajectories from the current policy,
  2. extract the policy’s last-layer hidden states,
  3. send those states into the reward model as policy feedback,
  4. update the reward model with the GREBT loss,
  5. use the refreshed reward model in the next RL iteration.

This algorithm view is useful because it shows what R2M changes and what it leaves alone. The policy optimization loop is still there. The main addition is a lightweight reward-model refresh step after policy updates, so the scorer stays closer to the policy’s current behavior.

The practical point is that R2M does not require new labeled preference data. It reuses the policy’s own hidden trajectory as a live signal.

How the reward model actually sees policy feedback

The reward model still begins the way a normal reward model does: it processes the query-response pair and produces a reward token embedding, the compact representation that the scoring head would normally consume.

R2M adds a second input stream. From the policy, it collects the last-layer hidden states for the same query-response pair. Those hidden states are not just semantic summaries. They also encode how the policy itself is internally representing the sequence at the current training step.

The key design choice is to keep the reward model’s large language-model backbone frozen and only update a small path on top of it. That path has two parts:

  • a sequence-to-token cross-attention module,
  • a time-step-based mix between the original reward token embedding and the feedback-aware embedding.

That keeps the update cheap. It also avoids throwing away the reward model’s general semantic knowledge just to adapt to policy drift.

The cross-attention update

The first trick is to let the reward token embedding query the policy hidden-state sequence.

The image is showing a useful asymmetry. The reward model does not ingest the whole policy sequence in a heavy, symmetric fusion block. Instead, the reward token embedding acts like a single query token that pulls out what it needs from the policy states. That keeps the update small while still letting the reward model borrow policy-specific information.

In intuition terms, the reward model is asking: “Given the way the policy is currently thinking about this response, what parts matter for the score?”

Technically, the paper defines a cross-attention update from the reward token embedding to the policy hidden-state sequence. The output is an aggregated reward token embedding that is richer than the original one because it now mixes the reward model’s semantic representation with policy feedback.

Why this matters:

  • the policy states carry distribution-shift information,
  • the reward model can use that information without retraining its full backbone,
  • the fusion is still lightweight enough to run iteratively.

This is the first half of R2M’s adaptation story.

Why the original embedding is not discarded immediately

If the model trusted policy feedback too early, it could become noisy or unstable. The policy’s hidden states are useful, but they are not perfect. They should influence the reward model gradually, not replace its original semantic representation on day one.

That is why R2M uses a time-step-based weighted combination. Early in training, the method leans more on the original reward token embedding. Later, it shifts weight toward the feedback-aware embedding from the cross-attention module.

The shape of that schedule matters more than the exact formula. It is a controlled exploration-exploitation tradeoff:

  • exploit the stable semantic embedding early,
  • explore the policy feedback more strongly as the RL loop progresses.

The effect is to avoid overreacting to fresh feedback before the reward model has enough context to use it well.

What gets optimized in the reward model step

Once the reward model can see policy feedback, it still needs a training signal. R2M uses two signals together.

The first is the familiar Bradley-Terry preference loss. For each group of sampled responses, the method selects a winner and a loser and pushes the reward model to score the winner higher.

The second is the Group Reward Entropy term. This is the paper’s way of preventing score collapse when the responses in a group start looking too similar. That problem shows up late in RL training, when many candidates are close enough that a vanilla reward model gives nearly the same score to everything.

The combined objective is:

LGREBT=(1−α)LBT+αLGRE\mathcal{L}_{\text{GREBT}}=(1-\alpha)\mathcal{L}_{\text{BT}}+\alpha\mathcal{L}_{\text{GRE}}LGREBT​=(1−α)LBT​+αLGRE​

Here is the intuition behind each term:

  • BT keeps the reward model anchored to preference ordering.
  • GRE keeps the scores spread out enough to remain useful.
  • \alpha controls how much the method cares about preventing collapse versus preserving the usual pairwise ranking objective.

If BT is the sentence-level rule of “this is better than that,” then GRE is the group-level rule of “do not flatten the whole batch into the same score.”

That combination is the main optimization trick in R2M.

Why group entropy helps

The paper’s concern is not abstract. In later RL stages, the candidate responses within a group often become more homogeneous. Once that happens, a reward model can start assigning almost the same score to every response, which weakens the learning signal and makes the policy harder to steer.

Group Reward Entropy pushes against that collapse by making the within-group score distribution sharper. In plain language, it encourages the reward model to preserve relative distinctions among responses instead of smoothing them all together.

The useful part is that GRE does not invent arbitrary differences. It only has something to amplify when the group already contains meaningful variation. That is why it fits this setting better than a blunt diversity penalty.

The training loop is deliberately small

One of the paper’s main engineering choices is to freeze the reward-model LLM backbone and update only the cross-attention module plus the scoring head. That keeps the method light enough to run iteratively.

This matters because the whole point of R2M is continuous adaptation. If the update were expensive, the method would lose the practical advantage it is trying to create. The paper is explicit that a full reward-model retraining phase is too costly, so it replaces that with a narrow update path that still captures the moving policy distribution.

That design gives R2M a useful shape:

  • the backbone keeps its general language understanding,
  • the cross-attention path absorbs live policy feedback,
  • the head turns the fused representation into a scalar reward,
  • the next policy update uses that fresher reward signal.

That is why the method feels like a repair loop rather than a new reward model family.

What the paper is claiming, in plain terms

The method is not just saying “more data helps.” It is saying that policy hidden states are themselves a useful alignment signal, because they reflect how the policy is changing in real time.

The broader lesson is easy to remember:

A reward model that only sees static preference labels will eventually lag behind the policy it is judging.

R2M’s response is to let the policy help supervise the judge. That sounds slightly circular, but the paper’s argument is that the hidden states contain enough useful state information to act as cheap, live feedback, especially when the reward model backbone is frozen and the update is kept small.

Where the method is strong

The paper’s strongest practical advantage is cost. It avoids full reward-model retraining, but still improves reward quality. That makes it a good fit for iterative RLHF setups where you want the scorer to keep up with a moving policy without paying the price of rebuilding the whole judge every round.

It also has a nice systems property: the method can slot into REINFORCE-based RLHF variants without changing the policy optimizer itself. That means the benefit is mainly on the reward side, not from redesigning the entire RL stack.

What can go wrong

R2M depends on fresh, usable policy hidden states. If those states are stale, noisy, or hard to communicate, the feedback stream stops helping and can start behaving like noise.

The paper also uses a specialized iterative training loop, so this is not a one-shot reward scorer. The method only really makes sense when you are already doing repeated policy updates.

And there is a deeper assumption underneath the whole approach: the policy feedback and the reward space must be aligned enough that hidden-state fusion is meaningful. If they are badly mismatched, the extra signal may not repair the scorer.

So the method is lightweight, but not free. It trades a small fusion-and-update loop for better reward freshness.

What to remember

The simplest way to remember R2M is this:

the policy is always changing, so the reward model should look at the policy’s own hidden states before scoring the next round.

Everything else in the paper supports that idea:

  • cross-attention lets the reward model absorb policy feedback,
  • time-based weighting keeps the fusion stable,
  • GREBT keeps the score distribution from collapsing,
  • freezing the backbone keeps the update cheap enough to repeat.

If you remember only one sentence, remember this one:

R2M is a lightweight way to keep a reward model synchronized with a drifting policy by feeding the policy’s evolving internal states back into the scorer.
