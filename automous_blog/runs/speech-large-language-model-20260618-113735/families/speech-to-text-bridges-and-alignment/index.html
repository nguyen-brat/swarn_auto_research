Bridging Speech Encoders to Text LLMs

Speech is not naturally shaped like text. A speech encoder produces long, dense, time-aligned features; a text LLM expects a shorter sequence of token-sized embeddings and then predicts words one step at a time. This subtopic is about the interface between those two worlds: how to compress speech, project it into the LLM’s space, and keep enough information that the decoder can still produce the right transcript.

The common theme is not a single algorithm. It is a shared interface problem. Some methods focus on alignment between a frozen speech model and a frozen LLM. Others focus on reducing sequence length, fusing multiple encoders, making the decoder streamable, or teaching the model to follow text-side context such as keywords and instructions. The design question is always the same: what should survive the bridge from audio frames to language tokens?

The Recurring Bridge

That pipeline hides several hard choices.

  • The speech side may be supervised, self-supervised, or lightly adapted before integration.
  • The sequence often has to be shortened before the LLM can afford to read it.
  • The bridge itself may be as small as a linear layer or as structured as a query bottleneck, adapter, or fusion block.
  • The decoder may behave like a standard text LLM, or it may need extra machinery to stay aligned with streaming audio.

Some systems keep the bridge minimal. SLAM-ASR freezes both large backbones and trains only a linear projector after frame concatenation. Speech-LLaMA compresses speech with a CTC-based front end before mapping it into LLaMA-sized embeddings. Q-Former Connector uses a fixed query bottleneck to squeeze long audio into a small set of connector tokens. The underlying lesson is simple: the bridge is not just plumbing. It decides what temporal detail is discarded and what linguistic structure reaches the language model intact.

Where the Interface Fails

The bridge fails when it preserves the wrong things.

For speech recognition or translation, the model must keep phonetic content, token order, and language cues. It can often discard raw waveform detail, speaker style, or very fine timing if the task is plain text output. But if compression is too aggressive, the LLM receives a short sequence that is easy to decode and hard to understand. If compression is too weak, the LLM sees a sequence that is faithful to the audio but too long, redundant, and expensive to process.

This is why the bottlenecks in this family look different on the surface but point to the same interface tension:

  • Speech-LLM alignment: can a text decoder interpret speech features at all?
  • Encoder mismatch and redundancy: if two encoders disagree or overlap, what should be fused and what should be dropped?
  • Label-synchronous decoding: how does the decoder stay tied to acoustic timing instead of drifting like a pure text generator?
  • Encoder-LLM alignment cost: where should compute be spent, before integration or during joint adaptation?

Compression Before Fluency

Speech is longer than text, so most bridge designs start by shortening the sequence. That can mean frame stacking, blank removal, averaging, fixed query tokens, or explicit intermediate units.

Speech-LLaMA is a clean illustration. Its main point is not that a decoder-only LLM suddenly understands audio on its own. The point is that a pretrained compressor can shorten the speech stream first, and only then should a small bridge translate those compressed acoustics into the LLM’s embedding space. That is why its recipe is best understood as “compress first, bridge second, adapt lightly.”

Other methods probe the same axis from different angles. Audio-Prefix ASR studies how coarse the audio prefix can become before recognition starts to fail. PF-S2T inserts phonemes as an intermediate target, effectively turning the bridge into a more linguistically structured channel. Mel-LLM tests the opposite instinct: instead of a heavy speech encoder, can the backbone learn alignment from normalized log-Mel features plus projection alone? These are different engineering choices, but they all answer the same question about how much structure the bridge should impose before the LLM starts decoding.

Alignment Can Be Simpler Than It Looks

One of the strongest messages in this area is that alignment is often the bottleneck, not architectural novelty. SLAM-ASR makes that point sharply: a strong speech encoder, a frozen chat LLM, short prompting, and a single linear projector are enough to produce strong ASR behavior. The result is conceptually important because it suggests that once the speech representation is good enough, the bridge may not need to be elaborate.

The evidence in the compact input supports that reading. SLAM-ASR with HuBERT X-Large plus Vicuna-7B reaches 1.94% WER on test-clean and 3.81% on test-other. Against a prior segment-QFormer connector, it reports 17.4% and 26.9% relative WER reductions on the same split. The prompt also matters: short prompts beat long prompts, while random prompts degrade performance. Together those results suggest that the decoder wants a concise, well-aligned conditioning interface, not extra prompt noise.

This also clarifies why some methods invest in data reuse or staged alignment rather than larger bridges. BLSP reuses transcript-side supervision to bootstrap the speech side. SALM couples a speech adapter with instruction-style training so the LLM learns how speech should appear at its input boundary. TextPro-SLM pushes on the same idea from another direction by reducing the modality gap and teaching the model to preserve prosodic information that plain transcripts would lose.

More Encoders Are Only Better If They Disagree Usefully

Using multiple speech encoders sounds automatically helpful, but redundant features can crowd the bridge just as easily as they can enrich it. The useful question is not “how many encoders?” but “what complementary cues do they provide, and how do we merge them without inflating the LLM context?”

Parallel Speech-Encoder Fusion is a good example because it keeps the fused output length fixed. That choice matters: if fusion improves acoustics but doubles the sequence length, the LLM still loses. The design space then shifts to whether fusion should be static or dynamic, frame-wise or sequence-level, and whether extra text-side hypotheses should be fed back in offline settings.

The compact evidence shows why simple concatenation is not enough. In Dutch monolingual ASR, temporal Transformer fusion reaches 6.8/8.3 WER, better than both Whisper alone and plain concatenation. The conceptual takeaway is broader than that single result: multiple encoders help when the fusion layer learns which view to trust, while preserving frame alignment and a fixed budget for the LLM.

Related methods probe nearby variants of the same axis. Zipper-LoRA asks how adaptation capacity should be shared across languages. TextPro-SLM keeps speech input closer to text-side behavior during adaptation. These are not the same mechanism, but they live on the same design frontier: reducing mismatch without letting the bridge become bloated or brittle.

Streaming Needs a Clock

A pure decoder-style LLM is good at wording and weak at timing. It can generate plausible transcripts, but it does not naturally know when enough audio has arrived to emit the next token. That is the label-synchronous decoding problem.

TRADE addresses it by separating timing from wording. The transducer branch acts like the clock: it decides when a token can be emitted from the current audio evidence. The LLM branch handles the richer surface form: spelling, punctuation, and casing. This is a different kind of bridge problem from simple projection, but it sits at the same interface between acoustic time and text generation.

That design matters because streaming ASR is not just offline ASR with smaller chunks. It needs bounded memory, incremental emissions, and a principled end-of-utterance signal. TRADE’s compact results show the payoff: the same checkpoint supports offline, streaming, and long-form decoding, with 6.71% average WER on the Open ASR Leaderboard and 8.40% at 960 ms streaming. The deeper lesson is that an LLM alone is not a timing model. If the task must stay synchronized with incoming audio, the bridge needs an explicit controller.

Promptability Changes the Job of the Bridge

Once speech is mapped into a prompt-like interface, the decoder can be steered by text context. That is useful, but it also raises a harder requirement: the bridge must preserve enough speech evidence that textual hints help rather than hallucinate.

SALM is the clearest example here. Its contribution is not just a speech adapter plus LoRA. The important idea is speech-supervised in-context training, which teaches the model how keyword prompts should interact with the incoming audio. Without that training, prompt-following stays weak; with it, keyword boosting becomes much more reliable. This turns prompting from a text-only convenience into part of the speech interface itself.

The same theme shows up in lighter forms elsewhere. Audio-Prefix ASR and Speech-LLaMA both treat audio as a prefix that the decoder should continue from. BLSP reuses transcript-side behavior so speech can inherit text-side capabilities more directly. These methods differ in training strategy, but they converge on one idea: if the speech bridge looks enough like a prompt, then instruction tuning and context biasing become part of the ASR design space.

When the Bridge Also Has to Speak Back

Not every bridge ends in text alone. Spectron shows what changes when the model must also reconstruct or continue speech. In that setting, the bridge has to preserve more than lexical content. It must carry speaker identity, prosody, and enough temporal structure that the decoder can produce future spectrogram frames after generating text.

That is why Spectron trains recognition and reconstruction together instead of treating ASR as the only target.

Purpose: combine transcript prediction with acoustic continuation so one decoder learns both language and speech generation.

Intuition: if the bridge only teaches the LLM to emit text, the model may ignore acoustic information that matters for continuing speech. Adding a reconstruction term forces the prefix to preserve information useful for both what was said and how the speech should continue.

Why it matters: the bridge is no longer only a speech-to-text adapter. It becomes a compact representation that a text LLM can read while still retaining information needed to continue speaking.

Spend Compute Before the Bridge or After It?

Another practical axis is where to pay the adaptation cost. Encoder First Integration argues that the front end should become strong before expensive multimodal adaptation begins. That recipe treats encoder quality as a prerequisite, not as something the LLM should fix later through brute-force joint training.

This is a useful correction to a common intuition. Large LLM-based ASR systems can make it tempting to spend compute on the language side because that is where most parameters live. But if the encoder is weak, the downstream stack burns FLOPs compensating for bad acoustic features. EFIN’s staged recipe pushes in the opposite direction: pretrain or fine-tune the encoder first, stop projection alignment at preliminary convergence, then use efficient LLM-side updates. In the compact input, that strategy cuts FLOPs substantially while improving CER, which supports the broader design lesson that not all bridge problems should be solved inside the bridge.

What To Remember

Speech-to-text bridges are where speech LLM systems either become usable or quietly fail. The main challenge is not choosing a fashionable connector block. It is deciding what information crosses from a long acoustic sequence into a text-style decoding interface, under tight budgets for length, compute, and timing.

If the task is offline ASR on clean speech, a strong encoder plus a very small projector may be enough. If the task must stream, the bridge needs an explicit timing controller. If several encoders offer complementary views, fusion must stay budget-aware. If prompting or keyword biasing matters, the bridge must be trained as a prompt interface rather than a passive feature pipe. And if the model must generate speech as well as text, the bridge has to preserve richer acoustic structure than a plain transcript requires.
