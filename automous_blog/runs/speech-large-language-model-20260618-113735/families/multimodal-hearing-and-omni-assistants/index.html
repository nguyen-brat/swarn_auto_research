How Multimodal Assistants Learn to Hear, Remember, and Speak

Making a language model “hear” is not just a matter of attaching an audio encoder. A useful assistant has to decide what in the signal matters, compress it into something the language model can handle, preserve the user’s instruction, and sometimes speak back without losing meaning or style. That is why this area is best understood as a shared interface problem rather than a single shared algorithm.

Across these systems, the recurring goal is to turn raw multimodal input into a form that a large language model can reason over without collapsing into brittle shortcuts. Some models focus on hearing many kinds of audio, some on following mixed speech instructions, some on keeping token counts manageable, and some on generating expressive speech. The common theme is not one mechanism. It is the difficulty of building a stable bridge between audio-heavy perception and language-heavy reasoning.

The Common Pipeline

Most of these assistants follow the same coarse recipe:

The names change, but the jobs stay similar.

  • The audio front end turns waveform or audiovisual streams into learned features.
  • The bridge module compresses or projects those features into token-like inputs.
  • LLM adaptation teaches a mostly frozen language model how to use those inputs.
  • Instruction tuning teaches task behavior.
  • A final stabilization step may be needed when instruction tuning damages generality.

This is why dual encoders show up often. One encoder may be better at speech content, while another captures non-speech sound or acoustic detail. SALMONN uses Whisper plus BEATs for exactly that reason, and WavLLM similarly separates semantic and acoustic cues. The lesson is broader than either paper: once audio is routed into an LLM, the front end has to decide what the language model should see and what it can safely ignore.

Why Hearing Is Harder Than It Looks

Text arrives already segmented into tokens with explicit order. Audio does not. It is long, redundant, noisy, and mixes several kinds of information at once: lexical content, timing, speaker identity, emotion, background events, and sometimes visual context from lip motion or images.

That creates four recurring failure modes in the embedded examples:

  • The model overfits to easy audio tasks and starts acting like a transcription engine.
  • The same adaptation setting fails across very different prompts and task mixtures.
  • Raw speech or audiovisual streams create more tokens than the decoder can comfortably use.
  • Semantic content and voice style get tangled, making alignment and generation harder.

These are not cosmetic issues. They determine whether the model can answer a question about sound, execute a compound spoken instruction, transcribe efficiently, or reply in a controllable voice.

When Instruction Tuning Collapses Into Transcription

The first bottleneck is task overfitting on audio instructions. A model can learn to do well on transcription, captioning, or other trained tasks, yet still ignore open-ended prompts and answer with short transcript-like text. SALMONN is a clean example because it builds a strong hearing stack first, then shows that the stack alone is not enough.

Its recipe is conceptually simple: encode speech and non-speech audio, compress them with a window-level Q-Former, and adapt a frozen Vicuna with LoRA. The interesting part is what happens next. After instruction tuning, emergent behaviors such as question answering and storytelling can fade, even though the model remains good at trained tasks. SALMONN addresses this with activation tuning on self-generated story responses, effectively adding a small corrective stage after instruction tuning.

The evidence here is useful but should stay narrow. SALMONN is reported as competitive on trained ASR, translation, captioning, emotion, speaker, and music tasks, yet without activation tuning, SQQA, Story, and SAC largely fail to follow instructions. With activation tuning, Story reaches 82.57 and SAC 0.50 while ASR remains at 2.1 WER. The durable lesson is that hearing-capable assistants need regularization against “easy-task gravity.”

When One Prompt Template Cannot Cover Everything

The second bottleneck is multi-task prompt brittleness. Once a speech model is expected to follow many instruction types, one fixed adaptation pattern may work for one prompt style and fail for another. WavLLM teaches this through separation and control rather than through a new front end alone.

Its main design choice is to split what the audio means from how it sounds. Whisper handles semantic content, while WavLM captures acoustic and speaker cues. That already reduces interference. Then the training schedule goes from simpler single-task mixtures to harder multi-task instruction following. Finally, a prompt-conditioned LoRA adapter changes how strongly the model adapts for a given instruction.

This is a useful design axis: not just what representation you feed the LLM, but how much adaptation the current prompt should trigger. In a speech assistant, “translate this,” “identify the speaker,” and “answer the question and then summarize the emotion” are not small wording variations. They are different control demands on the same backbone.

WavLLM’s anchor result is best read as evidence for this control story, not as a benchmark race. The compact input reports 62.44% accuracy and 92.50% instruction-following rate on its II-tasks after advanced training, and the prompt adapter further improves those multi-task outcomes. The concept to keep is that multi-task hearing assistants often need prompt-aware adaptation, not only better encoders.

The Speech Token Bottleneck

Large language models are comfortable with tokens, not with long frame sequences. That makes compression one of the central engineering decisions in this area. Llama-AVSR makes the point clearly: audio and video features are extracted with frozen encoders, downsampled, projected into the LLM embedding space, then concatenated with a prompt so the decoder can transcribe autoregressively.

This turns speech recognition into an interface design problem. Compress too aggressively and you erase phonetic or lip-motion detail. Compress too little and the decoder is flooded with tokens, raising cost and reducing reliability. The paper’s tutorial value is that it treats ASR, VSR, and AVSR with the same token-interface perspective rather than with separate end-to-end systems.

That perspective generalizes beyond transcription. Any hearing assistant that listens over long stretches of time faces the same question: what temporal detail must survive the bridge? Llama-AVSR shows one answer, with mostly frozen backbones and lightweight projectors plus LoRA. The input reports 0.77% AVSR WER on LRS3+VoxCeleb2 with 57M trainable parameters, which is most interesting as evidence that careful compression and projection can carry a lot of information without retraining the whole stack.

Separating Meaning From Voice Style

Some assistants only need to understand audio. Others must also speak back, and then a new bottleneck appears: semantic-style entanglement. If the same latent stream carries both the words and the expressive properties of speech, alignment becomes harder and controllable generation becomes messy. EMOVA is useful here because it makes the split explicit.

The compact input says joint alignment beats variants that keep modalities separate or leave speech units entangled. That makes the broader point well: if a model must hear, reason, and answer in expressive speech, disentanglement is often more important than adding one more modality encoder.

Adding Speech Without Breaking the Rest of the Model

Once an assistant already has vision-language ability, adding speech creates a different problem: modality conflict. Training everything at once can cause one modality to degrade another. VITA-1.5 teaches a staged answer: teach sight first, then hearing, then speaking.

Its sequence matters. First align vision and language. Then add audio input with transcription pairs and speech QA. Then add codec-based speech output while freezing the LLM so earlier multimodal skills stay stable. This is not merely a training convenience. It is a way to keep each new capability from overwriting the shared representation before it settles.

That staged strategy is a design axis worth remembering whenever a multimodal assistant must both understand and generate speech. The point is not that all systems need the same three stages. The point is that new modalities often need controlled entry points and different trainable scopes at different times.

Memory, Latency, and Full Conversation

A final challenge appears when the system stops being a single-turn recognizer and becomes a real assistant. It now has to remember earlier visual and audio context, keep turn order straight, and speak back with low latency. InteractiveOmni is the clearest example in the input because it treats early multimodal alignment, post-training for conversation, and streaming speech generation as one connected problem.

The key idea is that cross-modal memory should be trained as part of the assistant, not bolted on after the speech interface works. If history is absent from training, a model may answer the current turn well while losing earlier references, style cues, or contextual commitments. InteractiveOmni therefore uses pre-training for shared alignment, then instruction tuning, speech conversation data, preference optimization, and multi-turn memory data before streaming responses through a speech decoder.

The compact input gives broad benchmark evidence here, but the main conceptual takeaway is simpler: for omni assistants, latency and memory are not downstream product details. They feed back into representation design, data curation, and the choice to keep one shared backbone instead of isolated per-modality subsystems.

Design Axes That Matter Most

Across the examples, a few design axes recur.

What the front end preserves

Some models use dual encoders because speech meaning and acoustic cues are different signals. That shows up in SALMONN and WavLLM. Others expand to audio-video fusion, as in Llama-AVSR, where visual lip information can rescue difficult speech cases. The core choice is what information must survive compression: semantics, temporal order, speaker cues, non-speech sound, or visual grounding.

How the bridge compresses time

Window-level Q-Former summarization, modality-specific downsampling, and projector-based tokenization all serve the same purpose: convert long sequences into something the LLM can process. The right compression rate depends on the task. Transcription tolerates some reduction. Visual speech cues often tolerate less.

How much of the LLM to change

Most examples keep the backbone largely frozen and rely on LoRA, adapters, or projectors. That choice is partly about efficiency, but it is also about preserving general language behavior while teaching a new interface.

How training is staged

The embedded methods repeatedly favor curricula over one-shot joint tuning: pretrain before instruction tune, simple tasks before complex tasks, vision before audio, hearing before speaking. The reason is stability. Multimodal assistants fail less often when each capability is introduced in a controlled order.

Whether generation is text-only or speech-native

Some systems stop at text answers, which simplifies alignment. Others, especially EMOVA, VITA-1.5, and InteractiveOmni, push toward spoken replies. That introduces style control, codec design, latency constraints, and memory demands that text-only hearing systems can postpone.

What the Evidence Actually Supports

The compact input provides limited direct evidence, but it supports a few stable claims.

  • SALMONN shows that activation tuning can recover instruction-following behavior without giving up strong ASR, which supports the claim that task overfitting is real and correctable.
  • WavLLM shows that dual encoders plus staged multi-task training improve instruction robustness, which supports prompt-aware adaptation as a real design axis.
  • Llama-AVSR shows that careful compression and lightweight adaptation can make frozen-backbone transcription surprisingly strong.
  • EMOVA shows that separating semantics from style is not cosmetic; it supports controllable speech and cross-modal alignment.
  • VITA-1.5 and InteractiveOmni show that stage ordering and conversation-aware post-training matter once the target is a full assistant rather than a recognizer.

What to Remember

Multimodal hearing assistants are not defined by one shared algorithm. They are defined by a recurring systems problem: how to turn long, mixed, instruction-bearing perceptual streams into a stable interface for language reasoning and, sometimes, speech generation.

If you remember one thing, make it this: the hard part is not only hearing better. It is deciding what to preserve, what to compress, when to adapt, and when to keep modalities from interfering with each other. Good systems differ in mechanism, but they converge on the same practical insight: treat hearing, prompting, compression, alignment, memory, and speaking as coupled interface decisions.
