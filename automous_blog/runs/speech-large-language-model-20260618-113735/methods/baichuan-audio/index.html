Baichuan-Audio

Baichuan-Audio is trying to keep speech inside the model instead of collapsing it into text too early. That sounds like a small change in architecture, but it is really the whole point of the paper. The system starts from a pretrained language model, feeds it compact 12.5 Hz speech codes, lets it alternate between text tokens and audio tokens, and then turns those audio tokens back into waveforms with a flow-matching decoder. The design goal is simple to say and hard to do well: preserve meaning, preserve speech cues, and preserve the language model’s reasoning ability at the same time.

That puts it squarely in the native speech-text language models family. The paper is not just attaching speech to a text model as an afterthought. It is asking what happens if speech and text are both first-class parts of the generation loop.

Figure 1 is the easiest way to see the whole system at once.

What to notice is the rhythm of the pipeline. Speech does not go straight into a transcript and then into a separate TTS system. Instead, the model keeps one foot in language and one foot in audio. It reads compressed speech, predicts text when that helps structure the answer, predicts audio tokens when it needs to speak, and then lets a decoder turn those tokens back into sound.

The interesting part is not that each piece exists. It is how the pieces are tuned so that none of them destroys the others’ job.

The tokenizer is the gatekeeper

Everything starts with representation. If the speech tokenizer throws away too much meaning, the LLM cannot recover it later. If it keeps every acoustic detail, the sequence becomes long and awkward again. Baichuan-Audio attacks that tradeoff with a multi-codebook tokenizer built on a pretrained ASR-style encoder.

The encoder first turns speech into high-level features, then a small convolutional stack downsamples the sequence to 12.5 Hz. That rate matters. It is low enough that the LLM can read the sequence without drowning in frames, but still high enough to keep the speech signal usable. Then residual vector quantization compresses those features into multiple codebooks instead of one. The paper uses eight layers, with smaller codebooks in later layers, so the representation can carry coarse semantics first and finer acoustic detail later.

Figure 2 makes that structure easier to visualize.

What the figure is really showing is a compromise. The tokenizer is trying to be semantic enough for the LLM and acoustic enough for speech reconstruction. That is a difficult balance, and the multi-codebook design is the paper’s answer to it.

The intuition is easier to remember if you picture the codebooks as a stack of shorthand notebooks. The top layer writes down the broad meaning of the utterance. The lower layers add details about the exact sound. One layer alone is too brittle. Too many layers, or the wrong training, and the representation becomes messy and redundant. The paper’s ablation makes the point very clearly: an 8-layer VQ tokenizer is far better than a 1-layer version, which loses a lot of speech content.

The loss is balancing three jobs at once

The tokenizer is trained with a compact objective that mixes reconstruction, language alignment, and quantization stability.

Purpose: keep the speech representation usable for both the LLM and the audio decoder.

Intuition: the tokenizer should not only reconstruct speech well. It should also stay aligned with the text model’s space and remain stable as a discrete code system.

Formula:

Losstotal=λ1⋅Lossreconstruct′+λ2⋅Lossllm+λ3⋅Losscommit\text{Loss}_{total}=\lambda_{1}\cdot\text{Loss}_{reconstruct}^{\prime}+\lambda_{2}\cdot\text{Loss}_{llm}+\lambda_{3}\cdot\text{Loss}_{commit}Losstotal​=λ1​⋅Lossreconstruct′​+λ2​⋅Lossllm​+λ3​⋅Losscommit​

Symbols: \lambda_{1} weights the reconstruction term, \lambda_{2} weights the LLM alignment term, and \lambda_{3} weights the VQ commitment term. \text{Loss}_{reconstruct}^{\prime} is the multi-scale reconstruction loss for the Mel outputs. \text{Loss}_{llm} is the text-audio alignment loss used to keep the tokenizer compatible with the language model. \text{Loss}_{commit} is the VQ commitment loss that keeps encoder outputs close to the chosen codebook entries.

Read-aloud meaning: the model is trained to reconstruct speech, stay aligned with the LLM, and keep the discrete codebook from drifting apart.

Why it matters: this is the paper’s core representation trick. It is not enough to discretize speech. The discretization has to stay useful to both reasoning and generation.

The paper adds a few training tricks to keep the codebooks healthy. Layerwise dropout randomly drops outputs from the later VQ layers during training so the top layers are forced to carry the important semantic content. A restart strategy reuses dead codes instead of letting unused clusters sit there forever. Gumbel sampling adds exploration so the tokenizer does not collapse too early. An L2 norm constraint keeps the codebook vectors in a bounded range, which helps downstream prediction, but the paper also notes that pushing this too hard can make the codebook too repetitive. The lesson is a familiar one: a good discrete representation needs both pressure and slack.

One more detail is worth remembering. The authors do not replace individual tokens with quantized outputs in a scattered way. They found it works better to replace whole samples progressively, moving from a small fraction of VQ output to full replacement over training. That is a nice example of how representation learning can be sensitive to the granularity of corruption. Whole examples are a cleaner curriculum than token-level noise.

How the middle of the model stays bilingual

Once speech has been tokenized, the model has to decide what to do with those tokens. Baichuan-Audio turns them into audio embeddings, then feeds those embeddings into the LLM alongside text. The language model alternates between generating text tokens and audio tokens, and a special marker tells it when to switch modes.

That switching behavior is the heart of the method. It means the model can ground itself in text before it speaks. Text acts like a scaffold for the answer, while audio tokens carry the spoken form of that answer. This is very different from a pure cascade, where ASR finishes the job and TTS starts over from scratch. Here the answer is being formed inside one generation loop.

The paper also gives the audio side its own head instead of forcing everything through the text output head. That matters because audio tokens are not just more text. They have their own local structure, their own codebooks, and their own prediction patterns. A separate audio head is the model’s way of saying that speech deserves a specialist decoder, even if the backbone is still a language model.

Figure 4 shows the training data pipeline that teaches this alternating behavior.

The key idea is to mix text and audio during pretraining rather than treating speech and text as two disconnected worlds. The paper uses two kinds of interleaved data. INTLV data alternates chunks of audio and text in longer mixed sequences, while ITTS data alternates text and speech in a way that directly supports text-guided speech generation. The sequences are segmented using punctuation or natural pauses, so the model sees speech at the same places a human would naturally break a sentence.

There is a subtle training choice here that matters a lot. The authors do not compute loss on the audio segments inside INTLV sequences during pretraining. At their scale, supervising those audio spans actually hurt performance. That makes sense once you think about the job of INTLV: it is there to teach cross-modal context and switching, not to force the model to continue text into audio in every mixed sequence. For ITTS, by contrast, they do supervise both the text and the audio parts, except for the opening text segment, because that task is directly about text-guided speech generation.

So the interleaved data is doing two things at once:

  • teaching the model when to move between text and speech,
  • teaching the model how to keep the answer coherent while it switches.

That is a better fit for a speech LLM than a simple ASR-plus-TTS pipeline, because the model is learning the transition itself rather than just the endpoints.

Turning audio tokens back into speech

The output side of the system is a flow-matching decoder trained to turn compact audio representations back into a Mel spectrogram and then a waveform. The reason this matters is straightforward: the tokenizer saves the model from reading raw audio, but it also makes the speech look abstract. The decoder has to bring back the smoothness and naturalness that the discrete representation removed.

Figure 3 shows that last stage.

The decoder is built as a flow-matching model on 24 kHz audio. In plain language, it learns a smooth generative path from intermediate representations to the target Mel spectrogram. A pre-net prepares the latent features for the vocoder, and a conditional U-Net-like decoder does the heavy lifting. The final Mel output is then passed to HiFi-GAN to produce waveform audio.

What matters conceptually is that the decoder is not trying to invent speech from scratch. The tokenizer and the reconstruction loss already preserve a lot of the acoustic structure, including speaker color and prosodic cues. That lets the decoder focus on recovering fidelity and smoothness rather than relearning every aspect of the voice from scratch.

The paper’s reconstruction result is a good sanity check on that design. Compared with the raw VQ output, flow matching pushes subjective quality much closer to the ground truth and slightly improves content preservation as measured by ASR. That is exactly what you want from this stage: cleaner speech without breaking the words.

Two stages so the language model does not forget how to reason

The biggest risk in a speech LLM is that audio adaptation will overwrite the thing the text model already knows. Speech is different enough from text that a naive full fine-tune can pull the backbone away from its original reasoning ability. Baichuan-Audio deals with that by splitting pretraining into two stages.

In stage 1, the language model is frozen. Only the audio embedding layer and the audio head are trained. That keeps the text side stable while the speech interface learns the basics. In stage 2, the model opens up much more of the network, but the text embedding layer and the LM head stay frozen. The idea is to let the audio pathway specialize without letting the text entry and exit points drift.

This is the paper’s answer to a very common failure mode in multimodal training: if you move everything at once, the model gets fluent in the new modality and forgets how to think in the old one.

The evidence is clear enough to be useful. Two-stage training improves story continuation and commonsense reasoning compared with single-stage training. The gains are not huge, but they matter because they show that the schedule itself preserves language ability better. The remaining gap to a text-only baseline is also important. Baichuan-Audio is not pretending that speech adaptation is free. It is trying to recover as much reasoning as possible while learning audio.

The same caution shows up in supervised fine-tuning. The paper builds about 242k audio instruction pairs from filtered text instructions, then synthesizes speech using many different voice tones. Responses are broken at natural conversational pauses before being turned into audio, which keeps the audio reply from sounding like a literal text dump. The authors also run ASR on the generated audio to check quality. Bad syntheses are recycled into the TTS data, and ASR errors are reused for ASR training. That is a practical way to turn failures into better training signal instead of just discarding them.

There is also an important realism check here: not every text response is a good audio response. Tone, speed, and spoken expression can make a sentence work on the page but sound awkward out loud. The paper explicitly acknowledges that and treats the text-to-audio conversion step as something that needs human judgment, not just blind synthesis.

What the paper is really teaching

Baichuan-Audio is not trying to win by one clever module. It works because the design splits the problem into three clean jobs.

  • The tokenizer compresses speech into a code stream that still carries meaning and acoustic detail.
  • The LLM predicts text and audio in one alternating loop, so the answer stays grounded while it is being spoken.
  • The decoder turns the discrete audio representation back into natural speech.

The two-stage schedule is what keeps those pieces from fighting each other. First protect the language model. Then teach it speech. The interleaved data is what teaches the switching behavior. The independent audio head is what keeps audio prediction from being forced through a text-only bottleneck. And the flow-matching decoder is what gives the final output enough naturalness to sound like speech rather than a compressed artifact.

The main limitation is that this stack is more complex than a simple cascade. It also depends on a lot of carefully curated audio, and some reasoning metrics still trail the text-only baseline. So the paper is not claiming that speech has fully replaced text as the best medium for reasoning. It is showing that a pretrained LLM can be adapted for real speech interaction without giving up too much of its original capability.
