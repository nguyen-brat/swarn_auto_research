EMOVA: Emotionally Omni-present Voice Assistant

EMOVA is trying to solve a very practical gap in open multimodal systems. Vision-language models can already read images and reason over them. Speech-language models can hear and answer in audio. But getting one open model to do both, while also controlling the emotion and pitch of the spoken reply, is still hard.

The paper’s answer is not to pour every modality into one undifferentiated stream. It splits speech into meaning and style, uses text as the shared meeting place for image and speech alignment, and only puts style back at the end when the model synthesizes the voice. That is the core trick to keep in mind.

In the broader multimodal hearing and omni assistants family, EMOVA is the clearest example of a model that wants to see, hear, and speak without giving up either visual detail or expressive speech control.

The whole system, left to right

The easiest way to understand EMOVA is to read the architecture as a data flow, not as a pile of modules.

What to notice in the figure is the division of labor. Images go through a continuous vision encoder and then a projector that lands them in the LLM’s token space. Speech goes through a speech-to-unit tokenizer that turns the waveform into discrete speech units. The LLM then consumes text tokens, visual tokens, and speech units in one shared sequence. If the output should be spoken, a unit-to-speech detokenizer turns the generated speech units back into waveform audio, with a separate style handle for emotion and pitch.

Why the speech branch is split in two

Speech is the hardest input to handle in this setting because it carries two very different things at once: what was said and how it sounded. If those two are entangled, the alignment problem becomes messy. The model has to solve semantics, timing, emotion, and voice identity all at the same time.

EMOVA’s speech tokenizer tries to avoid that by separating the semantic content from the acoustic style.

Intuition: keep the words in one stream and the voice color in another, so text can align with speech content without being confused by emotion or pitch.

Read-aloud meaning: the speech encoder turns the waveform into one latent representation, then splits that representation into what was said and how it was said.

Why it matters: only the semantic part is discretized into speech units for alignment. The style part stays available for synthesis later, which makes controllable speech much easier to supervise.

This is the paper’s most important representation choice. The speech units are treated like compact, text-like IDs, while the style path is kept separate so the model can recover expressive voice without polluting the alignment space. That is also why the paper can share a speech codebook across languages such as English and Chinese: the alignment target is content, not the exact acoustic surface.

Why joint alignment beats staging everything one after another

Once speech is represented this way, the next question is how to align it with text and vision. EMOVA compares several options: vision-language only, speech only, vision then speech, speech then vision, joint alignment, and joint alignment with entangled speech units.

The chart is really about one thing: whether the model should learn the modalities together or in sequence. EMOVA’s answer is that joint alignment is better.

Three observations matter here:

  • Joint training beats the vision-only and speech-only baselines.
  • Joint training beats both sequential orders, vision-then-speech and speech-then-vision.
  • Joint training with disentangled speech units beats joint training with entangled units.

The intuition is straightforward once you think about the shared text bridge. Image-text and speech-text datasets both point into the same semantic space, so the model does not need scarce image-text-speech triples to learn the connection. It can learn from public image-text data and public speech-text data at the same time, with text acting as the common meeting ground.

Sequential training is weaker because one modality can overwrite the other. If the model learns vision-language first and then later adapts to speech, it may forget part of the visual alignment. If it learns speech first and then adds vision, the same thing can happen in the other direction. Joint training avoids that tug-of-war by aligning both streams together from the start.

The entangled variant shows why the speech split is so important. If the speech units still carry too much prosody, the alignment path has to carry semantics and style together, which makes the shared space much less clean.

Teaching the model to answer with style

Alignment gives EMOVA a shared multimodal backbone. Instruction tuning teaches it how to behave like an assistant.

The paper does not have a large real-world corpus of image-speech-text conversations, so it synthesizes one. It starts from existing text and visual instruction datasets, filters out non-vocal content such as code and formulas, removes awkward formatting, and then assigns style labels such as gender, pitch, and emotion. Those labeled dialogues are then turned into speech with external TTS tools, using several different speakers to widen the variety of voices.

The important detail is that style is no longer implicit. The model is trained to choose it explicitly.

Read-aloud meaning: given an image and a spoken instruction, the model learns to predict the transcription, then the text answer, then the style labels, and finally the speech units.

Why it matters: this chain turns speech style into an explicit supervised decision instead of an accidental byproduct. The JSON-style output format used for deployment is just a practical wrapper around that chain.

After the LLM emits the speech units, the U2S detokenizer rebuilds the waveform from two inputs: the semantic unit stream and the chosen style embedding. The style embedding comes from a prototype reference speech for the selected emotion, pitch, and voice condition. In effect, the model says what to say first, then chooses how to say it.

That ordering is the reason EMOVA can produce emotional replies without letting style leak back into the content alignment path. The semantic branch stays clean, and the expressive branch is applied only when the voice is synthesized.

The three-stage training recipe

The paper’s overall schedule is easy to remember because each stage opens a little more of the system.

  • Stage 1: pre-align vision-language. Only the vision projector learns, so the visual features land in the LLM’s embedding space without disturbing the rest of the model.
  • Stage 2: jointly align image-text and speech-text. The LLM, the vision projector, and part of the vision encoder are trained together on the mixed alignment data.
  • Stage 3: instruction tune the full system. The model learns the multi-step omni-modal dialogue format, including style selection and speech generation.

This staged recipe is doing two jobs at once. First, it prevents the visual side from being destabilized too early. Second, it gives the speech side a clear bridge into the LLM through text before asking the model to generate expressive voice.

What EMOVA is really saying

EMOVA’s main lesson is not “add more modalities.” The lesson is more specific: if you separate speech content from speech style, then text can become the shared alignment space for both images and speech, and style can be restored only at the end.

That is why the method works well enough to support both vision-language tasks and expressive speech. It uses public image-text and speech-text data, rather than relying on rare image-text-speech triples, and it trains the modalities together instead of letting one stage overwrite the other.

What EMOVA still leaves open

The paper is also clear about the limits of the current design.

  • It still uses text as the intermediate bridge, so speech generation is not direct unit-to-unit.
  • It does not support duplex conversation, where the model listens and speaks at the same time.
  • It does not explore multiple vision backbones or visual generation.

Those limits are not minor footnotes. They define the next step for this family of models. The current design is strong because it keeps the alignment problem manageable. The next challenge is to make the system more conversational without reintroducing the entanglement EMOVA worked so hard to avoid.

What to remember

If you want one compact memory hook for the paper, use this:

split speech into meaning and voice, align the meaning jointly with text and images, then add the voice style back only when the model speaks.

That is EMOVA in one sentence. The architecture, the training data, and the staged optimization all follow from that idea.
