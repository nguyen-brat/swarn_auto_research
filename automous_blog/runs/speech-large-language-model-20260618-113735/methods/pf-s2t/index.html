Phoneme-First Speech-to-Text for Speech-Augmented LLMs

PF-S2T is a small detour that changes how a speech-augmented LLM learns to listen. Instead of jumping straight from audio to words, the model is asked to name the phonemes it hears first, then write the transcript. That extra step is cheap, because the phoneme labels can be derived from existing transcripts, but it is surprisingly effective because it forces the decoder to anchor itself in pronunciation before it starts guessing words.

You can think of the whole method as a bridge with one extra stepping stone in the middle:

The paper’s basic claim is simple: if speech and text live in different representational worlds, give the model a smaller target that sits closer to the speech side before asking it to cross into full words. Phonemes are that target. They are much cheaper than manual semantic labels, but they still capture the sound structure that direct speech-to-text often blurs.

Why direct S2T gets stuck between acoustics and meaning

Direct speech-to-text asks one model to solve two jobs at once. It has to read acoustic detail from the speech encoder and then immediately produce a semantic text sequence in the LLM’s vocabulary. That works, but the handoff is awkward.

Speech encoders are good at preserving pronunciation cues. LLM embeddings are good at representing concepts and context. Those are not the same thing. A language model can easily prefer a word that fits the sentence semantically, even if another word is closer to what was actually spoken. That is why similar-sounding words, homophones, and homographs are such a pain point. If the model hears something like cite versus sight, or cough versus though, it has to lean on pronunciation, not just plausibility.

PF-S2T changes the task framing. The model is no longer allowed to go straight from acoustics to semantic output. It must first explain what sounds were present. That small constraint matters because it narrows the space of mistakes. Instead of asking, “What word best fits the sentence?”, the decoder first asks, “What did I actually hear?”

That is one reason the method is so practical: you do not need a whole new architecture, just a different target format.

The cheapness of the idea comes from the labels. The phoneme targets can be produced automatically from transcripts with pronunciation resources and forced alignment, so the method does not depend on a large manual phoneme annotation effort. In the English experiments, the paper uses ARPAbet-style phoneme targets; in the Dutch setup, it uses pronunciation dictionaries and forced alignment variants. The exact label source changes, but the logic stays the same: get a sound-level target that is closer to the audio than plain text is.

The bridge under the hood

The rest of the pipeline is a fairly standard speech-augmented LLM stack. The speech encoder is frozen, the encoder output is stacked into shorter chunks, and a projection layer maps those features into the LLM’s embedding space. Then the decoder-only LLM is adapted with 4-bit QLoRA so it can learn the new speech-conditioned behavior without full fine-tuning.

That design choice matters. The paper is not trying to retrain the entire speech stack from scratch. It keeps the speech encoder as the stable acoustic front end and uses the projection layer to do the messy interface work. The LLM then learns how to interpret speech-like embeddings and produce either phonemes-first output or direct transcription output.

The default setup uses a Whisper-medium encoder, but the method is not tied to one backbone. The paper also tests Whisper-large and HuBERT-large variants, which is useful because it shows the idea is about the output strategy, not about a single favored encoder. If the encoder already carries strong pronunciation information, the phoneme-first target has a better chance of pulling that information into the decoder.

Why the joint version exists

There is one subtle problem with pure PF-S2T. Once the model has generated the phoneme string, the transcript stage can become too easy. During decoder-only training, teacher forcing gives the model the gold previous tokens, so the transcript part may learn to lean heavily on the phoneme tokens rather than on the speech features themselves. The paper’s attention analysis shows exactly that pattern: the LLM looks at speech to produce the phonemes, then mostly looks at the phonemes to produce the transcript.

That is why the authors add a joint training scheme. Instead of always using the phoneme-first prompt, they randomly mix the plain S2T prompt and the PF-S2T prompt during training.

This is the right compromise. If you train only the phoneme-first path, the model can overfit to the phoneme sequence and let those errors leak into the final words. If you train only direct S2T, you lose the pronunciation scaffold. Joint training keeps both routes alive. The model still learns the intermediate phoneme representation, but it also keeps a direct path from speech to text, which becomes useful when there is enough labeled data for the direct route to work well.

Where the gains show up first

The clearest story is in the low-resource English experiments. On LibriSpeech-100h and TED-LIUM-100h, phoneme-first supervision improves recognition over plain S2T, and the joint version is strongest overall. The best numbers are not the point by themselves; the pattern is. When labeled speech is scarce, a phoneme target gives the decoder a gentler stepping stone than full words do.

That is why PF-S2T feels especially natural in small-data settings. The model does not need to discover pronunciation and lexical mapping in one leap. It can first learn the acoustic shape of the utterance, then turn that shape into words. On the low-resource benchmarks, the joint PF-S2T variant reaches 6.8% average WER on LibriSpeech-100h and 6.7% test WER on TED-LIUM-100h, which is better than the plain S2T baseline.

The intuition matches the numbers. If the model is unsure whether it heard one word or another, a phoneme target gives it a more local, sound-based objective to learn from. That local objective is often easier to optimize than a direct transcript target, especially when the training set is not huge.

The method still scales when you give it more data

The paper does not stop at the low-resource regime. It repeats the experiment on full LibriSpeech-960h to see whether phoneme-first supervision still matters when the model sees much more labeled speech.

It does. Joint training improves both Whisper-based and HuBERT-based setups, and the HuBERT-L joint model reaches 3.2% average WER. That is the important takeaway: PF-S2T is not just a small-data trick that disappears at scale. It still helps the LLM learn how pronunciations and transcripts line up.

There is, however, a subtle shift in behavior. In the larger-data regime, direct S2T decoding often becomes the better inference path because the model has seen enough examples to map speech to text more directly. So the role of PF-S2T changes a little. In small data, it is often the best route at inference time. In larger data, it is still valuable as training signal, but the direct prompt can become the cleaner decoder. That distinction is easy to miss, and it is one of the more useful lessons in the paper.

Why label quality matters

The Dutch corpus experiment isolates a different question: how much does the quality of the phoneme labels matter?

The answer is “quite a bit.” Manual phoneme labels are better than automatic ones, and the combined setup is best of all. But the more important result is that automatic phoneme labels still help. That means the method does not depend on perfect manual phoneme annotation. A decent pronunciation lexicon and forced alignment are enough to make the phoneme-first detour useful.

This section also clarifies why joint training matters so much. Pure PF-S2T can be very good at phoneme prediction and still let those phoneme errors leak into the final transcript. Joint training softens that failure mode. It lets the model benefit from the intermediate pronunciation target without becoming trapped by it.

The paper also notes that the phoneme-first approach is useful for explainability. If the model produces phonemes as part of its output, you can see which sounds it thought were present and where the pronunciation likely went wrong. That is a different kind of transparency than a plain transcript gives you.

What the alignment analysis is really measuring

The final analysis in the paper is less about word accuracy and more about acoustic faithfulness.

To check that, the authors compare the phonemes of the predicted transcript against the phonemes of the reference transcript. They use a grapheme-to-phoneme system to convert both sides into sound sequences, then compute Phoneme Error Rate, or PER. WER tells you whether the words match. PER tells you whether the predicted transcript sounds like the reference.

That distinction is the whole point of PF-S2T. The method is supposed to make errors more acoustically sensible, not just lower the final word error count. On clean speech, the paper finds that PF-S2T can reduce PER much more than WER. In plain language, the transcript may still be imperfect, but the mistakes are closer to what was actually spoken. The model is less likely to hallucinate a completely unrelated phrase and more likely to land on a nearby-sounding one.

That is why the authors argue the method gives better grounding. It does not merely improve the transcript. It improves the relationship between the transcript and the sound that produced it.

What to remember

PF-S2T is easiest to remember as a pronunciation scaffold inside the decoder. The model listens, names the sounds, then writes the sentence.

  • It is cheap because the phoneme target can be derived from transcripts and pronunciation resources.
  • It is useful because phonemes sit closer to acoustics than words do.
  • It works best when data are scarce or pronunciation is easy to confuse.
  • Joint training matters because it keeps the direct speech-to-text path alive and prevents the model from overfitting to the phoneme string alone.
  • The tradeoff is real: the output is longer, inference is slower, and dedicated ASR systems can still be a bit better when data are plentiful.

If S2T is “listen and guess the sentence,” PF-S2T is “listen, say the sounds, then write the sentence.” That extra pronunciation step is the whole method, and it is enough to make the decoder more acoustically grounded without turning the system into a much heavier architecture.
