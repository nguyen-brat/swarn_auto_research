AudioDER: Redundancy-Aware Data Construction for Reasoning-Oriented Post-Training of Large Audio-Language Models

AudioDER sits in the Evaluation, benchmarks, and post-training data family, but it is not a benchmark in the usual sense. It is a data construction pipeline. The paper’s claim is straightforward: if an audio-language model keeps seeing repeated acoustic content, raw scale stops being useful very quickly. You pay for more data, but you do not get much more reasoning diversity.

So the paper does something practical instead of flashy. It cleans up the raw audio pools first, converts different annotation styles into one shared question format, and then adds worked explanations on top. The result is a dataset built for post-training, not just for storage.

Why more audio is not always more supervision

The first thing to understand is the bottleneck the paper is attacking. Audio-language post-training sets are often assembled by collecting many public datasets and stacking them together. That sounds like a good way to scale, until you notice that a lot of the clips are acoustically similar. Different dataset names, same kind of sound. Different annotation styles, same underlying example.

That kind of overlap matters because post-training is only useful when the model sees new patterns. If two samples are nearly the same clip in different packaging, they mostly teach the same lesson twice. The paper argues that this is exactly what happens when audio corpora are aggregated without any redundancy control.

The motivation figure makes that problem visible.

What to notice is the overlap. The points from different source datasets cluster on top of each other in CLAP space, which means the datasets are not as diverse as their names suggest. CLAP is a joint audio-text embedding model, so if two clips land close together there, they are probably acoustically similar. AudioDER uses that shared space as a practical filter: if two samples are too close, one of them is probably redundant.

The cutoff is intentionally strict. The paper measures similarity with cosine similarity on CLAP embeddings and removes near-duplicates before it spends effort on annotation generation. That choice matters because it moves the cleanup step upstream. Instead of generating captions, questions, and rationales for repeated audio, the pipeline removes repetition first and saves the annotation budget for clips that actually add diversity.

The pipeline: clean first, standardize second, explain last

The whole method can be read as a three-step funnel:

Here is the pipeline at a glance.

What to notice in the figure is the order of operations. The paper does not start by generating reasoning traces. It starts by reducing duplication, then it reshapes the surviving annotations into one format, and only then does it ask a teacher model to write explanations. That order is the whole point.

Stage 1: remove redundancy before you annotate anything

The first stage works directly on the raw audio collections. AudioDER embeds each clip with CLAP, then looks for pairs that are extremely close in that representation space. A high similarity score means the audio is likely repeated or nearly repeated, even if it came from a different source dataset.

This is the cheapest place to fix the problem. If you wait until after annotation, you end up spending time and model calls on audio that does not add much new information. By filtering early, the pipeline keeps the later supervision focused on genuinely different clips.

There is also a subtle reason this matters for reasoning data. Reasoning training benefits from variety in what the model has to compare, infer, and justify. Redundant clips do the opposite: they train shortcuts. The model keeps seeing the same acoustic pattern tied to the same answer shape, so it has fewer chances to learn robust evidence-based reasoning.

The tradeoff is obvious too. If the threshold is too aggressive, you can throw away rare but useful sounds. If it is too loose, you leave behind near-duplicates that still dilute diversity. AudioDER is built around that balancing act, and the paper is clear that CLAP-based deduplication is a practical proxy, not a perfect semantic duplicate detector.

Stage 2: force different source datasets into one question style

Once the audio pool is cleaner, the paper turns the surviving annotations into a single caption-conditioned multiple-choice format.

This is a big design choice. Instead of regenerating captions and questions from scratch, AudioDER reuses what the source datasets already provide. Captions stay captions. Question-answer pairs become standardized multiple-choice questions. For AVQA, the wording is adapted from video-centric language to audio-centric language by replacing video references with audio references.

That sounds modest, but it does a lot of work. The pipeline is trying to make many heterogeneous datasets look like one coherent tutoring set. Once the annotation shapes match, the model no longer has to relearn a different prompt format for every source dataset. It can focus on the reasoning task itself.

The output of this stage is already more useful than the raw input. Every sample now has the same basic structure: an audio clip, a caption, a question, four answer candidates, and one correct answer. The diversity now comes from the audio content and the reasoning content, not from messy format variation.

Stage 3: add a worked explanation, not just a label

The final stage is where AudioDER becomes reasoning-oriented rather than just formatted.

The paper uses Qwen3-30B to generate a chain-of-thought rationale for each unified sample. The rationale is conditioned on the caption, the question, the candidate answers, and the correct choice. In other words, the teacher is not improvising from scratch. It is being asked to explain why one option fits the audio evidence better than the others.

The generation process is structured into four beats:

  • first, identify the main reasoning path,
  • then extract the relevant acoustic or semantic cues from the caption,
  • then connect those cues to the answer choices,
  • and finally summarize the conclusion in a short explanation.

That structure matters because it turns the rationale into a worked example. The model is not only shown the right answer; it is shown how a plausible explanation arrives there. For post-training, that is often more useful than a bare label, especially when the task involves subtle audio evidence rather than obvious text matching.

The final sample format is therefore:

  • audio clip
  • caption
  • multiple-choice question
  • four answer candidates
  • correct answer
  • generated CoT rationale

That is the real product of the pipeline. AudioDER is not just a pile of audio clips. It is a uniform set of teaching examples.

What the dataset actually teaches

AudioDER covers three major audio domains: sound, speech, and music. That domain mix is important because reasoning in audio is not one thing. Sometimes the model has to identify a sound event. Sometimes it has to answer a speech-related question. Sometimes it has to reason over musical content.

The paper reports roughly 191k samples in the final corpus. But the more interesting fact is not the size by itself. It is the shape of the data. AudioDER tries to preserve the useful parts of the original annotations while stripping away source-dataset formatting and duplicate acoustic content.

That gives it a very specific teaching profile:

  • use existing annotations instead of paying to recreate them,
  • standardize the task into one multiple-choice interface,
  • and add explicit reasoning traces so the model can learn how to justify the answer.

The paper’s deeper lesson is that this combination is more valuable than raw accumulation. If the corpus is more diverse and the supervision is more explicit, the model sees more useful reasoning patterns per training example.

Why the teacher-generated rationale matters

The rationale stage is the part that makes AudioDER feel different from a normal QA collection.

Without the rationale, the dataset would still be useful, but it would mostly teach answer selection. With the rationale, each example becomes a miniature explanation task. The model is encouraged to connect the audio description to the question, compare options, and defend the choice in words.

That is also where the main limitation enters. The rationales are generated by a teacher model, so they can inherit that teacher’s habits and biases. If the teacher explains something in a particular style, the student may imitate that style whether or not it is ideal. The paper is honest about that tradeoff: the extra reasoning signal is valuable, but it is still synthetic supervision.

The multiple-choice framing has a similar tradeoff. It is easy to standardize and train on, but it can underfit open-ended audio reasoning. A model trained this way may get very good at choosing among options while still being less comfortable with free-form explanations or conversational audio tasks.

Why the results are a data story, not an architecture story

The experiment section makes the paper’s point clearly. The backbone model is Qwen2-Audio-7B-Instruct, and the training recipe is simple supervised fine-tuning on AudioDER. There is no new architecture hiding the gain.

Even so, the improvement is substantial. On MMAU-mini, the backbone moves from 59.60% under direct inference to 66.70% after SFT on AudioDER. The same setup reaches 56.49% on MMSU and 50.10% on MMAR, which are the strongest reproduced open-source results reported in the paper.

That matters because it tells you where the leverage is. The gains do not come from a more complicated objective or a more elaborate model wrapper. They come from better post-training data:

  • less redundancy,
  • more consistent annotation format,
  • and explicit reasoning traces.

In other words, the method is not saying “train harder.” It is saying “train on a cleaner, better shaped corpus.”

Where the method can still fail

AudioDER is a strong data recipe, but it is not a magic fix.

  • Teacher-generated rationales may reflect Qwen3-30B’s biases.
  • The multiple-choice format may be too narrow for open-ended audio reasoning.
  • CLAP-based deduplication can miss semantic duplicates or remove rare clips that were actually worth keeping.

Those are not minor details. They are the main assumptions behind the pipeline. If the deduplication threshold is off, the corpus can become either too repetitive or too thin. If the MCQ format is too restrictive, the model may learn a narrower kind of audio reasoning than the benchmark set suggests.

So the method is best understood as a practical compromise: it buys cleaner supervision by accepting some formatting constraints and some teacher-model dependence.

What to remember

The easiest way to remember AudioDER is as a funnel that turns overlapping audio collections into one clean tutoring set.

The first gate removes clips that are too similar in CLAP space. The second gate turns different annotation styles into one four-choice question format. The last gate adds a worked explanation so the model learns not just the answer, but the reasoning path.

That is why the paper matters. It shows that for audio reasoning, the biggest gains can come from better data construction rather than from a more elaborate model. If the corpus is cleaner, more diverse, and more explicitly explained, supervised fine-tuning becomes much more effective.

If you want the shortest summary, it is this:

AudioDER does not ask the model to reason over more audio. It asks the model to reason over better audio supervision.
