InteractiveOmni

InteractiveOmni sits in the multimodal hearing and omni assistants family, but it pushes the idea farther than the usual “audio model plus language model” stack. The paper is trying to build one assistant that can see images and video, hear audio, keep track of earlier turns, and speak back in a streaming voice. That sounds like several systems glued together, but the core message is the opposite: the model becomes more useful when those abilities are trained as one connected loop instead of as separate modules.

The practical problem is easy to state. Most multimodal systems can understand one turn of input, then fall apart when the conversation becomes longer, the history becomes visual, or the answer needs to come back as speech. InteractiveOmni tackles that by combining a shared LLM backbone with modality-specific encoders and a speech decoder, then training the whole stack in stages so the model learns alignment, conversation, and memory in the right order.

One brain for seeing, hearing, and speaking

The clearest way to read the method is as a single reasoning core with two sensory front ends and one speaking back end. Figure 3 is the right mental picture.

What to notice in the figure is the division of labor. The vision encoder turns images and video into visual tokens, the audio encoder turns waveforms into audio tokens, the LLM reasons over those tokens, and the speech decoder turns the response into speech tokens and then waveform audio. The model is not trying to keep raw pixels or raw wave samples inside the language model. It first translates each modality into a token space the LLM can handle.

That pipeline is simple on purpose. InteractiveOmni is not built around a clever new hidden-state trick. It is built around a clear interface: convert every input into a shared internal language, let one backbone reason over it, and then let the output branch into either text or speech.

The model choices follow that logic. InteractiveOmni-4B uses InternViT for vision, Whisper for audio, Qwen3-4B as the language backbone, and CosyVoice2 for speech. The 8B version keeps the same shape but widens the LLM to Qwen3-8B. That matters because the paper is not proposing one special-size model; it is showing that the recipe scales.

The token plumbing is the real engineering

The paper spends a lot of effort making the front ends compact enough for long, mixed-context interaction. For audio, the input is resampled to 16 kHz and converted into a 128-channel mel-spectrogram, then pooled down to 25 Hz. In plain terms, one second of audio becomes 25 tokens. That is a good compromise for a speech assistant: the model keeps enough timing and content detail to understand speech, but the sequence is short enough that the LLM is not flooded with frame-level noise.

The vision side follows the same principle. Images are split with a dynamic-resolution strategy into 448 x 448 tiles, so the model preserves aspect ratio and does not blindly squash every image into one fixed square. Then pixel shuffle reduces the token count to one-sixteenth of the original. A 448 x 448 image ends up as 64 visual tokens, which is small enough to coexist with audio and dialogue history.

The speech decoder is split into two jobs for the same reason. The speech-token LM predicts discrete speech tokens, and the token2wav generator turns those tokens into audio. That split is important because the language model wants symbolic outputs, while the user wants an actual voice. InteractiveOmni also streams speech by interleaving generated text tokens and speech tokens in a 5:25 ratio. In practice, that means the model can keep reasoning in text while still releasing speech in chunks, instead of waiting until the whole answer is finished.

The last detail is easy to miss but important for the user experience: speech style is not fixed. The paper uses user instructions and text hidden features to steer the generated voice, so the system can sound more emotional or more expressive when the task calls for it. That is a small design choice with a big effect on how human the interaction feels.

The data has to teach memory, not just recognition

The architecture would not mean much without data that actually requires the model to remember things. InteractiveOmni does not rely on ordinary single-turn question answering. It builds a multi-turn dialogue pipeline designed to force the model to use what happened earlier in the conversation.

Figure 4 shows the recipe.

The important idea is that each turn can draw from a different kind of context. The paper organizes questions into five groups:

  • Image-irrelevant: the answer does not depend on the current image or video at all.
  • Image-relevant, current turn: the answer comes from the current visual input and the current question.
  • Historical image memory: the answer requires remembering a visual detail from an earlier turn.
  • Historical text memory: the answer depends on a previous spoken or written exchange.
  • Historical image and text memory: the answer requires both older visual context and older dialogue text.

That mix is doing more than creating variety. It teaches the model when to trust the current frame and when to reach back into history. Without that pressure, a multimodal assistant can look good on the latest turn while quietly forgetting the image that appeared three exchanges ago.

The paper builds these conversations up to roughly 20 turns, so the memory burden becomes real rather than symbolic. It also turns the text-form question-answer pairs into speech-based pairs through TTS, which means the same interaction can train both understanding and speech generation. That is the bridge from ordinary multimodal QA to actual spoken dialogue.

The scale of the training data reflects that goal. The method uses millions of image-text and video-text pairs, hundreds of thousands of hours of audio, and large omni-modal corpora that mix audio, image, video, and text. On top of that, it adds large TTS corpora and smaller style-controlled speech sets that teach the model about speaking rate, emotion, dialect, and persona. The point is not the exact dataset count. The point is that long-horizon interaction has to be learned from examples where history, style, and modality mix together.

Training in the order the problem actually appears

InteractiveOmni is trained in two broad phases, but each phase has its own internal logic. The model is first taught to align modalities, then taught to use them in conversation, and finally tuned to prefer better responses and better speech.

The first phase is about alignment. The model starts from pretrained Qwen3, InternViT-300M, and Whisper-large-v3 components, then learns in three steps: vision-text first, audio-text second, and finally mixed multimodal data such as audio-image-text and audio-video-text. That ordering is sensible because each new modality is introduced only after the previous one has a stable bridge into the language model. Instruction-following data is also mixed into pretraining, so the model does not treat instructions as an afterthought.

There is also a simple efficiency trick here: the training uses data packing with a maximum token length of 32,768. The paper does not present that number as a conceptual breakthrough, and it should not be read that way. Its job is to keep long video sequences and long audio-visual dialogues trainable without collapsing everything into tiny fragments.

The second phase is where the system learns to act like an assistant. Multi-task supervised fine-tuning exposes the model to speech-based questions and text-based answers, including speech-to-text and image-speech-to-text examples derived from the multimodal corpora. This is the point where the model stops being only a cross-modal encoder and becomes an instruction-following conversational system.

To make end-to-end speech conversation work, the speech decoder is then trained directly. Large Chinese-English TTS corpora are used first to align text tokens with speech tokens. The paper also uses hard sample mining because synthetic TTS data contains many easy cases, and easy cases can drown out the difficult ones. After that, the model is trained on speech-to-speech conversational data and style-controllable speech data so it can answer with more natural rhythm, emotion, and role-play behavior.

Finally, the paper applies DPO to sharpen the quality of the generated content. The description is practical rather than ceremonial: in multi-turn dialogue, the final turn is what users actually feel, so optimizing that turn improves the interactive experience most directly. The authors also report that checkpoint merging helps on the omni-modal understanding side, which is a reminder that some gains come from simple engineering choices after the main training recipe is in place.

The important lesson here is not just that there are many stages. It is that the stages match the actual bottlenecks. First teach the model to align modalities, then teach it to follow instructions, then teach it to speak, then teach it to prefer better answers, and finally teach it to remember across turns.

Why the memory benchmark matters

InteractiveOmni is not just claiming to be an omni-modal model. It is trying to show that memory is a first-class capability, so the paper builds a benchmark around that failure mode. Figure 5 is the most direct proof.

What to notice is the shape of the curve, not the exact point values. As the turn distance grows and more historical images must be recalled, every model gets worse. InteractiveOmni still degrades, but it degrades more gracefully than the open-source baselines. That matters because long-horizon dialogue is not about perfect recall. It is about not losing the thread too quickly.

The benchmark itself, MMMB, is built to make that difficulty obvious. It contains dialogue groups with up to 15 turns, and only the final turn is scored. Earlier turns exist as context, not as a place to make the task easier. The final question may require text memory, image memory, or a combination of both.

The headline result is easy to remember. InteractiveOmni-8B reaches 58.17 average on MMMB, close to Gemini-2.5-Flash at 60.84 and well ahead of the open-source baselines. The 4B model is also strong, and the abstract’s broader point is that it stays close to the larger variant while using about half the parameters. That combination is what makes the model interesting as an open-source route to a real interactive assistant.

Speech interaction is not just speech generation

The second benchmark, MSIB, asks a different question. It is not “can the model transcribe or synthesize speech?” It is “can it hold a spoken conversation that feels coherent, expressive, and responsive over multiple turns?” The benchmark covers basic conversation, emotional expression, speech rate control, role-playing, creative capacity, and instruction following.

That human evaluation figure is useful because it reminds you that speech quality and content quality are not the same thing. A model can say the right words and still feel flat, overly formal, or emotionally wrong. InteractiveOmni is evaluated on both automated scores and human preference, and the story is consistent: the model is preferred when the judge cares about the whole interaction, not just the transcript.

The numbers line up with that reading. On the model-as-judge version of MSIB, InteractiveOmni-4B reaches an overall average of 3.95, and InteractiveOmni-8B reaches 4.03. That puts both above Qwen2.5-Omni-7B and Kimi-Audio on the overall conversational score. The point is not that one system wins every subcategory. The point is that the method produces a balanced assistant that can keep content, speech quality, and style together.

This is also where the 5:25 text-speech interleaving starts to make sense. The model is not waiting until the end to figure out how to sound. It is generating spoken output as part of the reasoning loop, which is what makes the interaction feel more native.

What the broader benchmarks say

The paper does not only evaluate memory and conversation. It also checks whether the model stays competent on ordinary image, video, audio, omni-modal, ASR, TTS, and spoken dialogue benchmarks. That breadth matters because a memory-focused model could otherwise look good only on the new benchmark it was designed to impress.

The broad pattern is reassuring. InteractiveOmni remains competitive with strong vision-language models on image and video understanding, performs well on audio understanding and speech-to-text tasks, and produces speech that is close to dedicated TTS systems on some settings. The 8B model is especially notable on omni-modal and speech benchmarks, while the 4B model is the practical surprise: it keeps much of the larger model’s behavior with far fewer parameters.

That is the real payoff of the recipe. The model is not just a memory demo. It is a unified system that can understand multiple modalities, answer in text, answer in speech, and stay coherent across turns without needing a separate ASR-TTS stack bolted around it.

Where the method is still fragile

InteractiveOmni is strong, but it is not free of tradeoffs. The paper depends on large, carefully curated multimodal and speech datasets, which means the recipe is data-hungry and not something you can reproduce casually. Long-horizon memory also still degrades as the recall burden grows; the model is better than the baselines, but it is not immune to forgetting. And on some narrow speech or ASR metrics, specialized systems can still outperform a broad omni-modal assistant.

Those limits are worth keeping in mind because they show what the method really is: a strong general-purpose interactive model, not a perfectly solved speech agent. The hard part is not only speech generation. It is deciding what to preserve, what to compress, and how to keep the history alive without making the model sluggish or brittle.

What to remember

InteractiveOmni’s main idea is simple enough to remember and detailed enough to matter. Use one shared backbone, give vision and audio their own encoders, train the model to align modalities before you ask it to converse, then use speech conversation data, DPO, and memory-heavy multi-turn data to make the assistant feel continuous rather than reset after every turn.

If you want one sentence to carry away, make it this: InteractiveOmni treats hearing, remembering, and speaking as one coupled design problem, and that is why it can act more like a real assistant than a stitched-together pipeline.
