Llama-AVSR

Llama-AVSR is best understood as a token interface for speech and lip motion. Instead of training a full end-to-end AVSR stack, it freezes strong audio and video encoders, shortens their feature streams, projects them into the embedding space of a Llama-style decoder, and lets that decoder generate the transcript autoregressively. The LLM is not being asked to become a speech recognizer from scratch. It is being asked to read modality tokens that have already been shaped to look like prompt context.

That is why the paper sits naturally in the multimodal hearing and omni assistants family. The central problem is not just hearing more modalities. It is deciding how much temporal detail has to survive the bridge so the LLM can still see phonetic content, lip motion, and the task prompt without drowning in tokens.

What to notice in the figure is the shape of the whole method. The encoders are frozen. The projectors are small. The decoder stays mostly frozen too. The only thing that really changes from task to task is which modality tokens are present before the text prompt.

That is the core mental model. Llama-AVSR is not a special speech head bolted onto an LLM. It is a way of turning speech and visual speech into a sequence the LLM already knows how to continue.

The bridge is the model

The first thing the paper does is keep the heavy backbones out of the trainable set. In the main setup, Whisper-medium handles audio and AV-HuBERT Large handles video. Those encoders are good at extracting speech-bearing features, but they are not what gets updated during training. The Llama-style decoder is also kept frozen. Training is concentrated in the small modules that bridge the gap: modality-specific projectors, plus LoRA where needed.

That choice matters for two reasons.

First, it keeps the method parameter-efficient. The paper’s strong ASR and AVSR results come from training only tens of millions of parameters, not from rewriting the whole stack. Second, it makes the system modular. You can swap encoders or the LLM without redesigning the entire architecture, which is exactly what you want if the real question is how to route speech into a language model.

The projectors are intentionally simple: two linear layers with a ReLU in between. That sounds modest, but it is the right kind of modest. Their job is not to invent new speech features. Their job is to translate encoder outputs into the LLM’s token space while preserving enough local structure for the decoder to use.

The hidden bottleneck is sequence length. A few seconds of audio or video quickly turns into a long run of frame-level features. If every frame becomes a token, the LLM gets swamped. So the projector is also the efficiency gate: it decides how much of that stream survives and how many tokens the decoder has to read.

Why token concatenation is the clean move

Llama-AVSR chooses decoder-only token fusion instead of a separate cross-attention block. That is a small architectural decision with a big effect. Cross-attention would make the LLM look into a side memory bank of speech or video features. Concatenation makes the multimodal input behave like one long prompt. The decoder just reads left to right.

That keeps the interface simple:

  • audio and video are encoded separately,
  • each modality is compressed to a manageable length,
  • each modality is projected into the LLM embedding space,
  • the resulting tokens are concatenated with the text prompt,
  • the decoder predicts the transcription autoregressively.

The paper’s main decoding rule is just next-token prediction over that combined sequence.

Formula card: autoregressive transcription

Purpose: define how the model generates the transcript one token at a time from audio, video, and prompt tokens.

Intuition: once the encoders have turned speech and lip motion into tokens, transcription becomes ordinary left-to-right sequence generation.

Formula:

p(Y∣Xaud,Xvid,Xtext)=∏i=1Np(yi∣Xaud,Xvid,Xtext,y<i)p(\mathbf{Y}\mid \mathbf{X}_{\mathsf{aud}},\mathbf{X}_{\mathsf{vid}},\mathbf{X}_{\mathsf{text}})=\prod_{i=1}^{N}p(y_i \mid \mathbf{X}_{\mathsf{aud}},\mathbf{X}_{\mathsf{vid}},\mathbf{X}_{\mathsf{text}}, y_{<i})p(Y∣Xaud​,Xvid​,Xtext​)=i=1∏N​p(yi​∣Xaud​,Xvid​,Xtext​,y<i​)

Symbols:

  • \mathbf{Y} is the full output transcript token sequence.
  • y_i is the i-th output token.
  • N is the number of output tokens.
  • \mathbf{X}_{\mathsf{aud}} is the audio token sequence.
  • \mathbf{X}_{\mathsf{vid}} is the video token sequence.
  • \mathbf{X}_{\mathsf{text}} is the prompt or instruction tokens.
  • y_{<i} means all previously generated output tokens before position i.

Read-aloud meaning: the probability of the whole transcript is the product of the probability of each next token, given the multimodal prefix and the text already generated.

Why it matters: this is why the model can stay decoder-only. There is no separate transcription head or fusion classifier. The whole system is trained as next-token prediction over a multimodal prompt.

LoRA is the narrow place where adaptation happens

The paper keeps the trainable surface small on purpose, but it does not keep it zero. The LLM gets LoRA adapters, and for the VSR task the video encoder can also get LoRA. That extra flexibility is useful because visual speech is harder to align than audio speech. The video side is trying to turn lip motion into features that behave like linguistic evidence, and a little task-specific adaptation helps.

The ablations make the point clearly: adding LoRA to the LLM helps across ASR, and adding LoRA to both the LLM and video encoder helps VSR even more. In other words, the LLM alone does not magically understand the visual prefix. It still needs a small amount of adaptation to treat those tokens as useful conditioning context.

The choice of backbone matters too. The paper uses AV-HuBERT for video because its self-supervised units are already close to linguistic structure, which makes them easier for the LLM to align with. That is one reason AV-HuBERT does better than a more generic visual representation in this setup. The same idea holds on the text side: the results shift when the LLM changes, so the method is modular without being backbone-agnostic.

This is a useful design lesson. You do not need to fine-tune everything if the front end already captures most of the structure. You do need a small adaptation layer so the LLM can interpret the new tokens as speech evidence rather than as random embeddings.

Compression is where efficiency and detail fight

The second major idea in Llama-AVSR is modality-aware compression. The paper does not feed the decoder every frame. Instead, it concatenates K consecutive features along the hidden dimension, which reduces the sequence length by a factor of K while making each token wider. Put simply, it packs several frames into one token.

That is a good trade for ASR because speech is redundant and phonetic content is spread across nearby frames. It is a riskier trade for VSR because lip motion can be more fragile. If you compress video too aggressively, you erase cues the LLM still needs.

The paper’s compression plot makes that tradeoff easy to see.

What to notice in the figure is the difference between the modalities. ASR tolerates more compression than VSR. Video starts to lose accuracy sooner because the visual signal is already thin and timing-sensitive. AVSR sits between them: audio can be compressed fairly hard, but video usually needs to stay more detailed.

That is why the paper uses different compression rates for audio and video in the AVSR setting. The lesson is not “compress everything as much as possible.” The lesson is “compress each modality until the decoder can still recover the cues that modality is best at carrying.” Audio can usually be squeezed harder than video. When the input is noisy, the LLM leans more on the visual stream, so preserving lip detail becomes even more important.

This is also the practical reason the model is efficient. The LLM does most of the compute, so reducing token count matters a lot. But the figure shows the ceiling: once compression destroys the very evidence the task needs, smaller context is no longer a win.

What the method buys you

The headline result is not just that the model works. It is that it works while barely moving the frozen backbones. On LRS3 plus VoxCeleb2, Llama-AVSR reaches 0.79% WER on ASR with 42M trainable parameters and 0.77% WER on AVSR with 57M trainable parameters. That is the main proof that the token-interface recipe is strong enough to compete at the top end.

The more interesting nuance is that the gains are uneven across tasks. ASR is the easiest fit for this setup, because audio tokens already carry most of the linguistic signal. AVSR benefits from the visual stream, especially in noisy conditions. VSR is the hardest case: the model improves and beats prior LLM-based work, but it still trails the strongest specialized non-LLM methods.

That gap is important because it shows the limits of the approach. The paper is not claiming that a frozen LLM plus a small bridge automatically solves visual speech recognition. It is showing that the bridge is powerful, but only when the encoders, compression rate, and LLM choice are all tuned carefully.

What to remember

The simplest way to remember Llama-AVSR is this:

  • extract audio and video features with frozen encoders,
  • compress the time dimension so the sequence is manageable,
  • project the result into the LLM’s embedding space,
  • concatenate those tokens with a short text prompt,
  • let a mostly frozen Llama-style decoder generate the transcript.

That is the whole idea. Speech recognition becomes tokenized prompting, not end-to-end retraining. The strength of the paper is that it makes the bridge explicit, keeps the trainable surface small, and then shows that the bridge still has enough capacity to deliver strong ASR and AVSR. The remaining open questions are just as practical: can a better projector close the last VSR gap, can the design transfer beyond LRS3, and can the same recipe keep its gains in streaming settings?
