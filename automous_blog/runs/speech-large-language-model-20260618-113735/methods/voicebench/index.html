VoiceBench: A Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-Based Voice Assistants

VoiceBench sits in the evaluation, benchmarks, and post-training data family, but it is really about a simpler question: what happens when a voice assistant hears the same intent through different accents, microphones, speaking styles, and kinds of speech messiness? The paper argues that clean speech scores are not enough. A system can look fine on ASR-style tests and still fail when the user speaks with noise, disfluency, or a different accent.

At a high level, VoiceBench is a benchmark for checking whether a voice assistant keeps the user’s intent intact. It does that in two layers. First, it measures core capability on general knowledge, instruction following, and safety. Then it stress-tests the same models under speaker, environment, and content variation.

Figure 1 gives the whole picture in one glance.

What to notice is the split in the middle. The left side is the ordinary “can it answer?” part. The right side is the realistic “does it still work when speech gets messy?” part. VoiceBench treats both as part of the same evaluation problem, which is the main conceptual move in the paper.

The benchmark is built to score understanding, not transcription

That distinction matters. The paper does not want to reward a model just because its speech recognizer was accurate. It wants to know whether the assistant’s response was right, safe, and well formed. So the evaluation focuses on content quality rather than speech output naturalness or raw ASR quality.

This is why VoiceBench runs the same task in two forms: as text instructions and as spoken instructions. If a model performs well on text but falls apart on speech, the gap is about speech handling, not about the task itself. That makes the benchmark useful for finding where the failure actually lives.

The scoring is task-specific:

  • Open-ended QA on AlpacaEval and CommonEval is judged with a GPT score from 1 to 5.
  • Reference-based QA on SD-QA is checked against human answers, using PANDA or GPT-based correctness checks.
  • Multiple-choice QA on OpenBookQA and MMSU uses rule-based answer extraction.
  • IFEval uses loose and strict instruction-following accuracy.
  • AdvBench uses refusal rate, because safe behavior means declining harmful requests.

That mix is deliberate. Open-ended answers need a flexible judge. Multiple-choice and refusal tasks can be scored more mechanically. VoiceBench uses the simplest scoring method that still matches the task.

What goes into the core task set

VoiceBench starts by curating three capabilities that matter for real voice assistants.

First is general knowledge. The paper includes open-ended QA, reference-based QA, and multiple-choice QA because these stress different kinds of reasoning. Open-ended questions check whether the model can give a useful answer without being boxed into one exact phrase. Reference-based questions check factual correctness when a ground-truth answer exists. Multiple-choice questions check whether the model can still choose cleanly when the answer space is constrained.

The reference-based part is more careful than it looks. Some SD-QA questions ask about facts that change over time and cannot be answered reliably without context. To avoid that trap, the paper keeps only questions that at least one strong LLM can answer correctly from internal knowledge. That keeps the benchmark focused on reasoning and recall instead of live web lookup.

The multiple-choice setup is also adapted to speech. For MMSU, the paper randomly samples four options to keep the audio manageable, and it removes math and computer science examples because those categories are less voice-friendly. The benchmark is not just copied from text evaluation; it is reshaped so the audio channel can actually carry the task.

Second is instruction following. The benchmark uses IFEval, but trims it to short, speech-friendly instructions. That keeps the task aligned with voice interaction instead of copying a text benchmark wholesale.

Third is safety. AdvBench supplies harmful prompts, and the expected behavior is refusal. That sounds simple, but it turns out to be one of the clearest places where speech and text behavior can diverge.

The dataset construction is careful about voice-specific constraints. For the speech version of the data, the paper limits spoken instructions to 30 seconds. That is a practical guardrail, not an arbitrary one: the benchmark is meant to fit the capabilities of current systems.

Real speech and synthetic speech both matter

The paper uses both real and synthetic spoken instructions, and that is one of its strongest design choices. Real speech is expensive to collect and hard to control, but it is the best proxy for deployment. Synthetic speech is easy to scale, easier to perturb, and easier to standardize across tasks.

The construction pipeline is simple in spirit. The paper first normalizes the text into something a TTS system can pronounce naturally, then synthesizes speech, and then manually checks the result. That step is important because raw benchmark text often contains things that sound fine on a page but awkward in speech, such as symbols, abbreviations, or formatting artifacts.

The point is not to make synthetic speech identical to human speech. The point is to make it useful for controlled evaluation. The paper later shows that synthetic data generally makes models look better than real speech, but the relative ranking is similar. That is enough to make synthetic speech a reliable evaluation tool, as long as you remember that it is still easier than real-world audio.

CommonEval is especially useful here because it comes from real recordings made in everyday settings, not polished studio speech. It acts as the reality check against the cleaner synthetic set.

What the first results say about today’s voice assistants

Before the paper even starts on robustness, the main benchmark already exposes a gap. Simple ASR-plus-LLM pipelines beat the open-source end-to-end voice assistants by a large margin on spoken instructions. The gap is more than 20 points overall, which is hard to ignore.

That result is important because it flips a common intuition. End-to-end audio models may look impressive on ASR or audio understanding tasks, but that does not mean they are better voice assistants. VoiceBench shows that a plain pipeline can still be stronger when the job is “hear the instruction and answer correctly.”

The paper also finds that the text-to-speech gap is real. Some models do fine when the exact instruction is given as text, then lose a lot of performance once the same instruction is spoken. In a few cases, the drop is dramatic, and it is especially visible on multiple-choice tasks where end-to-end models sometimes look barely better than random guessing.

Another sharp finding is safety. Some assistants refuse harmful text prompts, but when the same request is spoken, they answer instead of declining. That is a serious failure mode, and it would be easy to miss in a benchmark that only checks text input.

VoiceBench also compares real and synthetic CommonEval prompts. The ranking stays similar, but the synthetic version is easier across the board. That is a reminder that benchmark cleanliness can make a model look more robust than it really is.

Speaker variation is not a smooth slope

After the core tasks, the paper asks what happens when the speaker changes. This is where VoiceBench becomes a true robustness benchmark instead of just a task suite.

The speaker axis includes speaking speed, age, pitch, volume, and accent. Figure 2 shows the first four of those axes as a family of sweeps.

What to notice is that performance is not a steady downhill line. Most models stay fairly stable across a range, then drop sharply when the variation crosses a threshold. That is a useful mental model for speech systems: they are not gradually sensitive to every little change. They often seem fine until the acoustic conditions move far enough away from what they expect.

Speaking speed is the most disruptive axis. Very slow or very fast speech hurts the end-to-end models much more than it hurts the naive pipeline. Age, pitch, and volume matter less, except at extremes. Very low volume causes trouble, and some models are more sensitive to child speech or high-pitched speech than others.

Accent is the most socially important speaker shift. The paper checks both real and synthetic accents, and the trend is the same in both cases: high-resource accents are easier, while low-resource accents such as Indian English or Philippine English are harder. Real accent data is tougher than synthetic accent data, which makes sense because synthetic accent generation often softens the very differences that make the real accent challenging.

Figure 3 makes that pattern visible on the real-accent set.

What to notice here is not the exact ranking of every accent, but the gap between familiar and less-resourced speech varieties. The benchmark is telling you that accent robustness is not uniform. A model may look fine on one set of English accents and still stumble badly on another.

Environmental corruption exposes a different weakness

Speaker variation is about who is talking. Environmental variation is about where and how the speech is being heard.

VoiceBench tests far-field speech, clipping, reverberation, packet loss, and two kinds of noise. Each of these simulates a real deployment problem:

  • Far-field speech weakens high-frequency information when the speaker is far away.
  • Clipping simulates microphone distortion or over-amplification.
  • Reverberation adds echoes, which blur the signal over time.
  • Packet loss drops pieces of the signal, which is a realistic transmission failure.
  • White noise and babble noise simulate steady and competing background interference.

Figure 5 bundles those conditions into one picture.

What to notice is that the naive pipeline often looks surprisingly strong here. The likely reason is that ASR acts like an implicit denoiser before the LLM sees the input. That extra transcription step can smooth out some noise and distortion.

The end-to-end models do not get that buffer. They read the raw audio directly, so any corruption in the signal has to be handled inside the speech stack itself. That makes them more brittle in noisy conditions, especially when the corruption changes timing or removes key spectral detail.

This does not mean cascaded systems are ideal. It means they are still a very strong baseline when the deployment problem is “understand noisy speech and answer well.” VoiceBench makes that tradeoff visible instead of assuming end-to-end is automatically better.

Content noise is the most human kind of speech mess

The last variation axis is content itself. Spoken language is not just clean words in a clean order. People repair themselves, repeat phrases, insert filler words, start over, or mispronounce a term and keep going. VoiceBench tests those cases too.

The paper rewrites clean instructions into noisy versions that include repairs, repetitions, pauses, interjections, false starts, mispronunciations, and grammar errors. That is a smart move because it isolates a question that speech benchmarks often skip: how much extra wording can the assistant tolerate before it loses the user’s intent?

The answer is: a little clutter is usually fine, but meaning-changing noise is not.

Grammar errors are often the least damaging. They look messy on the surface, but they usually keep the intended words intact. Disfluencies like pauses or interjections are also tolerable when the core request remains obvious. Mispronunciations are worse because they can change the transcript and push the assistant toward the wrong word. Repairs are often the hardest of all, because the speaker briefly introduces the wrong term and then corrects it. If the model latches onto the first fragment, it can answer the wrong question entirely.

That is the deeper lesson of the content-noise experiments. Speech assistants do not just need to hear words. They need to recover intent from incomplete, corrected, and sometimes contradictory speech.

What the paper is really teaching

VoiceBench is not trying to crown a winner in a leaderboard. It is trying to correct the evaluation lens. The core lesson is that voice assistants should be judged on whether they preserve intent under realistic variation, not just on whether they can transcribe clean audio or answer a polished spoken question.

That is why the benchmark combines core capability tests with stress tests, and why it uses both real and synthetic speech. The benchmark is more expensive than a simple ASR test, but it sees failures that ASR tests miss.

It also has limits. It does not directly score speech-output naturalness or turn-taking. Synthetic speech is still not the same as real conversational audio. And several of the scores depend on GPT judges or rule-based proxies rather than a single universal metric. Those limitations do not weaken the benchmark’s main point, but they do define the next problems to solve.

If you want the shortest possible memory hook, it is this: VoiceBench asks whether a voice assistant can keep the meaning, the safety boundary, and the instruction shape intact when speech stops being clean. A voice assistant that only works on polished audio is not ready for real users, and VoiceBench is designed to make that gap visible.
