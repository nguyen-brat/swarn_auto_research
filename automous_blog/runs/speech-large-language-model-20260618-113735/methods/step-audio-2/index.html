Step-Audio 2 Technical Report

Step-Audio 2 is built around a simple idea with a lot of consequences: keep speech native for as long as possible. Instead of forcing every spoken input through a transcript-only bottleneck, the model freezes a pretrained audio encoder, adapts those features into an LLM, and lets the decoder emit interleaved text and audio tokens. That means the same system can listen, reason, and speak back without flattening away emotion, speaking style, or timbre.

The paper pushes that idea one step further by adding retrieval and tools. If the model needs fresh facts, it can call web search. If it needs a different voice or style, it can pull from an audio-search voice library. So the method is not just “speech in, text out.” It is a grounded speech assistant that can answer, imitate, translate, and keep the conversation going in a voice-like way.

The whole loop in one picture

The architecture is easiest to remember as listen, think, speak. Speech comes in, the language model reasons over a compressed acoustic representation, and speech tokens go back out through a detokenizer.

What to notice in the diagram is the separation of jobs. The audio encoder does perception, the adaptor makes the sequence shorter, the LLM does reasoning and token prediction, and the detokenizer turns the result back into a waveform. Step-Audio 2 does not ask one module to do everything. It keeps each piece narrow enough that the system can scale.

The deployment stack also uses voice activity detection to trim silence, which helps this behave like a live voice system instead of a batch recognizer.

Why the audio front end is frozen

The audio encoder is pretrained on speech and audio tasks such as recognition and acoustic understanding, then frozen during the rest of training. That choice is there for stability. If the perception stack keeps moving, the model has to relearn the meaning of its own acoustic features every time the rest of the system changes.

The adaptor is the small but important middle layer. It downsamples the encoder output from 25 Hz to 12.5 Hz before the LLM sees it. That cuts the sequence length in half, which makes speech easier for the language model to handle without throwing away the entire acoustic structure.

This is one of the paper’s core engineering instincts: do not make the language model swallow raw speech frame by frame if a shorter latent signal can keep the important evidence intact. The frozen encoder gives you stable perception; the adaptor gives you a manageable sequence length.

Why the model emits both text and audio tokens

The big shift from earlier speech LLMs is that Step-Audio 2 does not stop at text generation. It predicts an interleaved sequence of discrete text and audio tokens at a fixed ratio, padded so the format stays aligned.

That sounds like a small formatting detail, but it is the reason the model can keep paralinguistic information alive. Text tokens carry meaning. Audio tokens carry the voice layer: tone, rhythm, emotion, and timbre. If you collapse everything to text too early, you can answer the question and still sound flat, generic, or emotionally wrong.

The interleaving format keeps those two channels tied together. The model can reason in language while still reserving space for how the answer should sound. After generation, the audio tokens are extracted and sent to the detokenizer, while the whole interleaved sequence becomes part of the conversation history for the next turn.

That is why Step-Audio 2 feels like a speech model rather than a text model with a speech wrapper. The answer is not translated into voice after the fact. The model is trained to speak in a way that already contains the acoustic instructions.

How speech becomes waveform again

The detokenizer is a second stage, not an afterthought. Step-Audio 2 uses a flow-matching module plus a HiFi-GAN vocoder. The flow-matching stage converts the discrete audio tokens into Mel spectrograms, and the vocoder turns those spectrograms into waveform audio.

This split is useful because discrete tokens are good for language-model-style prediction, but they are not yet something you can play as sound. The spectrogram is the bridge back into a smooth acoustic space, and the vocoder is the last mile to natural audio.

The paper also strengthens the flow-matching block by adding a CNN-based encoder layer after each self-attention module inside the transformer block. The practical effect is better reconstruction of the Mel spectrogram, which shows up as cleaner pronunciation and more similar timbre. In other words, the detokenizer is not just recovering sound; it is recovering the feel of the sound.

The training recipe: align first, then broaden, then sharpen

Step-Audio 2 is trained in stages because the model has to learn several different things without destroying the parts that already work.

The first stage aligns the adaptor. The model starts with large ASR data while the audio encoder and LLM stay frozen. That teaches the adaptor how to map speech features into the LLM’s embedding space before the rest of the system starts changing.

After that, the model expands its vocabulary with audio tokens and continues pretraining on large mixtures of text and audio. The point of this phase is to let the language model live in a world where audio tokens are ordinary symbols, not exotic special cases. Text pretraining is preserved, but the backbone now has to understand and generate speech-bearing token streams too.

Then the training broadens into a larger multimodal mix and a cooldown phase with cleaner, higher-quality audio and text. This is where the model learns multilingual ASR, TTS, speech translation, interleaved continuation, and more conversational speech behavior. The paper’s key trick is not any single dataset. It is the ordering: stabilize the interface first, then scale the multimodal behavior, then clean up with better data.

Supervised fine-tuning turns that raw capability into something usable. The paper mixes ASR, audio understanding, speech captioning, TTS, speech translation, dialogue, tool-use scripts, and reasoning-oriented examples. Some of the SFT data explicitly asks the model to describe paralinguistic cues such as emotion, pitch, rhythm, speaking speed, and style. That is exactly what the architecture is built to preserve.

RL is there to make the model think before it speaks

The final post-training stage is reinforcement learning, and the goal is not generic “more alignment” in the abstract. It is to make the model reason efficiently enough for real-time audio interaction.

The first PPO stage uses a binary reward that prefers thinking traces that are neither empty nor too long. That is a practical way to stop the model from rambling before it responds. The second PPO stage replaces the binary rule with a learned reward model that scores response quality. After that, GRPO is used to push the model’s audio perceptual abilities a bit further.

The logic is straightforward: first teach the model to be concise, then teach it to be good, then tighten the audio side again. For a speech assistant, this matters because long internal reasoning can make the conversation feel slow or stiff.

Retrieval and tools turn the model into a grounded assistant

The other major idea in the paper is that the model should not rely on its own memory for everything. Step-Audio 2 can call external tools directly from voice instructions. The set includes web search, date/time, weather, and a custom audio-search tool.

That audio-search tool is the most distinctive piece. The paper builds a large voice library with clips, transcripts, and descriptions, so the model can retrieve a speaking style and then mimic the timbre or delivery. If the user wants a different voice, the model does not have to invent one from scratch. It can ground the request in an actual retrieved speech sample.

The usage examples make the scope obvious.

What to notice here is that the same system can shift across several kinds of speech behavior without changing its interface. It can answer in a default voice, move into a childlike or older voice, speak in a dialect, switch to a whisper, say something sadly, translate between languages, check a current fact, or respond with empathy to a distressed speaker. The point is not that each example is a separate model. The point is that one model can combine reasoning, voice control, and external grounding in one conversation loop.

That is also where the retrieval layer helps with hallucination. If the question depends on a fresh fact, web search gives the model something real to anchor to. If the question is about voice or style, audio search gives it a concrete acoustic reference. The model is still doing language modeling, but it is no longer doing it in a vacuum.

What the benchmarks say about the design

The results line up with the method. The model reaches 3.14% average WER on English ASR and 3.08% average CER on Chinese ASR. It gets 83.09 average accuracy on StepEval-Audio-Paralinguistic, 78.0 on MMAU, 39.26 BLEU on CoVoST 2 speech-to-text translation, 30.87 BLEU on CVSS speech-to-speech translation, and 86.8/99.5 trigger precision/recall on StepEval-Audio-Toolcall for audio search. It also performs very well on URO-Bench, especially in Chinese speech-to-speech conversation.

Those numbers are useful not because they are the whole story, but because they show the pipeline is doing the right kind of work. Step-Audio 2 is not just transcribing speech more accurately. It is preserving style, handling translation, grounding facts, and keeping spoken dialogue coherent.

What to keep in mind

The price of this recipe is real. The model depends on very large amounts of text and audio data, the training stack is multi-stage, and some of the evaluation is still on curated or in-house benchmarks. That means the method is powerful, but it is not lightweight.

The easiest way to remember the paper is this:

  • freeze perception so the acoustic front end stays stable,
  • interleave text and audio so the model can keep meaning and style together,
  • add retrieval and tools so the assistant can ground facts and voices in the real world,
  • use staged post-training so the model learns to reason without becoming slow or generic.

That combination is what turns Step-Audio 2 from a speech recognizer into a real spoken assistant.
