SODA: Scaling Open Discrete Audio

SODA is best understood as a recipe paper, not a new architectural trick. It asks a practical question: if speech is broken into discrete tokens that preserve both meaning and acoustics, can one decoder-only transformer learn audio continuation, text continuation, ASR, TTS, and even speech-to-speech translation from the same next-token objective? The paper’s answer is yes, but only if the data mix, token layout, and compute allocation are chosen carefully.

That is why it fits naturally in the native speech-text language models family. The goal is not to bolt audio onto a text backbone and hope the model figures it out. The goal is to make speech and text live in one token stream so a single decoder can treat them as different views of the same sequence.

The whole method can be read as a simple flow:

The key move is the interleaving step. Speech is not turned into a separate side input, and it is not forced through a word-level alignment pipeline. Instead, utterances are packed as token sequences that can be read in either direction: audio first for ASR-style learning, or text first for TTS-style learning.

What to notice in the figure is that the same utterance is shown twice, in two orders. Audio-first format teaches the model to continue from speech into text. Text-first format teaches the reverse path from text into speech. That matters because SODA is trying to become a unified backbone, not a model that only knows one direction well.

The paper also keeps the formatting deliberately loose at the utterance level. Adjacent chunks from the same document are interleaved, but not forced into word-level synchronization. That avoids brittle alignment errors and lets the training data stay large and diverse. In practice, this is what makes the method feel like a general-purpose language model rather than a hand-built speech pipeline.

The first question is not scaling. It is what data the model should actually see

Before SODA ever looks at scaling laws, the authors run a small sanity check called SODA-prelim. The result is instructive. A vanilla decoder trained on interleaved discrete audio tokens can already learn useful cross-modal behavior, including ASR and TTS, and it does a decent job on acoustic understanding. But it is weak on semantic understanding and general text knowledge.

That failure is the clue. Speech-only data is not enough if the model is supposed to behave like a broad speech-text foundation model. The speech stream contains a lot of useful structure, but not enough world knowledge on its own.

Which speech corpora survive the cut?

The paper compares three large public speech corpora rather than guessing. MLS looks attractive on paper because it is curated audiobook speech, but it performs badly in this setting. The reason is fairly concrete: its transcripts are uncased and unpunctuated, which makes them mismatch standard text, and its fixed-length chunks are too uniform. That hurts cross-modal tasks much more than it hurts pure speech scores.

Yodas and Emilia behave better, but for different reasons. Emilia gives the strongest TTS results, while Yodas gives better text knowledge. The final recipe uses both, because they complement each other instead of duplicating the same failure mode.

The useful lesson here is not “pick the largest corpus.” It is “pick the corpora whose formatting and speaking style match the task you want the decoder to learn.” SODA’s final speech mixture is therefore Yodas plus Emilia, with MLS dropped.

How much text should sneak into an audio model?

The next question is whether a small amount of text can repair the knowledge gaps without ruining audio behavior. The answer is yes, up to a point. The paper sweeps the Nemotron text ratio from 0% upward and finds a very clean tradeoff: text loss improves almost immediately, while audio loss stays flat only when the text share is still tiny.

What to notice in the figure is the asymmetry. A little text buys a large drop in text NLL, which means the model recovers general language knowledge quickly. But once text takes over too much of the mix, audio quality starts to slip. The sweet spot is about 5% text and 95% speech. That is enough text to repair world knowledge and reasoning a bit, but not so much that the decoder stops learning the audio side well.

This is the core data-mixture recipe used everywhere else in the paper. The text is not there to turn SODA into a text LLM. It is there to keep the speech model from becoming semantically narrow.

What happens when you change the token layout itself?

SODA then asks a separate question: should the model see only semantic audio tokens, only semantic plus acoustic tokens, or the full semantic + acoustic + text stream? The answer is easy to remember once you separate the jobs of each token type.

Semantic tokens capture what was said. Acoustic tokens capture how it sounded. Text tokens restore the model’s access to ordinary language knowledge and make cross-modal tasks possible. If you keep only semantic tokens, the model is good at speech meaning but cannot do real audio generation or speech-text conversion. If you add acoustic tokens, acoustic consistency improves, but semantic understanding drops because the model has to spend capacity on sound detail. If you also add text, the model does not suddenly become a better semantic-only recognizer, but it does gain the missing bridge to ASR, TTS, and text tasks.

That is why SODA keeps the full trio even though it accepts a semantic tradeoff. The paper’s goal is a general backbone, not the best possible score on one narrow metric.

Once the recipe is set, the paper needs a cheap way to steer scaling

The next stage is about compute. Rather than running every downstream benchmark on every model, the authors first check whether held-out negative log-likelihood can stand in for downstream performance. If it can, then they can use NLL to search the scaling curve much more cheaply.

It works surprisingly well. Across 64 models, validation NLL correlates strongly with ASR, TTS, and text metrics. The cross-modal tasks are especially tight: lower NLL usually means lower ASR WER and better TTS quality. Text tasks track closely too, and some of them even show a late-emerging jump as loss improves further. Speech-meaning tasks improve more slowly and begin to saturate earlier, which is exactly the kind of pattern you would want a proxy metric to reveal.

What to notice in the scatter plot is that the main trend line is useful even before the final models are trained. That means the authors can search for a compute-optimal recipe using held-out NLL rather than running a huge evaluation suite every time. They also choose the balanced NLL variant, computed on all tokens from the audio-plus-text setup, because it gives the best tradeoff between speech and text correlation.

The main scaling result is that discrete audio wants more data than text recipes usually assume

With the recipe fixed and NLL validated as a proxy, the paper runs an IsoFLOP sweep: 64 models, seven compute budgets, and model sizes ranging from small to several billion parameters. For each compute budget, they find the best model size and token count, then fit power laws across budgets.

Purpose: summarize how compute should be split between model size and training tokens for discrete audio.

Intuition: audio tokens are information-poor compared with text tokens, so the best model should spend relatively more compute on data than a text LLM recipe would suggest.

Formula:

N∗∝C0.367,D∗∝C0.579N^* \propto C^{0.367}, \qquad D^* \propto C^{0.579}N∗∝C0.367,D∗∝C0.579

Symbols:

  • N* is the compute-optimal model size.
  • D* is the compute-optimal number of training tokens.
  • C is the compute budget.

Read-aloud meaning: as compute increases, the best discrete-audio model should grow in both parameters and data, but the data side should grow faster.

Why it matters: this is the paper’s main scaling-law conclusion, and it is different from the classic Chinchilla-style intuition that model size and data should grow in lockstep.

The interpretation is straightforward. SODA trains on about 100 audio tokens per second, which is much denser than text in token count but not necessarily richer in per-token information. The paper argues that this lower information density is why the optimal token count rises faster than the optimal parameter count. In plain English: discrete audio is more data-hungry than text.

That also means the token-to-parameter ratio is not fixed. It rises with scale. The paper projects that the sweet spot is around 13 tokens per parameter at 102010^{20}1020 FLOPs and around 58 tokens per parameter at 102310^{23}1023 FLOPs. So the bigger the audio model gets, the more aggressively it wants data.

What scaling up looks like once the recipe is turned loose

The final SODA models are not trained at the exact compute-optimal point from the sweep. They are intentionally over-trained relative to that frontier because deployment often prefers a smaller model that can be run more cheaply at inference time. In other words, the authors care about a practical backbone, not just the mathematically best point on the loss curve.

What to notice in the figure is that the released models sit beyond the compute-optimal line. That is a deliberate choice. The 4B model is the closest to the frontier, while the smaller models are more heavily over-trained. The paper treats this as a deployment tradeoff: more data than the optimum can buy a more useful checkpoint size, even if it is not the absolute minimum-loss model for that compute budget.

The gains from the cleaned-up recipe are not subtle. Compared with the preliminary multilingual run, the 600M base model improves across the board: ASR gets much better, TTS improves, and semantic and text scores all move in the right direction. By the time the family reaches 4B parameters, the model is competitive across all of the paper’s main categories, including speech meaning, acoustic modeling, text knowledge, and cross-modal tasks.

That broad coverage is the point. SODA is not the strongest specialist in any one slice, but it is the first model in the paper that behaves like a genuinely unified audio-text backbone.

Cold-start turns out to be the safer default

The next question is whether the model should start from a text LLM checkpoint or from scratch. A warm-start seems attractive because the language model already knows a lot of text, but the paper finds that this advantage comes with real costs.

What to notice in the training curves is the stability gap. Cold-start runs improve smoothly and consistently do better on ASR. Warm-start preserves more text knowledge, but it can spike during training and interfere with learning the audio-to-text mappings the model needs for speech tasks. TTS ends up closer between the two, but for general audio capability the paper recommends cold-start as the default.

That is an important lesson because it goes against a common instinct in multimodal modeling. Reusing a text backbone is not automatically the best way to get a speech backbone. In SODA’s setting, learning the audio-text interface from scratch is cleaner than trying to bend a text-first prior to fit speech.

The same backbone can be repurposed for speech-to-speech translation

The paper’s final demonstration is a nice sanity check for the whole idea. Voice-preserving speech-to-speech translation is formatted as just another next-token prediction problem:

No new encoder-decoder stack is introduced. The same decoder-only model is fine-tuned on this sequence, and the result is much better than starting from scratch or from a text-only warm-start. Audio pretraining is the key ingredient, and multilingual pretraining helps even more when the source language is covered by the speech data.

That is what makes SODA feel like a backbone rather than a one-off model. Once speech and text are both token streams, new audio-to-audio tasks become a formatting problem plus fine-tuning, not an architectural redesign.

What to remember

SODA’s recipe is simple to remember once the moving parts are separated. Keep both semantic and acoustic speech codes, add just enough text to recover knowledge, train a decoder-only model on utterance-level interleavings, and spend compute more aggressively on data than on parameters. The payoff is one backbone that can do audio continuation, ASR, TTS, and speech-to-speech translation without changing the core model.

The tradeoff is just as important. Acoustic fidelity, semantic understanding, and text knowledge do not all improve at the same rate. Some skills saturate earlier than others, and the paper does not see strong spontaneous emergence in speech-specific abilities at the scales it tests. It also leaves open several design questions, including tokenizer rate, RVQ weighting, and other ways to balance audio detail against sequence length.

If you want the shortest possible mental model, it is this: SODA treats speech as a token stream with both meaning and sound preserved, then asks a decoder-only transformer to learn the rest from scale.
