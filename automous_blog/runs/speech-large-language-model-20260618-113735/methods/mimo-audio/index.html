MiMo-Audio: Unified Generative Audio-Language Model

MiMo-Audio is built around a strong bet: if speech can be turned into discrete units without throwing away the speaker, the prosody, and the meaning, then a language model should be able to learn speech the same way it learns text. The paper turns that bet into a full pipeline. First it trains a high-fidelity tokenizer for audio. Then it compresses the token stream again so a 7B LLM can read it efficiently. After that, it scales pretraining hard enough for generalization to emerge, and only then does it add instruction tuning and thinking-style supervision.

That sounds straightforward, but speech is a difficult medium. If you compress too aggressively, you lose timbre and rhythm. If you keep too much detail, the sequence becomes so long that the language model spends all its time counting frames. MiMo-Audio’s answer is to separate those two problems. The tokenizer protects the signal. The patch interface protects the LLM.

Start with the thing the tokenizer has to preserve

The paper begins from a practical observation: speech is not just text with extra noise attached. A good speech representation has to keep semantic content, speaker identity, prosody, and cross-modal alignment all at once. That is exactly where many existing approaches split apart. Semantic tokens line up nicely with text, but they flatten the acoustics. Codec-style acoustic tokens reconstruct audio well, but they are not as friendly to language modeling.

MiMo-Audio tries to keep those two pressures in one shared discrete space instead of maintaining separate semantic and acoustic systems. The tokenizer is trained from scratch on a very large corpus so that the model can learn one representation that works for both reconstruction and downstream language modeling.

What to notice is the flow. The waveform becomes a mel-spectrogram, the encoder turns that into a compact continuous sequence, the RVQ module discretizes it into codebook indices, and the decoder plus vocoder tries to recover the waveform. This is not just compression for its own sake. It is a learned speech alphabet.

The design is deliberately asymmetric. The encoder is bidirectional, so it can look across the whole utterance while forming the codes. The decoder side is causal, which helps with streaming-style generation. Between those ends sits residual vector quantization, which is how the model turns continuous audio into a stack of discrete IDs. You can think of those codebooks as a coarse-to-fine ladder: the early ones carry the broad shape of the sound, while later ones add refinements.

The paper also uses a skip connection from an intermediate encoder layer to the final output. That is a small but meaningful clue about the problem they are solving. Speech content and acoustic texture tend to pull in different directions. The skip path gives the tokenizer another way to keep mid-level information alive instead of forcing everything through a single bottleneck.

Why the tokenizer is trained in two passes

The tokenizer itself is not trained in one monolithic phase. It gets a two-stage schedule.

In stage 1, the model learns unified representation by combining audio reconstruction with audio-to-text supervision. That pairing matters. Reconstruction keeps the codes faithful to the waveform. Audio-to-text teaches the discrete representation to stay readable for the language model. If you only optimize reconstruction, you can end up with codes that sound fine but are awkward for language modeling. If you only optimize text alignment, you can lose the speaker and the rhythm. MiMo-Audio keeps both pressures in play.

The reconstruction loss is multi-scale, which is a sensible choice for audio. Matching only one resolution is not enough, because the decoder could get the coarse shape right while missing fine timbre or prosodic detail. Multi-scale comparison makes the decoder pay attention to both the broad envelope and the more local structure.

Stage 2 freezes the tokenization side and focuses on the decoder and vocoder. That freeze is important. Once the downstream model starts relying on these discrete codes, the code space should stop moving around. In this second stage the paper adds adversarial training, using discriminators that look at both periodic structure and spectral structure, plus a feature-matching loss that encourages the generator to imitate the internal activations of the discriminators. In plain terms, stage 1 teaches the tokenizer what to keep; stage 2 teaches the waveform renderer how to sound natural.

The end result is a tokenizer that is not just reconstructive, but also useful enough for the language model to treat as a stable speech interface.

The patch trick: make speech affordable for the 7B backbone

Once the tokenizer exists, the next problem is sequence length. Raw RVQ speech is dense. At 25 Hz with eight codebooks, you are effectively dealing with 200 token decisions per second. That is far too much for a normal LLM to read naively if it is also supposed to think, reason, and generate.

Figure 3 shows how MiMo-Audio makes that manageable:

The key move is patching. MiMo-Audio groups four consecutive audio frames into one patch, which drops the rate seen by the LLM from 25 Hz to 6.25 Hz. That is the difference between reading every tiny acoustic step and reading short chunks that already summarize a bit of local context.

Each frame still keeps its multi-codebook structure. The model uses separate embedding tables for the RVQ codebooks, sums the embeddings for a frame, and then lets a small bidirectional patch encoder absorb the four-frame block. Only after that does the 7B LLM see the result.

This is an important distinction. The patch encoder is not a second speech model trying to re-solve the whole problem. It is a rate reducer. Its job is to make speech look more like a sequence the LLM can handle, while still preserving the information that makes speech different from text.

The LLM itself is MiMo-7B-Base, so the system starts from a text-capable backbone instead of training a brand-new transformer from scratch. That matters because the paper is not trying to replace language modeling. It is trying to extend it into speech without losing the language side.

The output side is symmetric. When the model needs to generate audio, a patch decoder turns the LLM hidden state back into RVQ tokens. The decoder is causal, because generation has to happen one step at a time. In other words, the patch encoder and patch decoder are the two halves of the speech bridge, with the LLM in the middle.

One next-token rule for text and audio

Here is the simplest way to think about the main objective: MiMo-Audio does not invent separate training rules for speech and text. It trains on one interleaved sequence and asks the model to predict the next item, whatever modality that item belongs to.

Purpose: unify text prediction, speech understanding, and speech generation under one autoregressive rule.

Intuition: once text tokens and audio patches are mixed into a single sequence, the model can just keep predicting the next item from everything that came before it.

Formula:

p(S)=∏i=1Lp(si∣s1,…,si−1)p(S)=\prod_{i=1}^{L}p(s_i \mid s_1,\dots,s_{i-1})p(S)=i=1∏L​p(si​∣s1​,…,si−1​)

Symbols: S is the full interleaved sequence; s_i is the i-th item in that sequence; L is the sequence length; p(·) is the model’s conditional probability for the next item.

Read-aloud meaning: predict the next token or patch from all previous tokens and patches.

Why it matters: the same training rule can handle text tokens, speech patches, and text-audio mixtures without switching objectives.

This one rule is what makes the whole architecture feel unified instead of stitched together. When the next item is text, the model predicts text. When the next item is an audio patch, it predicts audio. The interface changes, but the learning rule does not.

The full model is trained in two stages too

MiMo-Audio’s pretraining schedule is another place where the paper is careful about ordering.

Stage 1: teach the model to understand speech

The first pretraining stage trains the patch encoder and the LLM, but not the patch decoder. The goal is speech understanding. The data mix includes text-only data, speech-text interleaved data, ASR, and audio captioning. The loss is applied only on text tokens in this phase, so the model learns to map speech representations into semantics before it is asked to speak back.

The scale is large: the paper describes 2.6T tokens here, with 1.2T text tokens and 1.4T speech-related tokens. Starting from MiMo-7B-Base helps preserve text ability while the model learns to read the audio side.

This is a good design choice because it isolates the hard part. First the backbone learns, “How do I understand speech?” Only later does it have to ask, “How do I generate it?”

Stage 2: add generation without losing the understanding side

The second pretraining stage turns the whole system on. The patch encoder, LLM, and patch decoder are all trained together on a larger mix of tasks: speech continuation, speech-text interleaving, ASR, TTS, general audio captioning, instruction-following TTS, and text pretraining.

At this point the model is no longer just reading speech. It is learning to complete speech spans too. To keep generation stable, the paper uses a text-guided interleaving strategy with a fixed 5:5 ratio between text and speech patches. That is easiest to picture as giving the model a textual scaffold before it has to paint in the audio. The text keeps the plan anchored; the speech patches fill in the sound.

The loss weights also reflect that structure. Text gets the heaviest weight, and the later codebooks get smaller weights than the earlier ones. Read that as a preference for semantic structure first, finer acoustic detail second. The model should not become obsessed with microscopic waveform refinement before it knows what it is trying to say.

Why the codebooks are delayed

The other technical trick that matters here is delayed codebook decoding.

If the model tries to predict all RVQ layers at exactly the same time, the problem becomes awkward. The deeper codebooks depend on the earlier ones, so simultaneous prediction creates a tough dependency tangle. MiMo-Audio solves that by shifting the codebooks in time. Instead of asking for every layer’s token at the same instant, it predicts a staggered version of the patch.

The intuition is simple: rough draft first, refinement a beat later.

That delay pattern helps the decoder produce cleaner audio, because it gives the model a little more structure before it has to settle the high-frequency detail.

Post-training turns general ability into usable behavior

Once the base model has learned speech understanding and generation at scale, the paper uses post-training to make it useful in real tasks. This stage is less about raw capability and more about behavior.

The training mix now covers audio understanding, speech generation, spoken dialogue, instruction-following TTS, ASR, and text dialogue. The corpus is large, around 100B tokens, and it is curated to activate the right behaviors rather than just add more of the same data.

For spoken dialogue, the authors do something practical: they rewrite text Q&A pairs into more colloquial style, then synthesize them into speech using MiMo-TTS with many different voices and emotions. That keeps the dialogue from collapsing into a single speaker identity or a single speaking style.

The paper also introduces thinking-style data during post-training. The exact mechanism is not presented as a new architectural block; it is more like a supervision style that encourages more deliberate reasoning on some audio understanding and generation tasks. The important point is that the model is being taught not just to answer, but to reason in a way that transfers back into speech tasks.

That said, the paper is careful here: thinking helps speech understanding, but it can also make sound and music reasoning worse. So this is a useful but not fully solved part of the system.

What the evidence says after the mechanism is in place

The interesting part of MiMo-Audio is that the pipeline does not just work in principle. A few anchor results show that each stage seems to matter.

The tokenizer preserves speech quality well, reaching 3.30 PESQ-NB, 2.71 PESQ-WB, and 0.89 SIM on Seed-ZH. That matters because those are exactly the qualities the downstream model needs to keep alive.

The base model also shows that broad speech intelligence can emerge from scale. MiMo-Audio-7B-Base reaches 69.1 on SpeechMMLU S2S and 66.0 on MMAU overall. More strikingly, the paper reports that few-shot speech abilities stay near zero until roughly 0.7T pretraining tokens, then rise sharply. That is the closest thing here to a “GPT-3 moment” for speech.

After post-training, MiMo-Audio-7B-Instruct reaches 74.90 MMAU overall and 72.90/60.20 on Big Bench Audio, and its instruction-following TTS beats GPT-4o-mini-tts overall on both English and Chinese subsets. Those are not just benchmark wins. They are evidence that the same backbone can be steered toward understanding, dialogue, and generation once the pretraining interface is good enough.

What still breaks

The paper is also honest about the remaining gaps:

  • Few-shot in-context learning is still limited on some generation tasks.
  • Spoken dialogue can be unstable in timbre, pronunciation, and prompt adherence.
  • Thinking helps speech understanding, but it can hurt sound and music reasoning.

Those limitations are not accidental side notes. They show where the model still struggles to hold all of speech at once. The paper points toward reinforcement learning as one possible next step for stabilizing dialogue and style control.

What to remember

If you want the mental model in one sentence, it is this: preserve speech detail in a faithful discrete tokenizer, compress that stream again so a language model can read it, train with one next-item objective across text and audio, and scale hard enough that general speech abilities start to appear on their own.

That is why MiMo-Audio is more than a speech codec with an LLM attached. It is a full attempt to make speech a first-class token stream for large-scale pretraining. The paper’s main contribution is not only the strong results, but the architecture of the bet itself: keep the signal, shorten the sequence, and let scale do the rest.
