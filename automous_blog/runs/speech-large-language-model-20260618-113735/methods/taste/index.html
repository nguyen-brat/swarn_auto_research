TASTE: Text-Aligned Speech Tokenization and Embedding

TASTE starts from a simple complaint: speech is too long for the amount of new information it usually adds. Most of the lexical content is already in the transcript, but ordinary speech tokenizers still turn the waveform into a long sequence that duplicates that content and then ask the language model to cope with the mismatch later.

TASTE moves the alignment problem upstream. It uses ASR text as the scaffold, then compresses speech into a sequence that lines up with that transcript. The resulting token keeps the things text does not say well on its own, such as duration, tone, and speaker identity, while letting the text stream determine the sequence length.

That is the mental model to keep in mind: text provides the spine, speech provides the color.

What to notice in Figure 1 is the contrast between the old and new setup. Speech-only tokenizers produce a long acoustic stream, and then joint text-speech models have to interleave, pad, or otherwise hack their way around the mismatch. TASTE instead makes the tokenizer itself obey the transcript length, so the downstream model sees a cleaner interface from the start.

Letting text decide the length

The paper is not trying to replace speech understanding with text understanding. It is trying to separate the two jobs more cleanly.

Text should carry most of the lexical burden. Speech should not waste capacity repeating that same lexical burden in another form. Once you accept that division of labor, the design follows naturally: ask an ASR system for the transcription, then use that transcription to tell the speech encoder where each text position should gather its acoustic evidence.

That is why TASTE feels different from the usual speech tokenizer stack. It is not a fixed downsampling scheme that ignores the transcript and hopes to line up later. It is a transcript-aware compression stage. The sequence length is driven by the text side, not by the raw waveform duration.

Figure 2 is the best place to read the full pipeline left to right. First comes a frozen Whisper encoder, which gives the model robust speech features without asking the tokenizer to relearn basic acoustics from scratch. Then comes the aggregator, which uses transcript tokens as queries and listens across the encoder states until each text position has gathered the speech it needs. Finally, a residual vector quantizer turns the aligned representation into discrete codes, or keeps it as an aligned embedding, depending on which downstream variant you want.

The important thing here is that the aggregator is not building an explicit forced alignment table. It is learning a soft alignment through attention. The transcript says, in effect, “give me the speech for this position,” and the encoder states answer by routing the relevant acoustic evidence to that slot.

How the tokenizer hears speech twice

The tokenizer has three pieces: an encoder, an aggregator, and a quantizer.

The encoder is a frozen Whisper ASR encoder. TASTE does not train it from scratch. Instead, it reuses Whisper as a strong speech feature extractor and keeps two kinds of hidden states. One is the last layer, which is good at capturing alignment cues. The other is a shallower layer from earlier in the encoder, which still preserves a lot of the acoustic detail that later layers tend to abstract away.

That split is the key trick. The last layer helps the model figure out where the words line up, while the shallow layer helps it remember what the speech sounded like.

The aggregator then mixes those two views with cross-attention. The transcript tokens serve as the queries, the final encoder layer serves as the keys, and the shallow encoder layer serves as the values. In plain language, the text asks where the relevant speech lives, the final layer helps answer that question, and the shallow layer supplies the actual material to be compressed.

If you want a compact intuition, think of it like this: the final layer knows the map, but the shallow layer still has the texture.

The paper’s attention visualizations show that this alignment is not hand-coded. Later aggregator layers develop a pretty clear text-speech alignment pattern, while early layers are messier and more mixed.

What matters in Figure 4 is the diagonal feel of the attention. Many heads are already locking onto the right transcript positions, which is exactly what you want from a text-aligned tokenizer. The model is learning to ask, for each text token, “where is the speech evidence for me?”

Figure 5 is the useful foil. The first layer is not yet as cleanly aligned, but some heads already specialize: one tracks clear alignment, another pays attention to silence. That is a nice reminder that the aggregator is not just learning “one head per word.” It is organizing the speech stream into multiple roles, some lexical and some acoustic.

Why the shallow layer helps

The paper does not choose the shallow encoder state by guesswork. Its appendix compares Whisper layers against the target speech units and finds that the earlier-to-middle layers correlate best with the reconstruction target.

Figure 6 explains the design choice very directly. By the time the encoder gets too deep, it has already become more abstract and more transcript-like. That is useful for alignment, but not enough by itself for high-quality reconstruction. The shallower layers still keep the speech texture that the decoder later needs to rebuild a convincing waveform.

So the design is asymmetric on purpose:

  • the last layer helps locate the right text positions,
  • the shallow layer supplies the acoustic detail,
  • the aggregator fuses the two into a text-length representation.

That is a better division of labor than trying to make one encoder layer do everything.

The coarse-to-fine part

Once the aggregator produces a text-aligned continuous representation, TASTE discretizes it with residual vector quantization.

The intuition behind RVQ is easy to remember. The first codebook captures the coarse shape of the representation. Each later codebook only has to explain the leftover residual. That makes the compression process progressively finer without forcing one codebook to carry the whole burden.

In TASTE, the quantizer outputs both a discrete code sequence and a quantized embedding, and both stay aligned to the transcript length. That matters because the downstream spoken language model can choose the interface it prefers: discrete codes when it wants explicit token prediction, or aligned embeddings when it wants a latent path.

The point of quantization here is not to make speech smaller just for the sake of size. It is to keep the aligned token compact enough to model, while still preserving the signal that survives text. If the token became a copy of the transcript, the reconstruction task would collapse. If it became too noisy, the downstream model would lose the useful paralinguistic residue. RVQ gives TASTE a middle ground.

Reconstruction keeps the token honest

TASTE is trained by speech reconstruction, and that choice is doing a lot of work.

The decoder reads the aligned speech representation together with the text tokens and predicts speech units autoregressively. A speaker embedding is also fed in so the system can keep global voice identity under control instead of only reconstructing content. A vocoder then turns those units back into waveform.

This matters because reconstruction is the test that forces the tokenizer to be useful. If the aligned token only carried what text already knows, the decoder would not gain much from it. If it carried timing, prosody, and voice information, the reconstruction gets better. The training objective pushes the token toward that second outcome.

You can think of the decoder as the referee for the tokenizer. The tokenizer is allowed to throw away redundant lexical detail, but it still has to preserve enough acoustic residue for the decoder to rebuild the original speech.

That is also why the method is naturally suited to editing. If the aligned token really tracks the speech residue at the text position, then swapping that token should change only the matching word or phrase, not the whole sentence.

Figure 3 makes the separation of content and paralinguistics very concrete. When two utterances share the same transcript, swapping the aligned tokens changes the timing of only the exchanged words. The untouched words keep their timing. That is exactly what you would hope for if the tokenizer has really learned to put lexical content in text and use the speech token for the extra voice-like details.

When TASTE becomes a spoken language model

Once the tokenizer is text-aligned, a pretrained text LLM can join the system much more naturally.

The paper builds two spoken language modeling variants on top of TASTE. The first predicts the text token and the aligned discrete speech codes together, using separate heads for the residual quantization layers. The second predicts the aligned latent embedding directly. Both variants keep the same high-level promise: the model does not need heuristic interleaving or padding because the speech and text streams already have matching length.

There is also a practical vocabulary issue to solve. The ASR tokenizer and the LLM tokenizer do not have identical token boundaries, so the authors use word-level grouping and averaging to line the aligned speech representation up with the text LLM’s vocabulary. That is a small-looking detail, but it is what makes the bridge between ASR-side alignment and LLM-side generation feel clean instead of brittle.

The adaptation itself stays lightweight. Rather than retraining the whole language model, the authors fine-tune a pretrained text LLM with LoRA. That choice matches the paper’s overall philosophy: keep the language model close to its pretrained strength, and make the speech side fit that interface instead of forcing the LLM to relearn the world.

The result is a model that can continue speech, choose likely continuations, and stay coherent enough to be evaluated on both acoustic and semantic benchmarks. The headline takeaway is not just that TASTE reconstructs speech well. It is that the same aligned representation also makes downstream spoken LM training much less awkward.

What the paper is really saying

The strongest result is conceptual, not just numerical. By aligning speech at the tokenizer stage, TASTE removes a structural mismatch that many joint text-speech models have been fighting downstream.

That design buys several things at once:

  • the token sequence gets dramatically shorter because text decides the length,
  • the token still carries speaker and prosody information,
  • the decoder can reconstruct speech with a clean division between lexical content and acoustic residue,
  • and a text LLM can be adapted without elaborate alignment hacks.

The paper’s experiments back that story up. TASTE reconstructs speech at roughly 150 bps on LibriSpeech while staying competitive with much higher-bitrate tokenizers. The downstream TASLM variants then improve speech continuation and remain strong on the semantic side of spoken benchmark evaluation.

Where the method breaks down

TASTE is elegant, but it is not universal.

  • It depends on reasonably accurate ASR transcripts.
  • It was evaluated on English speech.
  • It is not designed for turn-taking, overlapping speakers, or streaming.
  • Non-lexical events such as laughter or coughing are not the main target.

Those limits are not fatal, but they are real. The whole method works because the transcript is a reliable scaffold. If the scaffold gets noisy, or if the audio contains lots of non-lexical structure that text does not describe well, the alignment advantage weakens.

What to remember

TASTE is easiest to remember as “let text set the scaffold, then attach the speech residue to it.”

That one sentence captures the whole method:

  • ASR gives the transcript,
  • cross-attention aligns speech to that transcript,
  • RVQ compresses the aligned representation,
  • reconstruction keeps the token useful,
  • and LoRA lets a text LLM model the result without extra alignment tricks.

The deeper lesson is that joint speech-text modeling often fails because the modalities are handled at the wrong stage. TASTE fixes that by moving the alignment problem into the tokenizer itself. Once the tokenizer is aligned, the rest of the system gets much simpler.
