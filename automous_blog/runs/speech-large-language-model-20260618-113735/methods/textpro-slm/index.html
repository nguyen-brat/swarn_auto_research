TextPro-SLM

TextPro-SLM starts from a complaint that shows up again and again in speech LLMs: the model can be excellent on text, then lose a surprising amount of that ability as soon as the same request arrives as speech. A lot of recent work tries to fix that from the output side, by making the generation path look more text-like. TextPro-SLM argues that the remaining bottleneck is earlier in the pipeline. The input itself needs to look more like something a text LLM already knows how to read.

The paper’s answer is simple in spirit and surprisingly disciplined in execution: split spoken input into two streams. One stream carries what was said as transcript tokens. The other carries how it was said as compact prosody vectors. Instead of forcing one opaque speech latent to do both jobs, TextPro-SLM keeps the prompt mostly text-shaped and attaches a small side channel for style, emotion, accent, and other paralinguistic cues.

That separation is the whole method. Everything else in the paper is there to protect it.

The architecture in one glance

Here is the central picture to keep in mind:

What to notice is the split. WhisperPro does not just transcribe speech and stop there. It emits synchronized text tokens and token-aligned prosody states. Then the LLM receives a prompt that is almost indistinguishable from a normal text prompt, except for a tiny prosody signal attached to it.

That is the design philosophy in one line: keep the backbone text-compatible, and only add the minimum amount of speech-specific information needed to preserve how the utterance was spoken.

Why WhisperPro starts from ASR instead of from scratch

The first design choice is where the speech encoder should come from. The paper considers three broad routes: build from an ASR model, build from a prosody-rich codec model, or train everything from scratch. It chooses the ASR-first route.

That choice makes sense once you remember what the downstream LLM actually needs. The speech LLM cares most about semantic content. Fine waveform detail is useful only if it helps recover words and the speaking style attached to those words. A strong ASR backbone already spends huge amounts of pretraining on the part the LLM values most: accurate transcription under messy acoustic conditions. Whisper-large-v3 is the obvious starting point because it already has that semantic and robustness advantage.

So WhisperPro is not trying to be a brand-new speech foundation model. It is trying to turn a very strong ASR model into a speech encoder that still remembers prosody.

WhisperPro: keep the transcript and the style aligned

WhisperPro uses the Whisper encoder-decoder stack, but it does something clever with the decoder. The model takes the decoder hidden states from a middle layer, layer 16, and uses them as token-aligned prosody embeddings. Because those states are aligned with the generated transcript tokens, each word gets a partner vector that can carry pitch, energy, emotion, accent, or speaker traits.

That pairing matters more than it sounds. If prosody is stored as one global blob, the model loses the connection between a particular word and the way it was spoken. If prosody is attached token by token, the LLM can reason about the utterance in a much more localized way.

The paper then adds a mel reconstructor on top of those text-plus-prosody pairs. Its job is not to produce speech for the sake of speech. Its job is to make sure the encoder states still remember acoustic detail. In other words, the reconstruction loss is a guardrail: it prevents the prosody embeddings from collapsing into almost-text.

That is the key intuition behind WhisperPro. ASR keeps the content strong. Mel reconstruction keeps the acoustics alive. Together, they make the speech representation usable for an LLM without flattening it into a single opaque vector.

The training data choice follows the same logic. The encoder is trained mainly on LibriSpeech, with extra speech versions of question-answer data added so the model sees spoken QA-style utterances too. That is a practical detail, but it matters: the downstream LLM is not just going to hear clean read speech, so the encoder should not only be good at clean read speech.

Reading speech like text

Once WhisperPro has produced transcript tokens and prosody vectors, TextPro-SLM has to feed them to the LLM without breaking the LLM’s text habits. That is where the input formatting comes in.

The default path is global prepending. The model averages the token-level prosody vectors into one utterance-level summary, projects that summary into the LLM embedding space, and puts it in front of the transcript. So the LLM sees something very close to a standard text prompt, plus one small embedding that says, in effect, “here is how the sentence sounded.”

That is a deceptively strong choice. It keeps the prompt shape familiar, which is exactly what a text LLM wants. The prosody information is still present, but it does not overwhelm the backbone with a long acoustic sequence.

The richer variant is interleaving. Instead of one utterance-level summary, the prosody stream is split into several groups and inserted among chunks of text tokens at a fixed ratio. This is useful if you expect the speaking style to change inside one utterance. But the paper’s result is telling: the global prepend version already works very well, and the more detailed interleaving setup only gives modest extra gains. That suggests the LLM mostly needs a compact hint about style, not a full acoustic replay.

There is also an important tokenization detail here. WhisperPro and the LLM do not share the same tokenizer, so the transcript is tokenized again with the LLM’s tokenizer before being fed downstream. That keeps the final input aligned with the backbone’s own text vocabulary instead of forcing two incompatible token systems to coexist.

Teaching the backbone to keep its language skills

WhisperPro gives the LLM a better speech interface, but the backbone still needs to learn how to use it. TextPro-SLM does that with two kinds of supervision.

The first is knowledge distillation. The paper converts instruction-following and question-answering text data into speech, runs it through WhisperPro, and then uses the transcript to query a teacher text LLM. The student sees the WhisperPro transcript plus prosody and is trained to match the teacher’s response. This is the semantic preservation part of the recipe. It keeps the speech model close to the behavior of the original text model instead of letting it drift away from text intelligence.

The second is paralinguistic supervision. The model is trained on speech captioning and attribute-detection tasks that cover emotion, gender, accent, and age. This is where the prosody stream earns its keep. Captioning teaches the model that speech can carry descriptive cues beyond words, while the classification-style tasks make those cues explicit.

The training schedule is staged on purpose. First the prosody projector is trained while the LLM is frozen. Then the projector and LLM are trained together. That sequencing keeps the bridge stable before the backbone starts adapting. If you let the whole system move too early, the text model can drift before the new speech interface is reliable.

You can think of the whole fine-tuning recipe as a two-part promise:

  • keep the LLM’s text reasoning intact through distillation,
  • teach it to notice speech style through paralinguistic tasks.

TextPro-SLM works because it tries to satisfy both promises at once, rather than trading one for the other.

Why the method works better than a single speech latent

The strongest idea in the paper is not a new loss function or a giant model. It is the decision to keep speech content and speech style separate all the way into the LLM.

That matters because the LLM already knows how to process text. If you hand it an embedded speech blob, you are asking it to relearn text-like structure and acoustic nuance at the same time. TextPro-SLM avoids that trap. The transcript tokens carry the main meaning in a form the LLM already understands. The prosody channel adds just enough extra information to recover how the speech was delivered.

This is why the paper’s default design is so restrained. A single prepended prosody token is often enough. The more elaborate interleaving path is there as a richer option, not as a necessity. In practice, that is a very useful signal: the method is not trying to stuff all of audio into the language model. It is trying to preserve the few speech features the language model actually needs.

What the experiments are really saying

The experimental story follows the architecture very closely.

WhisperPro by itself keeps ASR strong while learning better prosody representations. That means the reconstruction objective is doing its job instead of breaking transcription. The probing results are the interesting part: the prosody vectors become much more informative about emotion, speaker identity, pitch, and energy than the original Whisper states.

At the full-system level, TextPro-SLM is the headline result. It reaches the lowest reported average modality gap among the compared speech LLMs, with the 7B model getting a gap of 0.7. That is far below the main baselines the paper compares against, including SALAD and Qwen2.5-Omni. On reasoning-heavy speech tasks, the gap also stays low, which is important because those tasks punish weak content preservation very quickly.

The paralinguistic side is just as important. TextPro-SLM gets the strongest average paralinguistic score among the compared systems, and the global-prepend version is already very competitive. The interleaving variant helps a bit more on some style-sensitive tasks, but the gains are modest. Again, the lesson is that compact prosody is useful, but the transcript remains the main carrier of meaning.

The ablations make the mechanism hard to ignore. Remove knowledge distillation, and the modality-gap story gets much worse. Remove reconstruction or remove paralinguistic training, and the style side collapses. In other words, both halves of the method matter:

  • WhisperPro needs reconstruction to keep prosody alive.
  • The LLM needs distillation and paralinguistic supervision to use that prosody well.

That is the real evidence for the paper’s thesis.

One more practical result is easy to miss: the method is data-efficient. The LLM adaptation uses roughly 1,000 hours of audio, which is small compared with the scale of many speech systems. That makes the input-side strategy feel less like a research curiosity and more like a plausible way to adapt existing text models without rebuilding the whole stack.

What the paper does not try to solve

The limitations are straightforward and worth keeping in mind. TextPro-SLM does not train or evaluate a speech synthesis module, because the paper is studying the understanding gap on text outputs rather than spoken naturalness. The paralinguistic benchmarks also cover only a slice of the full spoken world. They are a useful proof of concept, not a complete map of human speech cues.

There is also a latency tradeoff. WhisperPro is sequential rather than fully streamable, so the system waits until the utterance is finished before it starts the speech encoding step. The paper treats that as a limitation of the Whisper backbone rather than a rejection of the core idea, and that seems fair. In principle, the same input-side alignment recipe could be paired with a streaming encoder later.

Finally, the method is still speech-specific. Extending the same split representation to non-speech or mixed audio is not automatic, because there may be no clean transcript stream to anchor the content path.

What to remember

If you only keep one sentence from this paper, make it this: if the backbone already knows text, do not force it to ingest speech as one opaque blob. Give it a transcript it can read, then attach a small prosody channel so it can still infer how the words were spoken.

That is TextPro-SLM’s real contribution. It reduces the speech-text modality gap from the input side by making spoken input look like a prosody-aware text prompt, not by turning speech into a giant new latent language.
