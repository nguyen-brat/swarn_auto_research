SD-Eval: A Benchmark Dataset for Multidimensional Evaluation of Spoken Dialogue Understanding and Generation

SD-Eval sits in the evaluation, benchmarks, and post-training data family, but its main job is easy to say: it measures whether a speech model hears more than the transcript. A model can produce fluent text and still miss the emotion in a voice, the accent of the speaker, the age cue, or the background scene. SD-Eval is built to catch that failure.

Figure 1 is the cleanest way to see the paper’s basic assumption.

What to notice is the three-way split. The content layer is the words. The paralinguistic layer is who is speaking and how they are speaking. The environmental layer is the surrounding scene, such as noise or location. SD-Eval says a serious speech benchmark has to preserve all three, not just the words.

Four slices of the same speech problem

The benchmark is not one giant test set. It is four small lenses on the same underlying ability:

  • test-emo checks emotion.
  • test-acc checks accent.
  • test-age checks whether the model reacts appropriately to child versus adult speech.
  • test-env checks whether the model notices the background scene.

Across those four subsets, SD-Eval contains 7,303 utterances and 8.76 hours of speech. The data comes from eight public datasets, which is important because the benchmark is not just synthetic prompt engineering. It starts from real speech where possible, then adds controlled synthetic data where the paper needs cleaner coverage.

The four subsets are shaped differently on purpose. Emotion is built from datasets such as RAVDESS, MEAD, and JL Corpus, where the same content appears with different expressive tones. Accent comes from sources like VCTK and Common Voice, which gives the benchmark a wider spread of speaking varieties. Age uses MyST plus synthetic speech, and environment combines LibriSpeech with AudioCaps and generated dialogue. That mix lets the benchmark test both naturally occurring variation and carefully controlled scenes.

The point is not to build one universal speech challenge. It is to separate four kinds of hidden information so a model cannot hide behind a single score.

Cleaning the test set until the cue is really there

Benchmark construction is mostly a filtering problem. If the labels are inconsistent or the utterance is too ambiguous, then the benchmark stops testing the cue and starts testing the annotator’s luck.

SD-Eval normalizes the labels first. For emotion, it maps to a common set of basic emotions and then drops neutral and surprise, because those cases are too weak or too context-dependent for the paper’s purpose. For accent, it collapses the data into nine representative accent labels. For age, it reduces the task to child versus adult. For environment, it selects seven everyday scenes such as driving, rain or thunder, bells, sports centers, and transit noise.

Then the paper removes samples that are too ambiguous to trust. Figure 2 shows the filtering prompt that starts the process.

The important thing here is not the exact wording of the prompt. It is the workflow. GPT-4-turbo screens the utterances first, then three human annotators check the remaining samples with the same criteria. That two-step filter keeps the benchmark conservative. If humans cannot agree that a cue is really present, the sample should not decide whether a model passed.

The environment split gets an extra cleanup pass because some AudioCaps labels are wrong or too noisy. The emotion split gets another filter that removes utterances whose transcript sentiment already matches the emotion label too closely. That step matters because the paper wants emotion to change the response, not just the wording of the transcript.

There is also a small but useful transcript repair step. Some source datasets do not have punctuation, so the paper runs punctuation restoration before response generation. That sounds minor, but it makes the generated replies read like natural dialogue instead of run-on text.

How the benchmark creates answers to score

Once the inputs are clean, the benchmark needs candidate responses. SD-Eval does not rely on a single brittle gold answer. Instead, GPT-4o generates five diverse responses for each utterance while conditioning on the relevant cue.

Figure 3 shows the prompt pattern for the emotion subset.

What to notice is the instruction discipline. The response should stay grounded in the spoken content, but it also has to reflect the cue in the signal. For emotion, that means the answer should fit the mood. For environment, it should behave as if the background scene was heard. For age and accent, the answer should remain natural and respectful without flattening away the speaker’s style.

The five-response setup is a practical choice. A single reference sentence would make the benchmark too brittle. By generating several plausible replies, the paper gives evaluation a little more room to judge whether a model understood the speech signal instead of just matching one wording choice.

The model ladder the benchmark uses

To test whether the benchmark really measures the right thing, the paper compares several access levels to the same utterance:

That ladder is the heart of the experiment design. The cascade model only sees the transcript, so it can answer the words while ignoring the voice. VS-LLM takes speech directly, so it has a chance to learn paralinguistic and environmental cues from the waveform itself. The upper-bound model gets the perfect transcript plus the correct label, so it shows how much performance is still available if the input side were ideal.

The paper also trains a companion baseline set on a much larger dataset built with the same overall recipe. That training set comes from 11 open datasets and totals 1,052.72 hours and 724.4k utterances. The construction is simpler than SD-Eval: it drops ambiguous examples more aggressively and uses one response per sentence instead of five. The point is to train models at scale without copying the benchmark set itself.

The important distinction is this:

  • Cascade LLM tells you what happens if the model only has text.
  • VS-LLM tells you what happens if the model hears the speech directly.
  • LLM upper bound tells you how much room is left if transcripts and labels are perfect.

That makes SD-Eval more than a dataset. It is a controlled way to measure which part of the speech signal a model is actually using.

Why the judges are part of the method

SD-Eval does not trust one metric. It reports lexical metrics such as BLEU-4 and ROUGE-L, semantic metrics such as METEOR and BERTScore, and reference-free LLM judges. That mix matters because speech dialogue is open-ended. A fluent answer can be perfectly reasonable even when it does not overlap much with a reference sentence.

The LLM judge is the more interesting part. It is asked to score response naturalness, coherence, engagingness, groundedness, and whether the answer actually respects the relevant cue from the audio. Human evaluation on 200 randomly sampled utterances is used to anchor those automatic scores.

The key result is that the LLM judges track humans much better than the overlap metrics. GPT-4o is the strongest judge overall, with 0.613 Spearman and 0.468 Kendall-Tau correlation on the combined evaluation. That is the paper’s real measurement lesson: if the task depends on hidden speech cues, the judge has to see those cues too.

In other words, the evaluation protocol is not an afterthought. It is part of the benchmark design.

What the benchmark reveals

The main model comparison is easy to remember. VS-LLM beats Cascade LLM on every subset, which means direct speech input really does help the model recover information that ASR would throw away. That supports the paper’s main claim: speech carries paralinguistic and environmental information that transcript-only pipelines cannot fully preserve.

At the same time, the label-aware upper bound still does best overall. That is not a contradiction. It is a reminder that speech-side models are still losing information somewhere between the waveform and the response. Better speech understanding helps, but clean transcripts and labels still give the most leverage.

The off-the-shelf audio models are useful as a reality check. They are not terrible, but they do not solve the benchmark cleanly either. The message is not that generic audio pretraining is useless. The message is that cue-sensitive speech dialogue is a harder problem than plain speech recognition or audio tagging.

What to remember

SD-Eval is easiest to remember as a microscope for hidden cues in speech. It collects real and synthetic speech, normalizes the labels, filters ambiguous samples, generates multiple plausible responses, and then scores those responses with both humans and LLM judges that can see beyond n-gram overlap.

Its limits are clear too. The benchmark is still speech-to-text, not speech-to-speech. It is still single-turn, not multi-turn. And it does not yet cover every speaker attribute, such as gender. Those are real gaps, but they are also the right next questions.

If the transcript is all you score, you are not really evaluating speech.
