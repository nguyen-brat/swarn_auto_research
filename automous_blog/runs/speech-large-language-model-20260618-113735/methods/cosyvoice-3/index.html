CosyVoice 3

CosyVoice 3 is a speech synthesis system that starts from a simple but important bet: if the representation layer is weak, no amount of decoder cleverness will fully rescue the output. Earlier CosyVoice systems already sounded strong on cleaner Chinese and English speech, but they still struggled once the input became messy, multilingual, or style-rich. Rare words, dialects, special text formats, and expressive prompts all exposed the same bottleneck. The paper’s response is to improve the speech tokenizer first, then post-train the generator on token-level rewards, and finally scale the data and model size so the system can operate in the wild rather than only in broadcast-style conditions.

That puts the method squarely in the Speech tokenization, generation, and foundation stacks family. The family is about a shared idea: if speech is going to support voice cloning, instruction following, and multilingual generation, the token layer has to carry more than just coarse content.

The token layer is where control is won or lost

It is tempting to think the decoder is the main event in a TTS system. CosyVoice 3 argues the opposite. What matters most is what survives the journey from waveform to discrete codes. If the tokenizer discards pronunciation clues, prosody, language identity, or speaker cues, the downstream generator cannot invent them later.

That is the paper’s central lesson. Better speech tokens plus more diverse training usually buy more than simply making the decoder heavier. The model needs tokens that keep control signals intact, then a generator and post-training recipe that know how to use those tokens.

What to notice in the figure is the separation of labor. The speech tokenizer is not a side module glued onto the end of the system. It is trained as a speech-understanding model with multiple supervision sources, then its output flows into the LM and conditional flow matching stack. The dashed components are training-only helpers, which is a good reminder that the final inference path is simpler than the training recipe.

The tokenizer is built on MinMo rather than an older ASR-only encoder. That choice matters because MinMo already brings a broader speech understanding prior, including multilingual recognition and emotion-sensitive features. CosyVoice 3 then inserts finite scalar quantization, or FSQ, into the voice encoder so the model turns rich continuous representations into discrete speech tokens without flattening away too much useful information.

The supervision is also broader than plain ASR. The tokenizer is trained on a multi-task mix that includes:

  • multilingual ASR
  • language identification
  • speech emotion recognition
  • audio event detection
  • speaker analysis

That list is the clue to how the tokenizer is supposed to behave. It is not only learning to recognize words. It is learning to keep track of language, style, speaker identity, and background audio structure. Those are exactly the signals that a zero-shot TTS system needs later if it wants to imitate real speech instead of sounding like a generic voice.

Why the tokenizer is trained like a speech-understanding model

The FSQ block is the paper’s most important representation trick. In plain language, the voice encoder compresses speech into a lower-dimensional space, rounds each dimension into a bounded discrete value, and then maps that quantized vector to a token ID. That gives the system compact, discrete speech units without asking the tokenizer to become a brittle one-hot codebook.

This matters for two reasons. First, the tokens need to be short enough that the language model can work with them. Second, the tokens need to preserve the pieces of speech that later synthesis depends on. A tokenizer that is good for recognition is not automatically good for generation, so CosyVoice 3 explicitly optimizes both sides.

The paper’s ablation makes that point concrete. FSQ-MinMo keeps recognition strong while also improving downstream TTS compared with earlier tokenizers. In other words, the tokenizer is not just compressing speech. It is deciding how much content, style, and control survive into the generator.

The token rate is 25 Hz, which is a useful way to think about the compression level. That is dense enough to preserve speech structure, but still compact enough for sequence modeling. If the rate were much lower, the generator would lose detail. If it were much higher, the language model would be swamped by sequence length.

Post-training the tokens, not the waveform

The second major idea is DiffRO, CosyVoice 3’s differentiable reward optimization method. The motivation is practical. Classic reinforcement learning over full synthesized audio is expensive, because the downstream CFM and vocoder stack is heavy. It is also awkward, because once the pipeline produces audio, positive and negative samples can sound very similar, which makes reward modeling harder than it looks.

CosyVoice 3 avoids that by optimizing the discrete speech tokens directly.

The basic loop is:

  1. sample candidate speech tokens with Gumbel-Softmax so gradients can still flow;
  2. score those tokens with an ASR-like Token2Text reward model;
  3. use the reward to push the generator toward tokens that preserve the intended text;
  4. keep the policy close to the reference model with a KL penalty.

That is the real shift. Instead of asking a reward model to judge completed waveforms, the system rewards the representation that actually controls the waveform. The reward model is not just looking for fluency. It is checking whether the generated tokens still carry the text content clearly enough for an ASR-style model to recover the target transcription.

CosyVoice 3 does not stop there. It also uses multi-task reward signals, adding tasks such as speech emotion recognition, MOS prediction, and audio event detection. This broadens the reward from “did you say the right words?” to “did you say them with the right style, quality, and acoustic character?” That is why the paper can talk about following instructions for voice attributes rather than only transcription accuracy.

The tradeoff is important. Reward optimization improves content consistency substantially, but it can slightly reduce speaker similarity if the reward starts dominating the representation. The paper treats that as a real failure mode, not a side note. Reward tuning in speech is still a balancing act.

Repairing pronunciation before the model has a chance to guess

If the tokenizer is the representation layer, pronunciation inpainting is the first repair tool. Standard BPE-style text tokenizers are bad at ambiguous spellings, especially when a character can be pronounced in more than one way or when a rare word is not well covered by the training data. CosyVoice 3 responds by teaching the model to read mixed sequences of words and phonemes.

The auxiliary data construction is simple but effective. For Chinese, monophonic characters are replaced with pinyin in selected examples. For English, monophonic words are replaced with phonemes from the CMU pronunciation dictionary. That gives the model more opportunities to see the same semantic content paired with a more explicit pronunciation cue.

What this buys you is controllability. The model can keep ordinary text when spelling is enough, but it also learns to accept phoneme hints when spelling is not reliable. That is exactly what an industry TTS system needs for rare words, names, and other pronunciation traps.

Text normalization is turned into a learning problem

The paper uses the same trick for text normalization. Instead of relying only on hand-written rules to turn numbers, symbols, and odd formatting into spoken forms, CosyVoice 3 builds an auxiliary set that lets the model learn from raw text and normalized text pairs.

There are three sources for that auxiliary data:

  • rule-based text normalization followed by synthesis
  • LLM-based text normalization from Qwen-Max followed by synthesis
  • inverse text normalization on existing text-audio pairs to recover raw text

That third path is especially useful. It turns existing speech-text pairs into examples where the model sees the kind of raw input that users actually type. The result is a more end-to-end TTS system that can handle messy punctuation, special symbols, and text formats without depending entirely on brittle preprocessing.

The important intuition is that normalization is not just a front-end cleanup step. It is part of the speech problem itself, because the model has to know how to pronounce the text that real users hand it.

Instruction-following only works if the training data covers real styles

CosyVoice 3 also expands the instruction-following side of the system. The expressive speech data grows from 1,500 hours to 5,000 hours, and the number of speaking styles goes past 100. That includes emotions, speed changes, voice tones, dialects, accents, and role-playing styles.

The paper supports both natural-language instructions and fine-grained control tags. A prompt can include a spoken-style description plus an end token, or it can use markers such as [laughter], [breath], and <strong>XXX</strong> to push specific acoustic behaviors.

This part of the paper is easy to misunderstand if you only read it as a style catalogue. It is really about making instruction-following speech plausible. The more varied the training speech is, the more likely the model is to understand that “say it happily,” “whisper this part,” or “speak with emphasis” should map to consistent acoustic behavior rather than random variation.

The data pipeline is what makes the scale believable

The model is not trained on a neat studio-only corpus. It is scaled up to one million hours, with audio collected from audiobooks, videos, podcasts, and other in-the-wild sources. That is why the paper spends time on the multilingual preprocessing pipeline. Without it, the larger scale would mostly just add noise.

The pipeline has six stages:

  1. speech detection and segmentation
  2. noise reduction
  3. ASR transcription
  4. punctuation adjustment
  5. volume standardization
  6. filtering out abnormal audio-text length ratios

Each stage removes a different kind of failure. Segmentation keeps utterances from becoming too long. Noise reduction trims broken starts and ends. Cross-checked ASR transcription keeps only the more reliable labels. Punctuation adjustment makes the text better match the pauses in the speech. Volume normalization keeps the audio level stable. The final ratio filter catches weird alignments such as very short audio with a very long transcription, or the reverse.

The multilingual mix matters too. The paper is not just adding a few extra languages on top of English and Chinese. It broadens into nine languages and eighteen Chinese dialects, and the distribution is intentionally uneven rather than pretending every language has the same amount of data.

What to notice in the figure is that multilingual scaling is a coverage problem, not just a count problem. The point is not to maximize one language’s volume. The point is to expose the tokenizer and generator to enough linguistic diversity that cross-lingual cloning and dialect handling stop being rare exceptions.

Bigger back end, simpler plumbing

CosyVoice 3 does not only scale the data. It also scales the model. The text-to-speech LM grows from 0.5B to 1.5B parameters, and the CFM model grows from 100M to 300M parameters. The CFM backbone is changed to a diffusion transformer, which is strong enough that the paper can remove the more complicated text encoder and length regularization module used before.

That simplification is worth pausing on. Bigger models often add more moving parts. CosyVoice 3 goes the other way: once the backbone is strong enough, the system can rely on a cleaner interpolation step to handle the speech-token and Mel-frame rate mismatch. The architecture gets larger where capacity matters and smaller where extra machinery is no longer earning its keep.

The result is a better fit for the token-first strategy. The tokenizer learns better speech codes, the language model gets more capacity to reason over them, and the flow matching decoder gets a stronger generative backbone to turn those codes back into speech.

What the evaluations are actually telling you

The headline numbers are useful because they line up with the story above. On the SEED-TTS evaluation sets, CosyVoice 3-1.5BRL reaches 0.71% CER on Chinese and 1.45% WER on English, which is the best among the compared models in those settings. DiffRO also reduces WER by roughly 20% to 50% across multilingual and cross-lingual voice cloning tasks, especially in the harder low-resource cases.

The ablations make the same point from a different angle:

  • FSQ-MinMo keeps recognition strong while improving downstream TTS, so the tokenizer choice is not cosmetic.
  • DiffRO consistently improves content fidelity, but it can slightly reduce speaker similarity when the reward starts dominating.
  • The larger 1.5B model helps, but it does not always dominate the smaller one on the hardest set because the training data is still not large enough to fully exploit the extra capacity.

That last nuance matters. CosyVoice 3 is clearly a scale-sensitive system, but it is not a “bigger is always better” story. The gains depend on both the data and the token quality being good enough to feed the larger backbone.

What still breaks

The paper is straightforward about the limitations. CosyVoice 3 cannot directly control timbre from text instructions yet, which matters if you want fine-grained role-play or explicit voice editing. Singing generation is still weak. Rare words and some dialects still degrade.

Those limitations are not incidental. They point back to the same core constraint that drives the whole paper: the model is only as controllable as the token layer feeding it. Once a desired property is only loosely represented in the tokens, the decoder has very little chance of recovering it later.

So the remaining open question is not just how to make the decoder larger. It is how to make the representation layer carry timbre, style, pronunciation, and language identity with enough fidelity that later stages can act on them cleanly.
