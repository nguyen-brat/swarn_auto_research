LLaMA-Omni 2

LLaMA-Omni 2 is trying to solve a very practical speech problem: how do you make a spoken assistant that can understand a user, reason with a strong language model, and start speaking back quickly enough that the interaction still feels live? The paper’s answer is modular, not monolithic. Keep a capable text LLM intact, add a speech encoder in front of it, and add a streaming speech decoder behind it. That way the model keeps the reasoning power of Qwen2.5 while speech is handled as a separate, chunked pathway.

That is why it belongs in the real-time speech-to-speech and duplex agents family. The whole family is about the same interface problem: preserve meaning, keep latency low, and avoid the brittle handoffs of an old ASR -> LLM -> TTS cascade.

The whole system in one picture

Figure 1 is the clearest map of the method.

What to notice in the figure is the separation of labor. Speech does not go straight into the language model as raw audio, and speech does not come out of the model as a monolithic waveform prediction. Instead, the stack is split into four narrow jobs:

  1. a speech encoder and adapter turn the input audio into a manageable sequence,
  2. the Qwen2.5 LLM reasons over that sequence and produces text plus hidden states,
  3. a streaming TTS decoder turns the text-side signal into speech tokens,
  4. a chunk-aware flow matching model and vocoder turn those tokens into waveform audio.

That structure is the paper’s main idea in miniature: keep the language model smart, keep the speech side streamable, and let the glue between them do just enough work to preserve alignment.

Speech in: compress the acoustic stream before the LLM sees it

The input side starts with Whisper-large-v3. That choice is important because the model is not asking the LLM to learn speech perception from scratch. Whisper already knows how to turn speech into a useful representation, and the speech adapter only has to make that representation compact enough for the LLM to handle.

The adapter does two simple things. First, it downsamples the sequence by a factor of 5, which shortens the acoustic stream a lot. Second, it runs a feed-forward network over the downsampled features so they fit the LLM’s embedding space. In plain language, this is a compression step. The model keeps the important evidence from speech, but it stops treating every audio frame as a separate language-model event.

That compression matters because speech is long. If the LLM had to consume raw frame-level audio, the context window would disappear quickly and the model would spend too much effort just staying synchronized. The adapter turns speech into something closer to a clean note-taking system: enough detail to preserve meaning, short enough to remain usable.

Speech out: start from a pretrained text decoder, then teach it to write speech tokens

The output side is where the paper gets most interesting. Instead of training a speech decoder from scratch, LLaMA-Omni 2 starts the text-to-speech language model from Qwen2.5-0.5B and extends its vocabulary so it can emit discrete speech tokens as well as text tokens.

That design choice says a lot about the method. The TTS decoder is not a random generator that happens to produce audio. It is already a language model that knows how to continue sequences. The new job is to make the continuation happen in speech-token space.

The paper uses a pretrained speech tokenizer from CosyVoice 2 to turn the target speech into discrete units at 25 tokens per second. Once the speech side is tokenized, the TTS language model can learn the same kind of next-token prediction it already knows from text, just with a much larger and more speech-shaped vocabulary.

The gate is the bridge between context and exact wording

The TTS decoder does not rely on hidden states alone. It also receives the exact text tokens generated by the LLM. That gives it two complementary views of the same response:

  • hidden states carry contextual information,
  • text embeddings carry exact lexical content.

The gate fusion module combines those two views instead of choosing only one. That is the key trick in the paper, because speech generation wants both context and lexical precision.

The fused vectors are then fed into the TTS language model, which autoregressively generates speech tokens. The read/write schedule is what makes the process streamable. For every R fused representations that are read, the model writes W speech tokens. Once the LLM has produced enough text context, the TTS side can already start emitting audio tokens instead of waiting for the entire answer.

That is the practical meaning of the chunked design. It is not full-utterance speech generation with an end-of-sequence pause. It is speech generation that can start while the answer is still being formed.

After the speech tokens are produced, the chunk-aware causal flow matching model converts each chunk into a mel spectrogram, and HiFi-GAN turns the mel chunk into waveform audio. So the final output is not just text with a voice on top. The system really streams speech in the middle of the answer.

Why the model is trained in two stages

The two-stage training plan is there to avoid a common failure mode in modular speech systems: if everything is trained at once, the speech stack can become unstable before the language model has a chance to settle.

Stage I trains the two halves separately.

  • In Stage I(a), the speech encoder is frozen, while the speech adapter and the LLM are trained on speech-to-text pairs.
  • In Stage I(b), the TTS language model is trained on text-to-speech pairs.

The important detail is that the gate fusion module is not trained yet in this stage. The TTS decoder only sees text embeddings, which means it first learns a clean speech-generation interface before it has to juggle hidden states and embeddings together.

Stage II then teaches the speech-to-speech path. The speech encoder, speech adapter, and LLM are frozen, and only the gate fusion module plus the TTS language model are updated. This is the point where the model learns how to combine the contextual hidden state and the exact text token stream while staying faithful to the text response.

That order is sensible. First make speech understanding work. Then make speech generation work. Only after both halves are stable do you ask them to cooperate on end-to-end speech-to-speech dialogue.

Where the 200K dialogues come from

The model is trained on 200K synthetic multi-turn speech-to-speech dialogues, not on a giant bespoke ASR or TTS corpus. That choice is central to the paper’s argument. The authors are trying to show that a strong modular SpeechLM does not need millions of hours of speech pretraining if the training data is carefully shaped around the interaction problem.

The text side of the data starts from InstructS2S-200K, which itself rewrites instruction-following data into speech-oriented dialogue. To expand that into multi-turn conversations, the paper samples the number of turns from a Poisson distribution, clips it to the range 1 to 5, and then uses Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct to generate the dialogue turn by turn from the previous history.

The speech side is synthetic too, but it is synthetic in a way that matches the use case. The instruction side gets varied voices, while the response side is kept to a uniform voice. The paper first synthesizes a short random-voice prompt with fish-speech-1.5, then uses that prompt with CosyVoice2-0.5B to clone the voice for the instruction stream. For responses, it uses a fixed voice prompt.

That setup does two useful things at once:

  • it teaches the model to handle speaker variation on the input side,
  • it keeps the output voice stable, which makes alignment and evaluation easier.

In other words, the dataset is not just bigger. It is shaped to match the exact contract the model has to satisfy at inference time: listen to many voices, answer in one coherent voice, and preserve turn context across a dialogue.

What inference feels like

At inference time, the model behaves like a streaming spoken assistant rather than a batch generator. The LLM first autoregressively produces the text response from the speech instruction. After it has generated R text tokens, their hidden states and the decoded text are fed into the gate fusion module and the TTS language model. The TTS side then writes W speech tokens, which are converted to mel and waveform as a chunk.

That means the first audible response is a sum of several waits: the time for the LLM to produce the first text chunk, the time for the TTS model to write the first speech chunk, the time for flow matching to synthesize the mel chunk, and the time for the vocoder to render the waveform. The user does not wait for the whole answer to finish before hearing anything, but there is still a real chunk-sized delay before the first audio arrives.

The paper reports roughly 600 ms for that first chunk on the main 7B setup. That is not instant, but it is fast enough to feel interactive, which is exactly the boundary this paper is trying to hit.

What the ablations are really teaching

The ablations line up with the architecture story instead of telling a different one.

  • Removing gate fusion hurts both alignment and quality. The spoken answer becomes less faithful to the text when the hidden state and the text embedding are no longer mixed adaptively.
  • Training the TTS side from scratch is a disaster. The model loses almost all useful speech-generation behavior, which is the strongest argument in the paper for starting from a pretrained TTS decoder.
  • The read/write ratio is a real tradeoff, not a cosmetic knob. Smaller chunks lower latency, larger chunks can improve speech quality, and the main setting R=3, W=10 is the paper’s compromise point.
  • More data helps, but multi-turn structure helps too. The 200K multi-turn setup beats smaller training sets and also beats the single-turn version at the same scale.

The headline numbers make that point concrete without turning the chapter into a benchmark dump. On the 7B model, the paper reports 60.7% on Web Questions in S2S, a 4.15 ChatGPT score, 3.26 ASR-WER, 4.19 UTMOS, and 582.91 ms latency. The important part is not the exact leaderboard position. It is that the speech-to-speech path stays close to the text-side performance instead of falling apart.

That is also where the modular recipe earns its keep. Earlier systems either paid more latency or lost too much naturalness. LLaMA-Omni 2 is trying to hold both sides together: keep the response coherent, keep the speech aligned, and keep the first chunk fast enough for a live conversation.

What the paper admits it still cannot do

The method is strong, but it is not magic. The paper is explicit about three limitations.

First, it cannot yet control emotion, speech rate, or dialect directly from the input speech. The training data simply does not cover enough style variation for that to be a solved problem.

Second, the system depends on several pretrained modules. That keeps the model efficient to train, but it also makes the stack more complex to reproduce and maintain.

Third, the first chunk is still delayed by about 600 ms. That is still real-time, but it leaves room for improvement if future work can shorten the chunking loop without sacrificing quality.

What to remember

The durable lesson is simple: let the language model do the thinking, let the speech side do the streaming, and use a gate to mix hidden-state context with exact text before the audio decoder starts writing tokens.
