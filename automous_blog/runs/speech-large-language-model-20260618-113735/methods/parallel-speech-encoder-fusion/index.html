Fusing Multiple Speech Encoders for Speech-Aware LLMs

This paper is about a very practical idea: if two speech encoders already know different things about the same utterance, why force the language model to choose just one of them? Instead, keep both encoders in parallel, align their outputs to the same time grid, fuse them with a small learned block, and hand the result to the LLM as a single speech prefix.

That sounds simple, but the design choice matters. The paper is not trying to lengthen the audio sequence or build a giant new encoder stack. It is trying to preserve the LLM context budget while making better use of complementary acoustic knowledge. One encoder may be stronger on a language, another on speaker variation, another on difficult acoustics. The main question is how to combine those strengths without making the audio stream longer and harder for the LLM to read.

Why two encoders can be better than one

The paper starts from a familiar ASR pattern: one speech encoder is often good, but not equally good everywhere. A multilingual encoder may handle broad coverage, while a monolingual or domain-specialized encoder may do better on a specific language or acoustic setting. Their mistakes are often not identical. That is the opening for fusion.

The authors use this to motivate three kinds of combinations:

  • monolingual ASR, where a general encoder and a language-specific encoder can complement each other
  • multilingual ASR, where the model should keep the strengths of both languages instead of collapsing toward one
  • diarized ASR, where a speech encoder and a speaker encoder carry different kinds of evidence

That last case is important because it shows the method is not just about two speech encoders that both produce transcripts. It is more general: one stream can capture content, another can capture speaker identity, and fusion can still happen in the same framework.

What to notice in the figure is the placement of the fusion block. The two encoders stay separate until they have been brought to the same temporal resolution. Only then are they combined into one stream for the projector and the LLM. That is the whole design philosophy in one picture: parallel feature extractors, one fused handoff.

The first thing the paper gets right: the time grid

Before any fusion can happen, both encoders need to speak the same temporal language. Speech encoders often have different frame rates or different output lengths, so the paper first downsampled or stacked the features until the sequence lengths matched.

This step matters more than it looks. If the two streams are not aligned frame by frame, then a gate or attention layer does not know whether it is combining the same instant of speech or two different moments. The paper avoids that problem by making sure both encoder outputs have the same length T.

The result is a pair of sequences:

  • E1^t from encoder 1
  • E2^t from encoder 2

for each time step t = 1 ... T.

The paper keeps the fused output at the same length too. That is the big constraint that makes the method LLM-friendly. The fusion block is allowed to reorganize information, but not to inflate the number of speech vectors. That way the LLM context window does not get eaten by a longer audio prefix.

The simplest option: just concatenate

The baseline is plain concatenation. At each frame, the two feature vectors are put side by side and passed forward as one wider vector.

That is easy to implement and easy to understand. It gives the projector more raw information to work with, but it does not decide which encoder should matter more at a given moment. In other words, it is static. Every frame gets the same kind of combination rule, whether the utterance is clean Dutch, noisy English, or diarized multi-speaker speech.

That static behavior is why concatenation is a useful baseline but not the most expressive idea in the paper. It can preserve information, but it cannot adapt much to the input.

A better idea: learn to weight the streams

The next family of methods makes the fusion block more selective. Instead of just stacking features together, the model learns how much each encoder should contribute.

Sigmoid gate

The sigmoid gate is the most direct version of this. Each stream is first projected into a common inner space. Then the model predicts a gate value for each frame and uses that gate to blend the two streams.

The intuition is easy to remember: when encoder 1 is more reliable for the current frame, let it speak louder; when encoder 2 is better, turn it up instead. The gate acts like a soft switch between the two acoustic views.

This is a strong fit when one encoder often dominates but not always. In the paper, that turns out to be especially useful in the English setup, where a relatively simple gate works best.

Multi-head gate

The multi-head gate keeps the same basic idea, but lets several attention heads make their own mixing decisions. Instead of one scalar gate doing all the work, multiple heads can specialize in different aspects of the input.

This matters because the useful encoder may change from one utterance to the next, or even from one frame to the next. Different heads can learn different preferences: one may lean toward the multilingual encoder, another toward the monolingual encoder, and another toward speaker-related cues.

The paper finds this especially helpful in the multilingual setting. That makes sense: when the system has to stay good on two languages at once, a single fixed rule is less likely to be enough.

When fusion needs more context

The gating methods decide locally, frame by frame. The Transformer-based methods go a step further and let the fusion block reason over a larger context.

Positional Transformer

In the positional Transformer, the encoder features are concatenated at each position, reduced to a shared inner dimension, and then processed by a Transformer encoder.

The intuition is that some fusion decisions are not really local. If the model can see a wider span of frames, it may infer whether it is dealing with a certain language, domain, or speaking style, and then use that broader context to weight the encoders better.

This is a more expensive way to fuse, but still small compared with the LLM itself. The point is not to build a giant second encoder. The point is to give the fusion block enough context to make smarter mixing decisions.

Temporal Transformer

The temporal Transformer is the most interesting fusion block in the paper. Instead of combining the encoders in the feature dimension first, it interleaves their projected features in time and lets a Transformer process the resulting sequence. After that, pooling brings the length back down to the original T.

That design has two nice properties. First, the Transformer sees the two streams as a sequence of alternating acoustic views, which makes the interaction explicit. Second, the final pooling step restores the original length, so the LLM still receives one fused vector per frame.

The authors note that interleaving works better than simply placing all of encoder 1 before all of encoder 2. That is a useful detail because it tells you the fusion block is not just looking for broad summaries. It benefits from seeing the two encoders side by side at each moment.

This method ends up being the strongest option in the Dutch and diarized settings. That is a useful pattern to remember: when the task benefits from richer temporal interaction, the temporal Transformer seems to capture complementary cues best.

How the fused sequence reaches the LLM

Once the fusion block has produced one aligned sequence, the rest of the stack is fairly conventional. The fused features go through a small projector and are mapped into the LLM embedding space. The prompt then combines those projected speech features with the text instruction for ASR.

The paper keeps this downstream part intentionally lightweight:

  • the projector is a 2-layer MLP
  • the LLM is fine-tuned with QLoRA
  • most of the heavy lifting happens in the fusion layer and the projector

That design choice is important because it keeps the experiment focused. The paper is not asking whether a larger LLM can brute-force its way around bad audio features. It is asking whether better audio fusion makes the LLM’s job easier.

Why the sequence length stays fixed

This is the most important systems constraint in the paper.

If you fuse two encoders by concatenating them in time, the LLM has to read a longer prefix. That can quickly become expensive, especially if you want to combine more than two encoders. The paper avoids that by keeping the sequence length fixed throughout the fusion stage.

That constraint is what makes the method practical. The fusion block can be smarter than concatenation, but it must not make the audio prefix longer. The paper’s message is basically: combine more information, not more tokens.

What changes across tasks

One of the most useful lessons in the paper is that there is no universal best fusion block.

The Dutch setup favors the temporal Transformer, which suggests that the model benefits from richer interaction between the two speech encoders. The English setup is more favorable to the simpler sigmoid gate, which suggests that when both encoders are already quite strong, the task may not need a complex fusion mechanism. In the multilingual setup, multi-head gating does best, which fits the idea that different heads can specialize across languages. In diarized ASR, the Transformer-based methods are strongest again, which makes sense because speaker cues often need longer-range context.

That variation is not a weakness of the paper. It is actually the lesson. Fusion is not a one-size-fits-all trick. The right block depends on what kind of complementarity the encoders are providing.

The optional second stage: feed decoder hypotheses too

The paper also tries a second-stage refinement where the LLM is given not only speech features but also initial text hypotheses from the decoder side of the speech models.

This is a different kind of fusion. The core encoder-fusion block still does the acoustic combination, but now the LLM also sees text predictions that can act as hints. The idea is simple: if the ASR systems already have a decent guess, the LLM can use that guess as extra context and clean it up.

That works well, especially in Dutch. But the tradeoff is obvious: the system now has extra offline compute because it has to run the decoders too. So this is a good trick when accuracy matters more than latency. It is not the lightweight core of the method, but it is a useful add-on.

What the method is really buying you

This paper is not arguing that bigger or more complicated fusion is always better. It is arguing something narrower and more useful: when two pretrained encoders have genuinely complementary strengths, a small learned fusion block can do better than naive concatenation while still keeping the LLM context fixed.

That is why the method is appealing. It gives you a way to reuse existing speech encoders instead of replacing them, and it does so without turning the audio prefix into a sequence-length problem. In practice, that means you can combine multilingual and monolingual knowledge, or speech and speaker cues, with relatively little extra machinery.

The limitations are also clear. The paper studies short-form ASR, not streaming or long-form speech. The best fusion block changes with the setting, so there is no universal recipe. And decoder-hypothesis conditioning improves accuracy at the cost of extra offline work.

What to remember

The memorable version of this method is:

two encoders listen in parallel, their outputs are aligned to the same frame rate, a small fusion block mixes them into one sequence, and the LLM reads that fused sequence without paying a longer context cost.

That is the real contribution here. The paper shows that speech encoders are not just alternatives to each other. They can also be collaborators, and the right place to combine them is before the LLM ever sees the audio.
