Covo-Audio Technical Report

Covo-Audio is trying to solve a very specific problem that keeps showing up in speech assistants: how do you preserve the richness of speech without forcing the whole system through a brittle ASR-to-LLM-to-TTS relay? If you convert everything to text too early, you lose timing, emotion, and voice. If you keep everything in audio form, the model becomes hard to train and hard to control. Covo-Audio takes a middle path. It keeps continuous audio on the input side, discrete speech tokens on the output side, and lets one 7B backbone learn how to move between speech, text, dialogue, and audio reasoning.

The paper’s bigger claim is even more practical than that. A useful voice assistant should not only understand and answer, it should also be able to change voices without rebuilding the conversational brain from scratch. Covo-Audio tackles that with a shared audio-text latent space and a decoupling trick that separates dialogue intelligence from speaker style.

The cleanest way to picture the system is as a single backbone wrapped by a few carefully chosen speech interfaces:

That pipeline is the core of the paper. The rest of the method is about teaching the model how to use it well.

One backbone, two kinds of speech representation

The architecture starts from Qwen2.5-7B-Base, then adds three speech-specific pieces. A Whisper-large-v3 encoder reads the incoming audio. An adapter compresses those features so the backbone does not drown in frame rate. And a WavLM-large-based speech tokenizer turns speech into discrete audio tokens for generation. On the output side, the model does not stop at tokens. A flow-matching decoder first turns the discrete tokens into richer acoustic latents, and BigVGAN turns those latents into waveform audio.

What to notice in the figure is that Covo-Audio is not a text model with a speech addon. The same backbone reads continuous acoustic features, text tokens, and speech tokens inside one unified sequence. That is what makes the later stages possible. The model can learn to understand speech, answer in text, answer in speech, and even mix the two without switching architectures.

There are two different speech representations in play, and they have different jobs.

  • Continuous audio features are the rich input signal. They preserve the fine-grained timing and acoustic detail that speech understanding needs.
  • Discrete speech tokens are the compact output signal. They make speech generation manageable for an autoregressive backbone.

That split is important. The model does not try to make the language backbone solve waveform synthesis directly. It keeps the backbone focused on sequence modeling and delegates the very last step of audio realism to the speech decoder.

The adapter is also doing real work, not just bookkeeping. The encoder runs at 50 Hz, and the adapter reduces that to 6.25 Hz before the backbone sees it. That compression is what keeps the audio prefix from exploding in length. For multi-turn use, the paper also prefixes the current input with previous user audio and the model’s own earlier tokens, so the conversation history stays inside the same sequence instead of being thrown away between turns.

Why hierarchical tri-modal interleaving matters

The paper’s most distinctive pretraining idea is the tri-modal interleaving scheme. The model is trained to move among three kinds of symbols at once: continuous acoustic features, discrete speech tokens, and plain text. That sounds abstract, but the motivation is concrete. Speech is not just “audio that should become text.” It is also a structured signal with prosody and long-form flow. If you only interleave at the word or character level, the model can become brittle. A single tokenization hiccup or alignment error can throw off the whole sequence.

Covo-Audio therefore interleaves at two scales. Phrase-level interleaving keeps the local mapping between an acoustic chunk and the text that explains it. Sentence-level interleaving preserves the global meaning and the prosodic arc of longer utterances. The point is to give the backbone both local precision and long-range coherence.

In practical terms, the model learns patterns like:

  • speech chunk, then text, then discrete speech continuation
  • speech chunk paired with text and discrete speech targets

That is the “tri-modal” part. The model is not choosing between audio and text. It is learning how they fit together inside one generation stream.

This is also why the paper prefers the interleaved design over a Thinker-Talker style split. The whole point is to keep the speech side native, not to push everything through an explicit intermediate text reasoning step before audio generation.

Pretraining first teaches alignment, then teaches the model to mix tasks

The pretraining recipe is staged deliberately. Stage 1 freezes both the audio encoder and the backbone, and only trains the adapter on a large ASR corpus. That gives the adapter a chance to find the right semantic landing zone without disturbing the language model too early. In plain language, the model first learns how to hear in a way the backbone can understand.

Stage 2 unfreezes the adapter and the backbone together and broadens the task mix. At that point the model is no longer just learning a bridge. It is learning how to use speech and text interchangeably across multiple jobs:

  • ASR, where speech becomes text
  • TTS, where text becomes discrete speech tokens
  • audio-only modeling, where the model learns speech continuity
  • speech continuation, where it continues a spoken stream
  • speech-text interleaving, where speech and text appear in the same training sequence
  • text-only modeling, which keeps the language side from drifting
  • full-duplex pretraining, which prepares the model for live listening and speaking

That final text-only component is easy to overlook, but it matters. The model is not allowed to become “speech smart” at the cost of forgetting general language ability. The pretraining mix keeps the backbone grounded in language while teaching it to move between modalities.

The result is a backbone that does not just map audio to text. It starts to behave like a shared reasoning engine for speech and language.

From text intelligence to spoken conversation

After pretraining, the paper turns the backbone into Covo-Audio-Chat, the dialogue-oriented variant. This is where the method becomes more than alignment and starts to look like a usable assistant.

The post-training mix is organized around five goals. General intelligence gets the biggest share, spoken dialogue is next, and then smaller but still important slices are reserved for speech understanding, speech generation, and audio understanding. That balance is intentional. The model should stay smart first, then learn how to speak naturally, then learn how to react to audio-rich tasks.

The text-side instruction data is the anchor. The paper starts from a large internal instruction corpus, then converts a subset into speech with high-quality TTS so the same knowledge can be trained in text-to-text, text-to-audio, audio-to-text, and audio-to-audio form. That is the real transfer mechanism: the assistant learns reasoning in text, then mirrors that competence into speech.

The dialogue data is then rewritten to sound conversational instead of stiff. That helps the model produce spoken replies that are shorter, more natural, and less like written essays read aloud. For empathy, the authors add emotion-aware dialogues across several emotional categories, with the assistant trained to respond supportively and contextually. In other words, the model is not just asked to get the content right. It is also asked to sound like a decent conversation partner.

There is one more practical ingredient here: the paper uses a stronger text LLM as a distillation guide so the spoken model does not drift away from the underlying knowledge and reasoning ability. That keeps the speech variant from becoming a polished but shallow voice layer.

Why voice and intelligence are separated

The paper’s voice customization trick is easy to miss, but it is one of the most useful parts of the whole system. The problem is simple: genuine high-quality spoken dialogue data is scarce, while high-quality TTS data is much easier to collect. The catch is that TTS recordings are usually not shaped like dialogue, so they are awkward to use for training a chat model directly.

Covo-Audio handles that mismatch by decoupling speaker style from dialogue intelligence. Instead of forcing every speaker to carry the full burden of conversational competence, the training data is reformatted so that the model can learn the voice separately from the conversational content.

What to notice in the figure is the separation of jobs. The dialogue model learns what to say. The TTS side supplies the voice. The two are connected, but they are not fused so tightly that changing one destroys the other.

The recipe has two parts. First, the authors generate many speakers so that speaker identity is not tied too strongly to conversational intelligence. Second, they convert TTS recordings into pseudo-conversations and mask the text response portion from the loss. That way, the model can borrow a voice style from TTS data without being forced to relearn dialogue in a way that depends on the voice data itself.

That is a surprisingly elegant solution to a practical deployment problem. It means you can reuse high-quality voices without rebuilding the whole conversational model. The Covo-Audio-Chat-TTS variant shows the point clearly: the transferred TTS voice can be used in dialogue while keeping most of the chat performance intact.

Full duplex is a control loop, not just another decoder

The full-duplex variant, Covo-Audio-Chat-FD, pushes the system into real-time interaction. This is a different problem from ordinary spoken dialogue. Here the model has to listen and speak at the same time, react to barge-ins, handle backchannels, and decide when a pause is just a pause versus when it means the user has finished.

What to notice in the figure is that the paper does not treat full duplex as an afterthought. It changes the stream format itself. The audio encoder becomes chunk-streaming, and the user stream and model stream are interleaved in a 1:4 ratio so the model can process incoming audio while it is still producing output audio.

The control tokens matter here:

  • THINK means the model is listening and not yet speaking.
  • SHIFT moves it from listening to speaking once the user turn is done.
  • BREAK stops the speech turn when an interruption or an end-of-turn signal appears.

During the speaking phase, each chunk is guided by a text token followed by several speech tokens. That text anchor helps keep the chunk semantically stable while the speech stream stays fluid. The user stream is still coming in at the same time, which is what makes barge-ins and backchanneling possible.

The important design choice is that the paper trains this full-duplex behavior directly from the pretrained foundation, using a mix of half-duplex and full-duplex data, rather than building it through a long progressive pipeline. That keeps the recipe simpler and, according to the paper, more robust than a heavier staged alternative.

The main tradeoff is also clear. Full duplex improves interaction, but it can still react a little too early on short pauses. That is a control problem, not a language problem, and the paper is honest that pause sensitivity remains one of the sharpest edges.

The same backbone also learns speech understanding

Once the model can move between speech and text, the paper uses that same backbone for speech understanding tasks. The guiding idea is to train the model not only on what was said, but also on who said it and how it was said.

The authors split speech understanding into two parts. Content understanding covers tasks like ASR, speech-to-text translation, and spoken language identification. Attribute recognition covers things like gender, speaker count, and emotion. That split is useful because it keeps the method honest about what speech carries. Some tasks depend on lexical content, while others depend on paralinguistic cues that never appear in a transcript.

The training data is built as question-answer pairs over speech corpora. The same underlying speech example is reused in several forms:

  • text-to-text QA, where the source speech is paired with a text question and text answer
  • speech-to-text QA, where the query is spoken and the answer is textual
  • speech-to-speech QA, where both the query and the answer are spoken

That construction teaches the model to recover the same underlying attribute whether the query arrives as text or speech. It also helps the model avoid becoming too dependent on a single modality.

The paper also takes bias seriously. Because attribute datasets are uneven, it uses stratified sampling to keep the demographic balance as even as possible, with a priority order that tries to handle the hardest-to-balance attributes first. That is not glamorous, but it is the kind of thing that matters if the model is meant to be used in real conversations.

Audio understanding needs a second pass of reasoning

Speech understanding is not the same as audio understanding. In the audio branch, the model is asked to reason about sounds, music, and spoken content in ways that go beyond transcription. The paper treats this as a two-step process.

First, it trains on foundational audio perception data so the model learns the acoustic vocabulary of events, sounds, and cross-modal alignment. Then it adds reasoning-focused data so the model can explain its answers instead of only guessing them. That second stage matters because many audio tasks are not solved by pattern matching alone. Emotion cues can be subtle, cause-and-effect in audio scenes can be indirect, and the correct answer can depend on a small reasoning chain.

The supervised reasoning data is formatted so the model separates its thought process from its final answer. After that, the paper uses GRPO to polish the reasoning behavior on a benchmark-style task. The reward is a sum of four parts:

Purpose: make audio reasoning correct, well formatted, internally consistent, and actually thoughtful.

Intuition: a model that gets the answer right but writes a messy or inconsistent explanation is still not behaving like a reliable reasoner. The reward has to care about all of those pieces, not just one.

Formula:

Rtotal=Raccuracy+Rformat+Rconsistency+RthinkingR_{\text{total}} = R_{\text{accuracy}} + R_{\text{format}} + R_{\text{consistency}} + R_{\text{thinking}}Rtotal​=Raccuracy​+Rformat​+Rconsistency​+Rthinking​

Symbols: R_{\text{total}} is the total reward. R_{\text{accuracy}} rewards the final answer matching the ground truth. R_{\text{format}} rewards the required <think>...<answer>... structure. R_{\text{consistency}} rewards the reasoning and final answer agreeing with each other. R_{\text{thinking}} rewards the depth and clarity of the reasoning itself. R_{\text{thinking}} is scored on a bounded scale, while the other three components are simple reward checks.

Read-aloud meaning: score the model for being correct, using the right output format, keeping its reasoning aligned with the answer, and thinking clearly.

Why it matters: this is the paper’s way of teaching the model that audio reasoning is not only about recognition accuracy. The model has to explain itself in a way that is both usable and trustworthy.

The format reward is just a structure check, not a semantic judge. The consistency reward uses an auxiliary LLM judge. The thinking-quality reward scores logical soundness, factual correctness, error identification, language coherence, and conciseness. That combination is what keeps the reasoning stage from collapsing into a plain answer classifier.

What the paper actually proves

The experiments are broad enough to tell a coherent story without needing to drown the chapter in benchmark tables. The pretrained foundation shows strong speech-text alignment. The dialogue variant is good at instruction following, conversational flow, and empathy. The full-duplex variant keeps much of that reasoning ability while adding turn-taking, pause handling, backchanneling, and interruption control. And the same backbone can also handle speech understanding and audio understanding at a competitive 7B scale.

The more interesting point is the tradeoff structure. Covo-Audio is not magic. It is data-heavy and training-heavy, and the full-duplex version still has some early-response behavior on short pauses. Some knowledge and reasoning benchmarks also trail the strongest proprietary systems. But the paper does show that a 7B model can cover a much wider slice of speech intelligence than a plain cascaded stack usually can.

What to remember

The paper’s main idea is easiest to hold in one sentence: teach one backbone to live in a shared audio-text space, then separate voice style from conversational intelligence so the model can be reused, customized, and streamed more easily.

If you want the short mental model, keep this sequence in mind:

continuous speech in -> shared latent alignment -> unified reasoning backbone -> text or speech out -> optional voice rendering

That is what Covo-Audio adds to the speech LLM landscape. It does not just make speech possible inside a language model. It makes speech, dialogue, empathy, and low-latency interaction feel like different views of one system.
