On Decoder-Only Architecture For Speech-to-Text and Large Language Model Integration

Speech-LLaMA is a bridge recipe, not a new speech tokenizer. The paper is asking a practical question: if speech is too long and too awkward for a text LLM to read directly, can we shorten it first, map the shortened audio into the LLM’s own embedding space, and then let the language model finish the job? The answer here is yes. A pretrained CTC compressor trims the speech stream, a small audio encoder turns the compressed frames into LLaMA-shaped vectors, and the LLM decodes text from a task prompt plus audio prefix.

The second half of the paper tests a different idea: if we remove the pretrained text model entirely, can a smaller decoder-only network still learn speech-to-text from paired data alone? That baseline matters because it checks whether the decoder-only idea is just a transfer trick, or whether it is actually a viable speech architecture.

Start with the bottleneck

Speech and text are mismatched in a way that matters for modeling. Speech comes as a long, redundant acoustic sequence. Text LLMs expect a much shorter sequence of token-sized embeddings, and then they generate one token at a time. If you force the language model to swallow raw audio frame by frame, you either blow up the context length or ask the model to learn a brutal compression problem from scratch.

That is why this paper sits inside the broader speech-to-text bridges and alignment family. The real problem is not “how do we add audio?” It is “how do we get audio into the LLM without destroying the information the decoder needs?”

The main pipeline, read left to right

The core architecture is easiest to understand as a pipeline. The first diagram shows the main path from speech to text.

What to notice in the figure is the order of operations. The model does not hand the LLM a giant speech tensor and hope for the best. It first compresses the speech, then translates that compressed audio into the same semantic space used by the text model, and only then lets the LLM decode.

This is the whole trick in one line. The speech encoder side is doing two jobs at once: reducing length and preserving the content that matters for translation. The LLM side stays mostly in its comfort zone. It reads a prefix, conditions on it, and continues generation as if the speech had already been turned into a usable prompt.

The paper also keeps the control signal explicit. Training examples are prefixed with task-style instructions such as “translate the audio into English” or “audio -> English.” That is not cosmetic. It tells the model what kind of continuation to produce and keeps the audio from being treated as a vague condition with no task boundary.

Why the CTC compressor comes first

The first important design choice is compression. The paper uses a pretrained CTC module to shorten the acoustic sequence before the audio encoder sees it. That matters because the compressor is not there to solve translation by itself. It is there to remove redundancy while keeping the speech signal legible enough for the bridge.

The paper compares two ways to do that:

  • blank-removal: drop frames that the CTC model predicts as blank.
  • frame-averaging: keep runs of frames that belong to the same CTC class and average their hidden states.

Both methods try to turn a long speech stream into a shorter, denser summary. The difference is how aggressively they throw information away. Blank-removal is the blunt instrument: fast, simple, and easy to understand, but it can erase frames that still carry useful evidence. Frame-averaging is smoother. It preserves the local run of evidence and is more robust when the compressor is not perfectly certain about where the useful signal lives.

That is exactly what the experiments show. Frame-averaging beats blank-removal by a healthy margin, which is a good reminder that compression is not just a memory trick. It is part of the representation design.

The paper also makes an interesting choice about how the compressor is trained. It is pretrained on source-language transcription with the CTC objective, then frozen. In the authors’ setup, that works better than training the compressor directly for speech translation. The intuition is simple: before the model learns to translate, it should first learn how to keep the speech content stable while shortening the sequence.

The bridge into LLaMA space

Once the speech has been shortened, a small audio encoder maps the compressed frames into the continuous semantic space that LLaMA expects. This is where the method becomes more than a compression recipe.

The encoder is intentionally small and starts from random weights. It is not trying to become a second speech model. Its job is to translate the compressed acoustics into something the LLM can read like a prefix. That is a subtle but important distinction. The paper is not converting speech into discrete tokens and then asking the LLM to decode those tokens. It is feeding the LLM continuous embeddings that already live in the same vector world as text prompts and token embeddings.

That choice makes the interface feel more like a prompt than a separate modality. The model receives a task instruction, then a run of audio embeddings, and then it continues generating text. In other words, speech is treated as a long prefix that has to be shortened and semantically aligned before the language model can continue from it.

Why the attention mask is not a small implementation detail

The paper also checks how the prefix should be attended to inside LLaMA. A standard causal mask only lets each position look backward. That is natural for text generation, but the prefix here is not a streaming transcript. It is a block of compressed speech plus an instruction prompt.

So the authors test a non-causal prefix mask, where the prompt and audio prefix can attend to the full prefix context before generation starts. That matters because the prefix is supposed to summarize all the acoustic evidence, not just the immediately preceding frame.

This choice consistently helps. You can think of it as giving the bridge a little more room to organize the compressed audio before the decoder starts producing words. The benefit is especially clear when compression is harsher, because any lost detail is easier to recover if the prefix representation can look across the whole block.

LoRA comes after the bridge is already working

The paper does not fine-tune the entire LLM from the start. It uses a two-stage recipe.

First, it trains the audio encoder while keeping the CTC compressor and LLaMA frozen. That stage teaches the bridge to land in the right semantic neighborhood without disturbing the pretrained language model too early. Then it adds LoRA adapters to the attention matrices in LLaMA and continues training.

That staging is the right instinct for this kind of system. If the bridge is still unstable, fully opening the backbone can make training noisy and expensive. LoRA gives the model just enough extra freedom to adapt while keeping the parameter count small.

The paper applies LoRA to the attention matrices in each transformer layer and keeps the update set compact. The result is a meaningful gain for a very small parameter budget. In practical terms, the bridge becomes better at using the LLM without turning the whole model into a full retraining job.

A second architecture tests the idea from scratch

The second figure is important because it strips away the pretrained text backbone and asks whether decoder-only speech modeling still makes sense on its own.

Here the setup is simpler and more revealing. The text prompt, the audio encoder, and the CTC compressor are replaced by a randomly initialized convolutional encoder and a much smaller autoregressive decoder. An SOS token marks where text generation begins. After that point, the model predicts the transcript purely from the audio prefix and its own previous text outputs.

What this buys you conceptually is a cleaner test of the architecture. If the model works here, then the decoder-only idea is not just relying on a giant pretrained text model to carry the whole system. It can learn a speech-conditioned generation process from paired speech-text data alone.

The result is surprisingly close to the seq2seq baseline, which is exactly why this branch of the paper matters. It suggests that decoder-only speech models are not just a convenience for LLM adaptation. They can be a real speech-to-text architecture in their own right.

What the experiments say once the mechanism is in place

The numbers are there to confirm the mechanism, not to replace it. The full Speech-LLaMA stack substantially outperforms a strong seq2seq baseline on the 13-language speech-to-English task. A shallow LLaMA rescoring trick helps a little, but the deeper bridge helps much more.

Three comparisons are especially informative:

  • Frame-averaging beats blank-removal, which means the compressor should preserve local evidence instead of deleting too much too early.
  • Non-causal prefix attention helps, which means the prefix benefits from seeing the full compressed speech context.
  • LoRA adds another bump with only a small parameter increase, which means the backbone can be adapted efficiently once the bridge is stable.

The scratch decoder-only model is the other anchor result. It lands only slightly behind the seq2seq baseline, despite being much smaller and trained from scratch. That is a strong sign that decoder-only speech modeling is not a dead end.

Where the method stops

The paper is honest about its limits.

  • The main evaluation is speech translation to English, not open-ended speech dialogue.
  • The method still depends on paired speech-text data and a task prompt.
  • Compression quality and prefix masking matter a lot; if either one is weak, translation quality drops.

There are also natural next questions. Would the bridge scale cleanly to more diverse speech tasks? Would stronger source-language supervision make the compressor better? How far can decoder-only speech models close the gap as they get larger?

What to remember

The durable idea here is simple: shorten speech before the language model sees it, translate that shortened audio into the LLM’s own embedding space, and keep the adaptation layer small. Speech-LLaMA is useful because it makes that recipe concrete. It shows that you can get strong speech-to-text behavior without turning the whole system into a heavy multimodal redesign, and it also shows that decoder-only speech models can be competitive even when trained from scratch.
