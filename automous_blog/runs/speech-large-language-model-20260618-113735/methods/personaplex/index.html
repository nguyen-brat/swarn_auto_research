PersonaPlex

PersonaPlex sits in the Real-time speech-to-speech and duplex agents family, but its main contribution is narrower and more useful than “a better voice chatbot.” The paper asks a practical question: can a duplex speech model keep its live, low-latency conversation loop while also being told who to be and what voice to use? Most systems can do one of those things. They can sound lively, or they can imitate a voice, or they can follow a role prompt. PersonaPlex is trying to make those controls coexist inside one streaming model.

The answer is a hybrid system prompt on top of a Moshi-style duplex backbone. Text sets the speaking role. Audio supplies the target voice. The model then keeps streaming text and speech together during the live conversation instead of collapsing everything into a single fixed assistant persona.

What to notice in the figure is the split of responsibility. The user audio is the live input stream. The agent text stream carries the role-conditioned response plan. The agent audio stream carries the voice identity and the spoken answer. PersonaPlex is not asking one channel to represent behavior, identity, and timing all at once. It separates those jobs, then lets the duplex backbone keep the conversation moving.

Two prompts, two jobs

The hybrid prompt is the heart of the method. It is easier to understand if you think of it as a short boot sequence before the live dialogue begins.

The first segment is the text prompt. Here, the model sees a role description on the text channel while the audio channel stays silent. This is where PersonaPlex learns “what kind of agent am I?” A customer-service role, a QA tutor, or some other speaking persona can be written directly into the prompt without touching the voice.

The second segment is the voice prompt. Now the model gets the opposite pattern: a short speech sample on the audio channel while the text channel is padded. This is where PersonaPlex learns “what do I sound like?” Because the voice prompt is separate from the role prompt, the model can clone a target voice without confusing that voice with the role itself. That is the zero-shot part: after a short voice sample, the conversation can continue in that voice even if the model has never been trained on that exact speaker.

That separation is the key idea. In ordinary speech systems, role and voice often blur together because they are both pushed through one conditioning path. PersonaPlex treats them as different controls. Text shapes behavior. Audio shapes timbre and prosody. The duplex backbone then uses both while it streams the live interaction.

The paper notes that the order of the two prompt segments does not change performance. In the implementation, the voice prompt comes first so the model can prefill efficiently at inference time when zero-shot cloning is not needed. That is a nice example of the method’s practical bias: keep the conceptual split clean, then order the prompt in whatever way makes deployment cheaper.

There is one more quiet but important detail. During this prompt phase, the user-audio stream is replaced by a neutral 440 Hz sine wave, and custom delimiters mark the boundary between the setup prompt and the real conversation. That keeps the channel layout stable while the model is being primed. During training, the system prompt itself is masked out of the loss, so the model is not rewarded for reproducing the setup. The loss on padded text tokens and non-semantic audio tokens is also down-weighted, which keeps the scaffold from drowning out the real learning signal.

Why the data has to teach the split

A hybrid prompt only works if the training data repeatedly reinforces the same separation. PersonaPlex therefore trains on large synthetic dialogs where role, voice, and turn-taking are paired on purpose.

One data stream is service-style conversation. The paper starts by sampling a domain, then a scenario, then a higher-level description, and finally expands that into a full two-speaker transcript. A separate role context is generated for the service agent, so the model sees both the conversation and the job description that should govern it. That matters because the evaluation benchmark later asks the model to survive exactly these kinds of structured service settings.

The other stream is question-answering dialogue. Here the paper uses a fixed role, something like a wise and friendly teacher, and synthesizes short Q&A exchanges across different topics and follow-up patterns. This gives the model a cleaner setting where the text role is stable and the main variation comes from the conversation itself.

The speech side is built the same way. The paper uses a large pool of single-speaker voice samples to generate speech prompts and dialog audio. For service dialogs, it uses a multispeaker TTS model that can generate both sides of the exchange while preserving timing, interruptions, and room tone better than a simple stitched pipeline. For question answering, it uses a single-speaker TTS model and then stitches the turns together, adding silence when the conversation should feel normal and even compressing the gap when it wants to simulate barge-in or interruption.

That last trick is easy to miss, but it explains why the model feels more conversational than a plain speech synthesizer. It is not only learning what to say. It is learning when speech should continue, overlap, stop, or yield. In a duplex setting, those timing cues are part of the task, not a post-processing detail.

Why the benchmark had to change

The authors are explicit that the usual duplex benchmark is too narrow for this question. Full-Duplex-Bench mainly tests one assistant role, which is fine if you only want to know whether the model can talk. It is not enough if you want to know whether the model can stay in a specific role while still sounding like a chosen speaker.

So PersonaPlex extends the benchmark into Service-Duplex-Bench. The extension adds 350 customer-service questions on top of the original 400 questions in Full-Duplex-Bench, organized as 50 service scenarios with 7 questions each. Each question is a single-turn probe. That choice is deliberate: it isolates role adherence instead of mixing in long-horizon memory.

The questions are designed to stress the exact behaviors that a role-conditioned voice agent needs to keep:

  • proper noun recall,
  • context adherence,
  • handling unfulfillable requests,
  • staying calm under customer rudeness,
  • ignoring unrelated requests when they do not belong in the role.

The example in the paper is a good illustration. The user asks an insurance agent for account details, eligibility questions, impossible enrollment requests, rude complaints, and off-topic service requests. The model has to remain helpful without wandering away from the service role. That is a much sharper test of conditioning than a generic assistant benchmark.

What the numbers are really telling you

The strongest result is not just that PersonaPlex sounds good. It is that the hybrid split actually survives evaluation.

On Full-Duplex-Bench, PersonaPlex reaches a DMOS of 3.90, ahead of Gemini at 3.72 and Qwen-2.5-Omni at 3.70. On Service-Duplex-Bench, it reaches 3.59, again ahead of Gemini at 3.22 and Qwen-2.5-Omni at 2.37. Those are human naturalness scores, so they are telling you that the model still feels conversational even after you give it extra role control.

The speaker-similarity result is even more revealing. PersonaPlex reaches 0.57 on Full-Duplex-Bench, while Moshi is at 0.10. That gap says the voice prompt is not a cosmetic attachment. It is actually steering the output toward the target voice in a way the baseline duplex model does not.

On the service benchmark judged by GPT-4o, PersonaPlex reaches a mean score of 4.48. That is comfortably above Freeze-Omni and Moshi, though still below Gemini on this specific judge. The important point is not that PersonaPlex wins every scoreboard. It is that it stays competitive on service role behavior while preserving the voice-control gain that the paper is really after.

The tradeoff to keep in mind

The method is powerful, but it is not free.

First, the training pipeline depends heavily on synthetic dialogs and synthetic speech generation. That makes the method scalable, but it also means the model inherits whatever blind spots the synthetic pipeline creates.

Second, the evaluation focus is intentionally domain-shaped. Service-Duplex-Bench is useful because customer service is one of the most obvious places where role and voice matter. It does not prove that the same prompt split will cover every speaking role, every accent, or every tool-using agent scenario.

Third, the released checkpoint is not exactly the same as the experimental setup described in the main paper. The appendix says the released model adds real conversational data and a different synthetic voice recipe, which is good for quality but important to remember when comparing results.

Those caveats do not weaken the main lesson. They just remind you where the method is strongest: when the task needs a live duplex conversation, a specific speaking role, and a target voice at the same time.

What to remember

PersonaPlex is easiest to remember as a clean separation of control. Text tells the model how to behave. Audio tells it how to sound. The Moshi-based duplex backbone keeps both streams alive during the conversation so the agent can still listen, speak, and handle interruptions in real time.

If you want the one-sentence version, it is this: PersonaPlex makes role conditioning and voice cloning coexist by giving them different prompt channels instead of forcing one prompt to do both jobs.
