Mel-LLM

Mel-LLM is a very direct experiment: what happens if you delete the dedicated speech encoder and ask the language model to read normalized log-Mel features itself? Most speech-LLMs begin with a big acoustic front end that turns audio into vectors the LLM can handle. Mel-LLM trims that stack down to something much thinner. It keeps just enough preprocessing to make the audio compact, projects the result straight into the LLM, and lets the backbone learn the speech-text alignment on its own.

That makes it a clean fit for the speech-to-text bridges and alignment recipes family. The family is about the handoff between audio and text; Mel-LLM pushes that handoff to an extreme by removing almost everything except the bridge itself.

The interesting part is that the paper does not just test ASR. It uses the same backbone idea in the other direction too: the LLM can also generate speech through a VAE-style mel head. So the paper is really asking a broader question about interface design: how much of speech understanding and speech generation can one large transformer absorb if the input is shaped well enough?

Start with the smallest possible speech front end

The input side is intentionally simple. The raw waveform is first converted into 80-dimensional log-Mel features. Then those features are mean-variance normalized using training-set statistics so the model sees a consistent scale instead of a different loudness profile for every speaker and recording condition. If the sequence is still too long, the paper keeps a lightweight convolutional downsampler, but it removes the heavy Transformer or Conformer speech encoder blocks that usually sit in front of the LLM.

That last step is the whole point. Instead of asking a separate speech encoder to manufacture semantically rich audio features, the paper asks a single learned projection to do the minimum necessary conversion and then trusts the LLM to finish the job.

The tiny bridge in one line

Purpose: replace the speech encoder with one learned affine bridge.

Intuition: once the Mel patches are normalized and shortened, the LLM mainly needs a way to see them in its own embedding space.

Formula:

Es=Wproje+bprojE^{s}=W_{\text{proj}}e+b_{\text{proj}}Es=Wproj​e+bproj​

Symbols: E^{s} is the speech embedding sequence, W_{\text{proj}} is the learned projection matrix, e is a normalized Mel feature patch, and b_{\text{proj}} is the projection bias.

Read-aloud meaning: take a Mel patch, apply one learned linear layer, and hand the result to the language model.

Why it matters: this is the core encoder-free trick. The speech front end collapses from a large encoder stack into a single affine map.

Once those projected speech embeddings are ready, they are concatenated with the text prompt embeddings and fed into the LLM. The model then predicts transcript tokens autoregressively, just as a text-only decoder would continue a prompt.

The nice thing about this setup is that the bridge is not trying to solve every speech problem at once. It only has to preserve the information ASR really needs: time order, phonetic content, and enough language cues to produce the right transcript. Raw waveform detail, speaker style, and very fine timing can mostly stay outside the bridge.

Let the backbone learn the alignment

The ASR path only works if the LLM can internalize the missing encoder role. That is why the paper adapts the backbone with LoRA instead of full fine-tuning. The model is still mostly the same pretrained transformer; the LoRA layers simply give it enough flexibility to learn how speech patches should be interpreted.

The most important intuition is that the LLM does not learn speech encoding all at once. The lower layers start acting like a speech parser, while the upper layers keep doing the language-side work they already know how to do. The layer-freezing ablation makes this visible. Freezing only the upper LoRA layers barely hurts until the freeze reaches layer 24 and above, which suggests those upper layers already hold useful high-level language behavior. Once the freeze expands deeper into the stack, the model starts losing the ability to convert raw spectral patterns into something text-like.

That is a useful mental model:

  • lower layers learn to listen,
  • upper layers keep the language model fluent,
  • the bridge between them is just a projection and some light downsampling.

The paper’s results support that picture. With Phi-4-MM initialization, the encoder-free model reaches 7.12% average WER on OpenASR, only a small step behind the random-encoder baseline that still uses a trainable speech encoder. Random initialization works less well, and the gap becomes much more obvious when data is scarce. But with roughly 10x more in-house data, the encoder-free average WER improves from 13.93% to 12.99%, and the gap to the encoder-initialized model narrows sharply.

That is the real tradeoff in the paper. The encoder-free design is simpler and faster, but it is not magic. If the data is limited, a multimodal prior like Phi-4-MM matters a lot. If the data is abundant, the LLM can absorb more of the acoustic work itself.

Why the token rate matters more than it looks

The optional convolutional downsampler is not a side detail. It controls how many Mel patches the LLM has to read, which directly affects both speed and quality. The paper explores several token rates, and the sweet spot is 12.5 Hz for ASR. Higher rates such as 50 Hz or 100 Hz can be a bit more accurate, but they make the sequence much longer. Lower rates such as 6.25 Hz are faster, but they throw away too much detail.

The useful middle ground is 12.5 Hz because it matches the frame rate that Phi-4-MM already knew during pretraining. That makes the adaptation easier, and it gives the model a strong speed-quality balance: a 1.57x training speedup over the encoder-based baseline without a dramatic accuracy drop.

So the downsampler is doing two jobs at once:

  • shortening the sequence so the LLM can afford to read it,
  • keeping enough temporal structure that speech does not collapse into mush.

For ASR, that balance is exactly what the model needs.

The same backbone can also speak

Mel-LLM is not only an ASR story. The paper also uses the LLM to generate speech, again without a dedicated speech encoder. The output side is more preliminary, but the workflow is clear. Given text input, the LLM produces hidden states at speech positions. Those states are decoded into mel frames by a VAE-style mel head, then refined by a residual postnet, and finally turned into waveform audio by a HiFi-GAN vocoder.

This is a nice symmetry with the ASR path. On input, the model turns speech into LLM-shaped embeddings. On output, it turns LLM hidden states back into speech-shaped mel frames. The backbone is doing both jobs, which is what makes the architecture feel unified instead of like two separate systems bolted together.

The TTS head itself is deliberately conservative. At each speech position, the LLM state is mapped into a latent variable, sampled in VAE style, pushed through a small residual MLP, and then cleaned up by a 5-layer Conv1D postnet. A stop head predicts when the audio should end, and the training objective mixes reconstruction, KL regularization, stop prediction, and a flux loss that encourages smoother frame-to-frame trajectories.

The practical lesson is easy to read off the experiments:

  • random initialization can converge, but the output is inaudible,
  • Phi-4-MM initialization is needed to get usable speech,
  • dropping dropout too low makes autoregressive generation fall apart,
  • the TTS results are still preliminary rather than state of the art.

That last point matters. The paper is not claiming that encoder-free TTS is solved. It is showing that the shared backbone idea is viable, but the acoustic side still needs more work.

What this paper is really teaching

Mel-LLM is a paper about how much architecture you can remove before speech-LLM training stops working. Its answer is encouraging: if the Mel features are normalized, optionally downsampled, and projected into the right space, a large multimodal LLM can learn a surprising amount of speech encoding internally. The model does not need a separate speech encoder to become useful for ASR.

At the same time, the paper is honest about where the idea is fragile. Low-data training still benefits a lot from multimodal initialization. TTS is only a first pass. And the ASR and TTS experiments are trained separately instead of jointly, so the full promise of a shared encoder-free backbone is still open.

That is the main idea to carry forward from Mel-LLM.
