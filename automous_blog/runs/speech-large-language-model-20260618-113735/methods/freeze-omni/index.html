Freeze-Omni

Freeze-Omni sits in the Real-time speech-to-speech and duplex agents family, but its most important idea is narrower and cleaner than a general speech assistant recipe. The paper asks a practical question: if the text LLM is already strong, why keep retraining it for speech at all? Instead, Freeze-Omni freezes the LLM and learns the speech bridges around it. One bridge turns streaming audio into something the LLM can read. Another bridge turns the LLM’s output back into speech. A small controller decides when the user is still speaking and when the model should answer.

That choice changes the whole training story. Rather than collecting a huge spoken Q&A corpus and hoping a fine-tuned backbone does not forget how to reason, Freeze-Omni leans on ASR data, text-speech pairs, and a modest amount of multi-round question answering. The speech modules do the modality work. The LLM keeps doing the thinking.

The paper’s architecture figure makes that loop concrete.

What to notice is where the LLM sits. It is in the middle, not at either edge. Speech is compressed before it reaches the backbone, and the answer is expanded after the backbone finishes its reasoning. That is the whole philosophy of Freeze-Omni in one view: preserve the language model, surround it with modality-specific bridges.

Why freezing the LLM matters

Most speech-to-speech systems get expensive because they try to teach the backbone everything at once. That works when you have enormous spoken-dialogue data, but the paper is explicit about the downside: fine-tuning the backbone invites catastrophic forgetting, and the data requirement for spoken Q&A is hard to meet.

Freeze-Omni takes the opposite stance. Keep the backbone fixed, then teach the speech side how to land in the LLM’s space and how to read from it. In the experiments, the authors use Qwen2-7B-Instruct as the text backbone, but the recipe is meant to be backbone-agnostic. If a text LLM already knows how to follow prompts, role-play, and reason, the speech stack should adapt to that LLM instead of rewriting it.

That split also makes the system easier to think about. The input side is a comprehension problem. The output side is a generation problem. Duplex dialogue is a control problem. Freeze-Omni gives each one its own training signal instead of forcing one monolithic model to solve everything at once.

Teaching speech to become text

The input side is built around a chunk-wise streaming speech encoder and a small adapter.

The encoder uses downsampling convolution layers and Transformer blocks to turn incoming audio into chunk-wise features. The adapter then downsamples again so the LLM sees a shorter, more manageable sequence. This downsampling is not a cosmetic implementation detail. It reduces the frame rate, shortens the prefill path, and lowers latency before the LLM even begins to generate.

The paper trains this side in three stages, and the story is easiest to remember if you treat each stage as a different lesson.

Stage 1 is plain ASR. The model sees speech features and predicts transcripts with CTC loss. This gives the encoder a stable first skill: it learns to preserve semantic content from speech without needing the LLM in the loop.

Stage 2 connects that pretrained encoder to the frozen LLM through the adapter. The transcript still serves as the target, but now the model has to make the speech representation compatible with the backbone’s text space. The paper adds trainable special tokens to guide the model during this alignment phase. At this point, everything except the LLM is trainable, so the bridge can learn without disturbing the language core.

Stage 3 turns the problem into real dialogue. The authors build a multi-round Q&A dataset, have the LLM generate fresh answers, synthesize the questions into speech, and prepend a trainable prompt embedding before each question. The special tokens from Stage 2 are dropped. The prompt embedding acts like a shared soft cue that marks each new spoken turn. The speech encoder is frozen so the acoustic robustness from Stage 2 is preserved, and the LLM stays frozen so its language ability does not drift.

That last stage matters because isolated ASR is not enough. A model can recognize speech without really behaving like a conversational system. Stage 3 teaches the encoder and adapter to work inside a prompt-driven dialogue format, which is what the duplex system actually needs.

The paper also uses dynamic chunk training so the encoder can tolerate different chunk sizes later. That flexibility matters in streaming setups. If you train only for one exact window shape, you may get slightly better numbers for that shape, but you lose robustness when the controller wants to change how speech arrives.

Teaching text to become speech

The output side follows the same staged logic, but now the question is how to make the frozen LLM sound like a person again.

Freeze-Omni uses a token-based speech decoder inspired by VALL-E-style generation. The decoder has a non-autoregressive prefix module and an autoregressive generator, and a codec decoder turns the resulting speech tokens into waveform. The single-codebook codec is a deliberate simplification. It is enough for the limited speaker setting in the paper, and it keeps the system simpler and faster than a heavier multi-codebook design.

Here too, the three stages are doing different jobs.

Stage 1 trains the codec on speech alone. This gives the system a discrete speech alphabet. The codec is not the final assistant; it is the tokenizer and detokenizer that makes speech prediction tractable.

Stage 2 trains the speech decoder on text-speech pairs. Text goes through the backbone LLM tokenizer and embedding layer, then those embeddings act as semantic features for the speech decoder. The autoregressive part learns to predict speech tokens with teacher forcing, using codec-derived labels from Stage 1. In effect, the decoder learns the basic mapping from text semantics to tokenized speech while the LLM embedding layer stays frozen.

Stage 3 is where the decoder learns to match the frozen LLM’s actual output style. The authors reuse the same multi-round Q&A data from the input side, feed the text tokens and hidden states from the backbone LLM, and add a separate prefix speech decoder that converts the LLM hidden state into attention cache for the main speech decoder. The speech targets again come from synthesizing the LLM’s own text answers into speech and then re-tokenizing them with the codec.

That last step is the subtle one. The text generated by the backbone is not the same as the generic text in a paired speech corpus, so the decoder has to adapt to the LLM’s own phrasing and structure. The prefix module is the bridge that closes that gap. You can think of it as teaching the speech decoder to listen to the backbone’s private dialect before it starts speaking.

The paper also reports that adding a tiny pre-network between the LLM embedding layer and the speech decoder helps a lot. That makes sense once you think about the problem: the semantic vectors coming out of the LLM and the discrete speech-token space are related, but they are not the same thing. A small extra translator gives the decoder one more chance to align the two spaces cleanly.

Letting the model decide when to interrupt

Once both bridges are trained, Freeze-Omni can already do speech input to speech output. Duplex dialogue needs one more thing: it has to decide whether the user is still talking, whether the system should keep listening, or whether the current turn should end and a new generation should begin.

The control path is simple enough to describe in plain language. An acoustic VAD detects when speech starts. The stream then enters Freeze-Omni chunk by chunk. At the end of each chunk, a small classification head looks at the LLM’s final hidden state and predicts one of three states.

  • State 0 means keep listening.
  • State 1 means the user is interrupting, so the system should stop the current flow and begin a new generation stage.
  • State 2 means the utterance has ended cleanly, so the system should stop streaming and reset the VAD.

The important detail is that the labels only matter on the last frame of each chunk. The model is not trying to label every acoustic frame. It is making a boundary decision. That is exactly the kind of decision duplex dialogue needs.

Training this head is folded into the same multi-task setup used for the Stage 3 speech-input training. That is a good design choice because turn-taking is not separate from conversation. It is part of how the speech model learns to behave like an active agent instead of a batch transcription system.

What the measurements are really saying

The numbers in the paper line up with the architecture story rather than distracting from it. On the input side, the Stage 2 speech encoder reaches 2.15% CER on aishell-1 and 3.29% WER on dev-clean with full-context decoding. On the output side, the prefix-tuned speech decoder plus the extra pre-network brings CER down to 1.69% at top-k=2. And on spoken question answering, Freeze-Omni gets 44.73% on Web Questions, which is close to the text-only backbone’s 45.13%.

That last comparison is the one that matters most. It says the frozen-backbone recipe actually preserves much of the language model’s original intelligence in the speech setting instead of collapsing into a weaker speech-only assistant.

Latency is also reasonable for a duplex system. The reported statistical latency averages 745 ms, and once the paper adds realistic network delay, the real-world end-to-end response is estimated at about 1.2 seconds. For live speech interaction, that is the difference between a conversational system and a sluggish one.

The tradeoff to remember

Freeze-Omni is best understood as a modular speech recipe, not as a single universal policy. That modularity is the point, but it also comes with cost. The method needs sizable ASR and text-speech paired data for staging, it uses separate speech encoder and decoder modules instead of one fully unified network, and the chunking plus interruption logic makes the system more complex than a plain text-only LLM.

That is the tradeoff the paper is willing to make. In return, it avoids fine-tuning the backbone, keeps the language model’s original behavior intact, and still reaches low-latency speech-to-speech conversation.

If you remember one sentence, make it this one: Freeze-Omni treats speech as a lossy interface to a fixed reasoning core. The speech encoder learns to compress meaning into the LLM’s language, the speech decoder learns to expand it back into audio, and the duplex controller keeps the conversation live.
