Step-Audio-AQAA: a Fully End-to-End Expressive Large Audio Language Model

Step-Audio-AQAA sits in the Native speech-text language models family, but it pushes that family toward a more direct end point. The paper asks a simple question with a hard answer: can a spoken assistant take an audio query and return an answer in speech without first collapsing everything into a text-only pipeline? The paper calls that task AQAA, short for Audio Query-Audio Answer.

That question matters because the familiar ASR -> LLM -> TTS stack is good at moving information between modules, but bad at preserving the full shape of speech. By the time a transcript reaches the language model, prosody, pacing, emotion, and voice control have already been thinned out. Step-Audio-AQAA tries to keep those signals alive by letting one backbone plan the answer and speak it in the same pass.

The whole loop at a glance

Figure 1 is the best way to see the system as a single loop.

What to notice is the separation of jobs. The tokenizer turns speech into two aligned token streams, the backbone turns those tokens into a mixed answer, and the vocoder turns only the audio side of that answer back into a waveform. The model is not trying to synthesize speech directly inside the LLM. It is learning how to keep the content plan and the voice plan close enough that the final speech sounds coherent.

That design choice is the key to the whole paper. Text is not thrown away. It stays in the answer stream as a semantic scaffold, while the audio tokens carry the actual voice layer.

Two token streams, one utterance

The first interesting idea is the dual-codebook tokenizer.

Step-Audio-AQAA does not ask one discrete audio representation to do everything. Instead, it splits speech into a linguistic stream and a semantic stream. The linguistic side captures higher-level structure such as phonetic and language-level information. The semantic side carries the more local acoustic texture that the vocoder will later need to sound natural.

Seen intuitively, the two streams solve different problems. The linguistic tokens say what was said. The semantic tokens help preserve how it sounded. If you only keep one of those, the model either becomes too abstract or too brittle.

The paper reports that the two codebooks help each other. Because the streams are mutually referenced, the next-token prediction problem becomes easier for both of them than it would be with a single codebook. That is a nice reminder that speech compression is not only about shrinking sequence length. It is also about choosing a representation that gives the backbone enough structure to predict the next step without guessing blindly.

The token streams are also time-aligned. They run at different sampling rates, so the model interleaves them into one input sequence instead of feeding the backbone two unrelated views of the same audio. That interleaving is what lets the language model read the speech as one continuous prompt-like sequence.

A backbone that can plan and speak

The backbone is a pre-trained 130B multimodal LLM. The paper starts from a model that already knows how to handle text and speech, then adapts it for AQAA rather than building a new speech model from scratch. In the post-training stage, only the text and speech capabilities matter.

That is why the output is still token-based. The model generates a sequence that mixes textual tokens with audio tokens, and the text tokens are doing real work. They keep the answer semantically anchored while the audio tokens provide the spoken realization.

This is a subtle but important point. The paper is not chasing a “speech-only” answer stream. It is making the answer stream hybrid on purpose. Text acts like a planning skeleton. Audio acts like the voice body. The two together give the model a way to stay coherent while still speaking naturally.

The final voice rendering is handled by a flow-matching vocoder in the CosyVoice family, but conditioned only on audio tokens. That keeps waveform synthesis focused on acoustics instead of asking the LLM to manage continuous signal generation directly. In other words, the backbone decides what to say and which speech tokens to emit, while the vocoder handles the last mile from tokens to sound.

How the model learns to answer

The training recipe is just as important as the architecture.

Figure 2 shows the training story more clearly.

The two supervised formats are worth separating:

  • AQTA means audio query, text answer. This teaches the model to understand speech and produce a clean semantic reply.
  • AQTAA means audio query, text answer, audio answer. This teaches the model to keep that reply available in a speech form too.

That combination is what makes the model feel end-to-end instead of cascaded. It is not learning to transcribe and then hand the job off to a separate TTS stage. It is learning to move through a mixed output format where the answer can stay in the same token space until the vocoder turns it into a waveform.

The first SFT stage updates the full backbone on the combined AQTA and AQTAA data. The goal here is broad alignment: teach the model the input-output contract and make the answer stream behave like a speech-aware assistant rather than a text-only decoder.

The second SFT stage then narrows to high-quality AQTAA examples. That extra pass is there to stabilize the interleaved output structure and strengthen harder behaviors such as singing. In practice, this is the stage that teaches the model to keep its voice format steady instead of drifting back toward plain text.

Why masked DPO matters

Supervised fine-tuning gets the model close, but the paper still adds Direct Preference Optimization.

The twist is that the authors do not apply DPO blindly to every token. When they tried optimizing all tokens, text and audio sometimes drifted out of alignment. The speech side was getting pushed too hard by a preference signal that was really meant to improve the answer quality, not to reshape the voice stream at every position.

Masked DPO fixes that by suppressing the loss on audio-token positions. That leaves the preference signal focused where it helps most, while protecting the speech pathway from being over-optimized into inconsistency. The intuition is straightforward: if the text scaffold is what carries semantic planning, then the preference signal should mostly refine that scaffold instead of attacking the whole mixed sequence equally.

The starting point also matters. DPO begins from the first-stage SFT checkpoint rather than the second-stage one, because the second stage is more specialized and can damage some broader abilities while improving others. Starting from the broader checkpoint gives DPO a better base to refine.

Why the model merge is not just housekeeping

After SFT and DPO, the paper does one more practical thing: it merges the checkpoints.

This is not a decorative ensemble. It is a way to keep the strengths of three different models at once. The first-stage SFT checkpoint keeps the broad question-answering behavior. The second-stage SFT checkpoint keeps the more stable text-audio interleaving and the harder speech-style abilities. The DPO checkpoint adds preference alignment.

A weighted average of those models gives the final backbone. That matters because the model stages are not solving exactly the same problem. If you keep only the final DPO checkpoint, you risk losing useful breadth. If you keep only the second-stage SFT checkpoint, you may miss some alignment gains. Merging is the compromise that preserves the useful pieces of each stage in one set of weights.

When one answer has more than one speech state

The paper also has a clever data trick for multi-label speech.

Sometimes one turn should not stay in a single speaking style. It may need to switch emotion, speaking rate, or another voice state within the same answer. To teach that behavior, the authors splice together single-label examples into multi-label examples and then interleave text and audio around those combined segments.

They compare three splicing strategies:

  1. Remove the special audio markers, concatenate the segments, then add the markers back.
  2. Interleave each label segment first, then concatenate the results.
  3. Keep the <audio_start> and <audio_end> markers in place while concatenating, then interleave.

The third option wins. The reason is simple: the markers preserve the boundaries the model has already learned from single-label examples. If you strip those markers too early, or if you reformat each piece before stitching them together, the model loses the stable pattern that tells it where one speech state ends and the next begins.

This is the kind of detail that looks minor until you think about what the model is really learning. The markers are not decoration. They are boundary signs. Preserving them makes it easier for the backbone to learn when to switch style inside one response.

What the ablations are really telling you

The ablations reinforce the same lesson from another angle: the text side of the output is not optional.

When the output is forced toward audio-only generation, quality drops sharply. The best setting is the one that keeps enough text in the answer stream to preserve chat quality, relevance, and factuality while still producing substantial audio content. In other words, the model works best when text remains a planning scaffold and audio remains the voice layer.

That is a useful design lesson beyond this paper. If a speech model tries to remove text too early, it often loses semantic stability before the vocoder ever sees the output. If it keeps text as a scaffold, it can plan the answer more cleanly and then fill in the speech layer with higher confidence.

The same pattern shows up in the marker-preserving multi-label setup. The model does better when the training format resembles the format it saw during simpler single-label learning. Consistency of boundaries and consistency of interleaving both matter.

What this buys you in practice

The method is strongest when the task really depends on speech control. That includes speech emotion control, creativity, role-playing, language ability, gaming, and voice understanding. The general pattern is easy to explain: if the output has to stay expressive while still answering the user correctly, the interleaved text-audio setup helps.

The weak spots are also easy to understand. Singing is still hard, partly because more singing data can hurt other capabilities. Voice instruction following is limited by sparse matched supervision, so the model does not always hear enough examples of “change the voice in exactly this way.” And the whole system still leans on textual scaffolding inside the generated output, which is powerful but also a sign that speech-only generation is not fully solved yet.

That leaves a few open questions the paper itself points toward: can audio tokens be generated without textual guidance, are discrete audio tokens the right representation for natural speech, and can singing improve without degrading everything else?

What to remember

The best way to remember Step-Audio-AQAA is to think of it as a speech model with two aligned layers.

The text layer is the semantic scaffold. It keeps the answer organized and coherent. The audio layer is the voice. It carries the actual speech texture that the vocoder turns into a waveform. The training recipe - two-stage SFT, masked DPO, and checkpoint merging - exists to keep those layers aligned instead of letting one overpower the other.

If you want the shortest version, it is this: Step-Audio-AQAA does not throw text away on the way to speech. It keeps text in the loop so the model can plan and speak at the same time, then uses preference tuning and model merging to keep that mixed answer stream stable.

That is the real contribution of the paper, and it is why the method feels more like a speech-native assistant than a stitched-together ASR and TTS pipeline.
