Spoken Question Answering and Speech Continuation Using Spectrogram-Powered LLM

Spectron is easiest to remember as a speech prompt, a text scratchpad, and then a speech continuation. The model takes a prompt audio segment, turns it into a compact prefix with a pretrained Conformer encoder, lets a decoder-only LLM finish the transcript in text, and then uses that text to reconstruct future spectrogram frames. It is not trying to jump straight from raw audio to raw audio. It is trying to make the LLM think in text first, then speak from that text.

That design choice is the whole paper in one sentence. If you treat the transcript as an intermediate workspace instead of the final destination, the model can reuse the language model’s strengths while still being trained to preserve acoustic detail. Spectron therefore acts less like a speech recognizer with a TTS add-on and more like a single model that learns to listen, reason, and continue speaking in one pass.

The shortcut: let text sit in the middle

Speech and text do not fit together naturally. Speech is long, continuous, and full of timing information. A text LLM expects a much shorter stream of embeddings and then predicts one token at a time. Spectron handles that mismatch by making the speech encoder do only one thing: compress the prompt into a prefix that the LLM can read.

The figure below shows the same idea in the training and inference flow. What to notice is the order. The decoder does not see a giant audio tensor and improvise. It first gets speech features as a prefix, then produces text, and only after that does it predict the future acoustic frames.

The bridge is also deliberately simple. The speech encoder connects to the LLM through a prefix only, with no cross-attention stack between the two parts. That matters because the decoder stays close to its pretrained behavior. The speech side becomes a conditioning prefix, not a second attention system competing with the language model’s own next-token machinery.

Why the transcript is a scratchpad

The most interesting part of Spectron is not the encoder. It is the fact that the model is trained to write text before it writes sound.

During training, each utterance is split into a prompt segment and a continuation segment. The prompt speech is what the encoder sees. The continuation speech is what the reconstruction loss has to recover. The corresponding transcript is also split into prompt and continuation pieces, so the decoder is asked to do three things at once: recognize the prompt, continue the transcript, and emit the acoustic continuation.

That makes the transcript an intermediate scratchpad. The decoder is not using text as a dead-end output. It is using text as a working representation that sits between speech understanding and speech generation. In practice, this is the part that gives the model a place to use language-model knowledge before it has to commit to audio frames.

The small pre-net and post-net projections are what make that possible. The pre-net compresses the ground-truth continuation spectrogram into the language model’s embedding dimension before the decoder consumes it. The post-net maps the decoder’s output back into spectrogram space. This is a useful bottleneck, not just an engineering convenience. It makes the speech target easier to decode and helps avoid the kind of repetitive behavior that frame-level generators can fall into.

There is also a nice simplification hidden in the way the losses are written. Because the text supervision covers the full transcript, the paper does not need a delicate speech-to-text alignment term in the objective. The prompt and continuation transcripts can be treated as one text sequence for cross-entropy, while the acoustic continuation is handled separately. That keeps the training recipe cleaner than a pipeline that needs explicit alignment between the speech cut point and the transcript cut point.

The loss teaches three jobs at once

The paper’s core training recipe is surprisingly compact. One term teaches text prediction, and one weighted term teaches speech reconstruction.

Purpose: combine transcript learning with acoustic continuation learning.

Intuition: if the model only learns to predict text, it may ignore acoustic structure that matters for speech generation. If it also has to reconstruct the continuation spectrogram, it must preserve enough information to sound like a continuation instead of merely producing a transcript.

Formula:

Ltotal(x,y)=LCE(y,y^)+λrLRecon.(xc,x^c)\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{total}}(x,y)=\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CE}}(y,\hat{y})+\lambda_{r}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Recon.}}(x_{c},\hat{x}_{c})Ltotal​(x,y)=LCE​(y,y^​)+λr​LRecon.​(xc​,x^c​)

Symbols: x is the input speech spectrogram, y is the full target transcript, \hat{y} is the predicted transcript, x_c is the ground-truth continuation spectrogram, \hat{x}_c is the predicted continuation spectrogram, \mathcal{L}_{CE} is the text cross-entropy loss, \mathcal{L}_{Recon.} is the reconstruction loss, and \lambda_r sets how strongly the reconstruction term matters.

Read-aloud meaning: train the model to get the text right, and also train it to reconstruct the future speech, with a tunable weight on the acoustic part.

Why it matters: the text term keeps the decoder language-smart, while the reconstruction term keeps it speech-aware.

Inside the reconstruction term, the paper does something subtle but important. It does not only compare the predicted spectrogram to the target spectrogram frame by frame. It also adds losses on frequency deltas and time deltas, with L1 and L2 penalties. A good way to think about this is that the model is being asked to match the shape of the sound, not just its raw pixel values. That is closer to matching edges and contours in an image than to checking whether every point is exactly equal.

The ablation study backs that up. Removing the text cross-entropy or removing the spectrogram derivative losses causes the largest performance drops. In other words, those are not decorative additions. They are the mechanism that makes the text scratchpad and the speech continuation cooperate.

Inference writes text first, then writes sound

At inference time, the model gets only a speech prompt. The encoder turns that prompt into a prefix in LLM space. Then the decoder generates the transcript autoregressively until it emits an end token. Only after that does it start generating spectrogram frames, again autoregressively, conditioned on the prompt prefix, the generated text, and the previously predicted frames.

That ordering is important. The text branch is not a side head. It is the control signal that shapes the acoustic continuation. The model first decides what the utterance should say, then uses that decision to decide how the sound should continue.

The vocoder comes last and is almost a mechanical step compared with the rest of the pipeline. Once the spectrogram continuation is predicted, a frozen vocoder turns it into waveform audio. So the real modeling work happens before the waveform ever exists. Spectron is not learning waveform synthesis from scratch. It is learning to preserve the right spectral trajectory so a vocoder can render it.

This also explains the main practical drawback. The text and speech branches are sequential, not parallel. The model cannot easily stream both at once, and frame-by-frame spectrogram generation is expensive for long utterances. Spectron is therefore strong as a proof of concept for end-to-end speech continuation, but it is not a cheap streaming system.

What the results tell you

The cleanest way to read the experiments is not to stare at every number. It is to ask what the numbers say about the method choice.

  • On LibriSpeech continuation, Spectron reaches 126.08 log-perplexity, ahead of the reported GSLM, TWIST, AudioLM, and SpeechGPT baselines in that setup.
  • Human ratings put naturalness at 3.68 +/- 0.29 MOS, which is ahead of GSLM and TWIST and close to the stronger AudioLM result.
  • For spoken QA with a 1B language model, the paper reports 22.9% on LLaMA-Questions and 6.1% on Web Questions.

The more revealing signal is the ablation table. The biggest degradation comes from removing the text loss or the spectrogram derivative losses, while removing the pretrained encoder or the pretrained LM also hurts. That is exactly what you would hope to see if the paper’s main claim is true: the bridge works because the model is taught both language and acoustics, not because one giant component accidentally carries the whole system.

There is also an important conceptual takeaway in the comparison with discrete-token speech models. Spectron stays in continuous spectrogram space instead of going through a discrete speech-token bottleneck. That makes the method simpler, and it preserves a lot of speaker and prosodic detail that would otherwise be quantized away. The tradeoff is speed. Continuous frame generation is slower and less streamable, but it keeps the signal closer to the original speech.

What to remember

Spectron’s mental model is simple once you strip away the implementation details:

speech prompt -> text scratchpad -> spectrogram continuation.

The speech encoder builds a prefix, the LLM uses that prefix to write text, and the same decoder then turns that text into future acoustic frames. The transcript is not just an output. It is the intermediate representation that lets a pretrained language model participate in speech generation without losing the structure that speech needs.

That is why the paper matters. It shows that a continuous spectrogram path can compete with more tokenized speech systems if the objective teaches recognition, continuation, and reconstruction together. The price is latency and sequential decoding, but the benefit is a simpler model that keeps more of the original speech signal intact.
