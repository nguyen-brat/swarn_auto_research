SALMONN: Towards Generic Hearing Abilities for Large Language Models

SALMONN is a hearing model first and a text generator second. The paper is trying to teach Vicuna to handle speech, audio events, and music without collapsing into a plain transcription pipeline. That sounds simple, but it is exactly where audio LLMs get stuck: once the model sees a lot of easy audio-text pairs, it learns to answer every prompt like an ASR system. SALMONN’s answer is to keep the language model mostly frozen, feed it a compact audio prefix, and then add a tiny correction stage when instruction tuning starts to narrow the model too much.

The architecture diagram makes that path easier to see.

What to notice is the order of compression. Raw audio does not go straight into Vicuna. The sound is first turned into features, then compressed into a short token-like prefix, and only then passed to the LLM. That is the core trick: the language model reads a prompt-shaped summary of the audio, not the full waveform.

How sound becomes a prompt

SALMONN starts by splitting the hearing problem into two complementary views of the same clip. Whisper is used for speech-sensitive features, including robustness to background noise and fine-grained spoken content. BEATs contributes higher-level non-speech semantics, which helps the model understand audio events and music rather than treating them as noise around the transcript.

The point of the dual encoder setup is not redundancy. It is division of labor. Whisper is good at what people say. BEATs is good at what the sound scene is doing. SALMONN fuses them so the later language model does not have to guess which part of the audio matters.

The pipeline is easiest to remember as a short sequence:

The bridge in the middle is the window-level Q-Former. Instead of summarizing the whole clip in one shot, SALMONN cuts the feature stream into windows and lets a small set of trainable queries read each window. Each window becomes a compact token block. That keeps long clips manageable and preserves the order of events, which matters when timing carries meaning.

Think of it like taking notes every few seconds instead of trying to remember the entire lecture from one final sentence. The notes are short, but they stay in the same order as the audio. For speech recognition, that alignment is important. For broader audio understanding, it keeps the model from flattening a rich sound scene into one vague summary.

Vicuna sits behind that bridge as the reasoning engine. SALMONN keeps Vicuna frozen and uses LoRA adapters inside the self-attention layers to adapt the model to audio tokens. That is a practical choice. The system only needs to train the bridge and the adapters, so it can learn hearing without rewriting the whole language model. At the same time, the backbone keeps most of its general instruction-following skill.

The training recipe is simple, but the order matters

SALMONN is trained in three stages:

The first stage is pretraining. Here the goal is alignment, not reasoning. ASR and audio captioning teach the bridge how to map audio into text space, and they do it with tasks that have relatively clear targets. That gives the Q-Former and LoRA a sensible starting point before the model has to deal with harder instructions.

The second stage is instruction tuning. This is where SALMONN learns a broader set of behaviors across speech, audio, and music tasks: translation, captioning, question answering, speaker-related tasks, emotion recognition, and more. The model starts to look like a general hearing assistant instead of a narrow recognizer.

But this is also where the paper finds a problem. Many of the training pairs are easy in the sense that they have short, deterministic answers. ASR has one obvious transcript. Captioning often has one obvious short summary. If those tasks dominate, the model learns a narrow reply habit. The result is a system that is good at the training tasks but hesitant or flat on harder open-ended prompts.

Why the model starts to prefer the easy answer

The paper’s main diagnosis is that instruction tuning can make the model too predictable. It learns an intrinsic audio-conditioned response pattern that is strong enough to overpower the user’s instruction when the prompt asks for something more open-ended.

One useful way to see that is with the paper’s Bayes-style decomposition.

Formula card: Why instruction prompts get ignored

Purpose: Explain how a strong audio-driven answer tendency can overpower the instruction.

Intuition: If the model already thinks one short answer is very likely from the audio alone, the instruction has to fight that bias.

Formula:

PΛ(Y∣X,I)=PΛ(Y∣X)⋅PΛ(I∣Y,X)/PΛ(I∣X)P_{\Lambda}(Y \mid X, I) = P_{\Lambda}(Y \mid X) \cdot P_{\Lambda}(I \mid Y, X) / P_{\Lambda}(I \mid X)PΛ​(Y∣X,I)=PΛ​(Y∣X)⋅PΛ​(I∣Y,X)/PΛ​(I∣X)

Symbols:

  • \Lambda: the learned SALMONN parameters.
  • X: the audio input.
  • I: the text instruction.
  • Y: the response sequence.
  • P_{\Lambda}(Y \mid X, I): the probability of a response given both audio and instruction.
  • P_{\Lambda}(Y \mid X): the response habit learned from audio alone.
  • P_{\Lambda}(I \mid Y, X): how well a candidate response fits the instruction for that audio.
  • P_{\Lambda}(I \mid X): the normalization term.

Read-aloud meaning: the final answer depends on both the model’s audio-only bias and how compatible that answer is with the instruction.

Why it matters: if the audio-only bias becomes too strong, the model defaults to transcription-like behavior unless a later tuning stage rebalances it.

Read that equation as a tug-of-war. P_{\Lambda}(Y \mid X) is the habit learned from audio alone. P_{\Lambda}(I \mid Y, X) is the check that says, “does this answer really match the prompt?” If the first term is too confident, the model takes the safe path. That is why SALMONN can look strong on the tasks it trained on and still ignore harder prompts like spoken-question QA, storytelling, or speech-audio co-reasoning.

Activation tuning: a small correction that changes the behavior

SALMONN’s fix is not to rebuild the model. It adds a short activation tuning stage after instruction tuning.

The trick starts at test time. The paper discounts the LoRA contribution, which weakens the model’s usual short-answer habit and makes it easier to generate longer, more varied responses. On its own, that discounted adapter hurts the trained tasks, so SALMONN uses the weakened model to generate a small set of story-like responses and then fine-tunes on those responses with ordinary teacher forcing.

That is why the paper calls it activation tuning. The weak adapter helps reveal behavior that was already latent in the model, but the extra fine-tuning step is what keeps that behavior around without destroying the original hearing skills.

Stories are the key supervision signal here because they force the model out of the safe transcription groove. A story answer is longer, less repetitive, and less deterministic than ASR. The paper also tries longer-answer QA and longer ASR labels, but those are less effective. Longer ASR labels make the transcription bias stronger, and text-only updates do not directly correct the audio-conditioned distribution. Story supervision is the better regularizer because it asks the model to stay grounded in audio while producing genuinely open-ended text.

This also explains why activation tuning is so cheap. The model does not need a giant new dataset or a new backbone. It only needs a tiny batch of self-generated story examples to nudge the response distribution back toward diversity.

What the results actually say

The important part of the results is the shape of the tradeoff, not the whole table. SALMONN stays competitive on the tasks it was trained on - ASR, translation, captioning, emotion, speaker, and music - but activation tuning is what unlocks the harder zero-shot behaviors.

The anchor numbers are the ones that matter here: with activation tuning, Story reaches 82.57 and SAC reaches 0.50, while ASR stays at 2.1 WER. Without that extra stage, SQQA, Story, and SAC largely fail to follow instructions. That is the clearest sign that the problem is not just perception. It is also the model’s answer habit.

The limits are just as informative:

  • Phone recognition and overlapping speech are still harder than plain ASR, because Whisper is not specialized for those exact cases.
  • Activation tuning needs self-generated story supervision, so the fix is practical but not universal.
  • Discounting LoRA too far can wake up open-ended behavior while hurting the easier trained tasks.

Those caveats do not weaken the paper’s main idea. They show where the bridge is strong and where it still needs help.

What to remember

SALMONN is easiest to remember as a three-part bridge:

  • Whisper and BEATs hear different parts of the signal.
  • A window-level Q-Former turns those features into a short audio prefix.
  • Activation tuning keeps Vicuna from collapsing into transcription mode.

The deeper lesson is simple: a strong audio bridge is not enough on its own. If instruction tuning makes the audio prior too narrow, you need a second correction pass to recover open-ended hearing.
