VoxtLM: Unified Decoder-Only Models for Consolidating Speech Recognition, Synthesis and Speech, Text Continuation Tasks

VoxtLM is a very direct attempt to erase the old border between speech and text. Instead of building one stack for recognition and another for synthesis, the paper turns speech into discrete tokens, merges those tokens with text tokens, and trains one autoregressive decoder to continue whichever token stream the task asks for. That single move lets the same model handle speech recognition, speech synthesis, text continuation, and speech continuation.

That is why the paper sits naturally in the native speech-text language models family. The point is not just to add audio to a text model. The point is to make speech and text behave like one shared language for a decoder that already knows how to continue sequences.

What to notice in the first figure is the simplification. The old encoder-decoder split keeps speech and text in separate lanes. VoxtLM collapses that split into one shared decoder. Once speech has been tokenized, the model no longer needs a special ASR branch or a special TTS branch just to decide what kind of sequence it is reading.

Turn speech into something the language model can read

The core trick is to make speech look like a token sequence. VoxtLM uses a HuBERT-based speech tokenizer, then clusters those features with k-means to produce discrete semantic tokens. These tokens are not meant to preserve every acoustic detail. They are meant to capture the linguistic content that a language model can already work with.

That choice matters. Speech is long and continuous, while a decoder-only LM wants compact symbols. If the model had to ingest raw waveforms directly, the sequence would be far too long. If it only saw a transcript, it would lose too much of the speech signal. VoxtLM sits in the middle: it keeps speech discrete, but still speech-like enough to be useful.

The paper then merges the speech tokens with the text vocabulary into one Voxt vocabulary. That is the real unification step. The decoder is no longer choosing between a text head and a speech head. It is predicting from one shared token inventory, where text tokens and speech tokens live side by side.

There is one more compression trick here. VoxtLM also applies subword modeling and replaces frequent patterns with metatokens. In plain language, that means repeated chunks can be collapsed into larger units. Text gets a more compact representation, and speech sequences become shorter and easier to model.

The k value in the tokenizer is the main granularity knob. A small k gives a tighter token inventory and can preserve linguistic structure well, but it can also throw away acoustic detail that TTS needs. A larger k keeps more detail, but it also makes the token space harder to learn. That tradeoff is one of the paper’s central design choices.

What to notice in the second figure is where the continuous world appears and disappears. Speech only leaves the waveform domain twice: once when the tokenizer turns it into discrete units, and once when the speech token decoder turns generated tokens back into audio. Everything in between is just a decoder-only language model working over a shared vocabulary.

That is the clean mental model to keep in mind. VoxtLM is not a speech encoder plus a speech decoder wrapped around an LLM. It is one sequence model surrounded by two conversion modules: one that turns speech into tokens, and one that turns generated speech tokens back into sound.

The one formula that matters

Once speech and text live in the same vocabulary, the training rule becomes ordinary autoregressive language modeling.

Purpose: describe one shared next-token objective for text-only, speech-only, and mixed speech-text sequences.

Intuition: after tokenization, speech is just another ordered symbol stream. The model learns to predict the next symbol from everything it has already seen.

Formula:

Symbols: Z is the full token sequence. z_i is the token at position i. t is the sequence length. Each z_i comes from the merged Voxt vocabulary. p(...) is the probability assigned by the decoder to the next token.

Read-aloud meaning: the model scores a sequence by multiplying the probability of each next token given all previous tokens.

Why it matters: this is what lets one decoder cover four jobs with one training objective. ASR becomes text continuation conditioned on speech. TTS becomes speech continuation conditioned on text. TextLM and SpeechLM are just the same next-token game in one modality.

The paper trains with teacher forcing, so the decoder always receives the true earlier tokens during training. That keeps the objective stable and makes each task look like a standard language-model prediction problem, just with a larger alphabet.

One decoder, four tasks, four control tokens

VoxtLM uses special tokens to tell the model what kind of continuation it should produce. There are two start tokens and two generate tokens:

  • <start-text> and <start-speech> mark the conditioning modality.
  • <generate-text> and <generate-speech> tell the decoder which modality to continue in.

That gives the model a very simple interface. The same backbone can be used as a text LM, a speech LM, an ASR model, or a TTS model just by changing the prompt prefix.

Here Y is text tokens and D is discrete speech tokens. The important asymmetry is that ASR and TTS need both a start token and a generate token. The start token says what kind of conditioning input the model is seeing. The generate token says when the conditioning prefix ends and the continuation should switch to the other modality.

That is the whole architectural idea in miniature. The model is not given a separate ASR head or a separate TTS head. It is given a routing signal inside the sequence itself.

At inference time, the user gives only the condition prefix. The decoder then continues autoregressively, and beam search is used to keep the output stable. For text tasks, the continuation stays in text. For speech tasks, the continuation stays in discrete speech tokens, which are later turned back into waveform.

Why the speech side still needs a vocoder

VoxtLM does not generate waveforms directly. That would be too hard for a text-style decoder and would blur the boundary between sequence modeling and sample synthesis. Instead, the model generates discrete speech tokens, and a separate speech token decoder turns those tokens back into audio.

The paper uses a HiFiGAN vocoder for that last step, conditioned on an x-vector speaker embedding. That speaker embedding is important because it gives the system a handle on voice identity. The speech tokens say what should be spoken; the speaker embedding helps decide how it should sound.

This setup keeps the generator simple. The language model only has to predict token sequences. The vocoder handles waveform reconstruction. The tradeoff is also clear: VoxtLM still depends on a speech tokenizer and a vocoder, so it is not fully waveform-native.

The design knobs that actually move the results

The paper’s method section is small, but the behavior of the system depends on a few knobs that matter a lot.

Token granularity

The k value is the first major knob. It controls how coarse or fine the discrete speech vocabulary is.

  • Small k compresses speech aggressively. That can keep the sequence short, but it also risks losing the acoustic detail needed for clean synthesis and recognition.
  • Larger k keeps more detail. That tends to help TTS and ASR, but the speech token space becomes harder to model.

The paper’s own results reflect that tradeoff. Very small speech vocabularies hurt TTS and ASR first. The model needs enough discrete symbols to preserve the signal, but not so many that the decoder loses its language-model simplicity.

Text initialization

VoxtLM also starts from a pretrained OPT text LM. That is a practical move, not a cosmetic one. The decoder already knows how to do next-token prediction over text, so the model begins with a strong language prior before it learns the new speech tokens.

The merged Voxt vocabulary still has to be learned from scratch, but the decoder weights do not. That helps especially when the supervised speech-text pairs are much smaller than the text-only and speech-only corpora. In the unbalanced setup, initialization makes the ASR jump much larger than you would expect from a random start, reducing test-clean WER from 21.0 to 13.1.

Data balance

The third knob is the task mix. VoxtLM is trained on a blend of text-only, speech-only, paired ASR, and paired TTS data. The paper tests both balanced and unbalanced mixtures because one task can easily dominate the others.

This is where the multitask story becomes interesting. Shared training helps TTS the most, but ASR is the first task to suffer when the mix is skewed. The authors explicitly note that the paired ASR data are much scarcer than the text-only and speech-only pools, so unbalanced training tends to punish recognition more than synthesis.

The balanced setting gives the clearest win for synthesis: TTS CER drops from 28.9 to 5.6, and MOSNet rises from 2.68 to 3.76. That is a big sign that shared training is not just a regularization trick. It is actually helping the model learn a more usable speech-generating representation.

What the method is really saying

VoxtLM makes a strong claim about how to unify speech and text: if every modality is tokenized into one vocabulary and the task is marked explicitly, one decoder can transfer learning across recognition, synthesis, and continuation tasks.

That claim works because the method preserves a few things very carefully:

  • token order
  • task identity
  • shared vocabulary
  • prefix context

Everything else is allowed to be compressed or delegated to the tokenizer and vocoder. That is why the system is elegant. It does not try to keep all acoustic detail inside the language model. It only keeps enough detail for the requested task to work.

The limits are equally clear. If the speech vocabulary is too small, synthesis and recognition lose detail. If the data mix is too skewed, ASR degrades first. And if the tokenizer or vocoder is weak, the whole system inherits that weakness immediately.

So the durable lesson is not “decoder-only is always better.” The lesson is narrower and more useful: once speech can be represented as tokens in the same sequence space as text, a single decoder can learn more than one speech task without rebuilding the architecture around every new objective.
