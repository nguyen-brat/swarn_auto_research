Language Model Can Listen While Speaking

LSLM is a speech language model that tries to do something human conversation does by default: keep talking while still hearing the room. Within the real-time speech-to-speech and duplex agents family, it is the clearest example of the bottleneck this family cares about most, which is real-time turn-taking. The paper does not treat interruption as a separate control loop outside the model. It folds listening into the same autoregressive process that is already generating speech.

That is the whole trick. Once the model can watch live audio while it is still predicting the next speech token, interruption becomes a modeling problem instead of a wrapper problem.

Start with the conversation shape

The easiest way to understand the paper is to step back from the architecture and look at the communication regime it wants to reach. Figure 1 shows the progression from systems that only listen, to systems that listen and speak in separate turns, to systems that can do both at once.

What to notice here is the final column. Full duplex does not mean “better speech generation” in some vague sense. It means the system can keep producing its own speech while still monitoring incoming audio for a takeover signal. That is the difference between a turn-based assistant and something that can actually be interrupted mid-response.

The paper calls this full duplex modeling, or FDM. LSLM is its concrete end-to-end realization.

The machine has two paths, not one

Figure 2 shows the whole system in one view. There is a speaking path that generates discrete speech tokens, and a listening path that processes live audio streamingly. A small projection layer makes the listening features readable by the decoder, and an IRQ token lets the model stop early when turn-taking begins.

The important thing to notice is that the two paths are not copies of the same thing. The speaking side is a decoder-only token generator for TTS. The listening side is a streaming self-supervised audio encoder. The architecture works because the model does not ask one module to be both a speech synthesizer and a live interruption detector.

That separation keeps the responsibilities clean:

  • the speaking branch decides what should be said next,
  • the listening branch decides whether something external deserves attention,
  • the fusion step decides how much the two should influence each other.

This is also why the paper can keep the system end-to-end. There is no separate ASR module handing off to a separate TTS module. The model owns the full duplex loop itself.

Why the speaking side is token-based

LSLM uses a token-based decoder-only TTS backbone instead of a more elaborate AR-plus-NAR stack. The paper is explicit about this choice: single-layer discrete audio tokens are a better fit for real-time interaction than multi-layer residual quantization schemes that ask the model to wait for extra refinement steps.

That design choice is easy to miss, but it matters a lot. If the speaking path had to finish a slow refinement stage before it could react to a new interrupt, the listening side would not feel truly live. A single token stream keeps the speaking side simple enough to stay responsive.

The content and the voice are also separated in a useful way. The decoder learns the token sequence for the speech content, while an acoustic prompt provides timbre. That means the autoregressive model can focus on language and timing, rather than trying to memorize speaker identity inside every prediction step.

That is a very ordinary language-model pipeline, just applied to audio tokens instead of text tokens. The clever part is that this ordinary pipeline can now live beside a live listening stream.

The listening side is the real novelty

The listening path starts from live audio, which can be speech, noise, or a mixture of both. The paper uses the same SSL encoder family for the audio front end and then projects the listening features into the decoder space with a simple linear mapping.

The projection step is modest on purpose. It is not trying to rewrite the listening signal into something completely new. It is trying to make live audio compatible with the autoregressive decoder so the decoder can condition on it at every step.

This is why streaming matters. If the encoder were not streaming, the model would only see stale chunks of audio and would not be able to react in time. For a duplex system, latency is part of the model behavior, not just a deployment detail.

There is also a practical reason the listening branch must stay conservative. The audio stream may contain noise that is not a human interruption at all. The model has to avoid treating every bump, ring, or burst of white noise as a takeover request.

The one equation that matters

Formula card: next-token duplex conditioning

Purpose: describe how the next speech token is chosen when the model is both speaking and listening.

Intuition: the decoder should look at what it has already said, what it is hearing right now, and the text context all at once.

Formula:

rtq∼P(rtq∣R1:t−1q,S1:t−1p,C;θLS)r_t^q \sim P(r_t^q \mid R_{1:t-1}^q, S_{1:t-1}^p, C; \theta_{LS})rtq​∼P(rtq​∣R1:t−1q​,S1:t−1p​,C;θLS​)

Symbols:

  • r_t^q is the next discrete speech token to generate.
  • R_{1:t-1}^q is the already generated speech-token history.
  • S_{1:t-1}^p is the projected listening history up to the current step.
  • C is the text context being conditioned on.
  • \theta_{LS} are the parameters of the listening-while-speaking model.
  • P is the conditional distribution over next tokens.

Read-aloud meaning: choose the next speech token from a distribution that can see past speech, live audio, and the prompt.

Why it matters: this is the core duplex update rule. It turns interruption into part of next-token prediction instead of a separate external classifier.

That single change explains almost everything else in the paper. The model can emit the normal end-of-sequence token when it is done speaking, or it can emit IRQ when the listening stream says someone else is trying to take the floor. The interruption detector is not bolted on after generation. It is represented as a token choice inside generation.

Where the listening signal enters is the main design knob

The paper does not stop at “add a listening encoder.” It asks where the listening signal should be fused into the speaking model. That is the actual tradeoff knob, and Figure 3 lays out the three choices.

The three options are easy to say but very different in behavior.

Early fusion: too much, too soon

Early fusion injects listening information at the input embedding level, before the Transformer starts its autoregressive work. That sounds direct, but it is also the most disruptive choice. The listening signal is mixed into the speech stream too early, so the decoder has a harder time preserving clean speech structure.

The paper’s results make that clear: early fusion badly damages TTS quality, with word error rates exploding into the 30% range. In practice, that means the model starts to lose the very fluency it was supposed to preserve.

Middle fusion: the sweet spot

Middle fusion feeds the listening signal into each Transformer block, alongside the speaking hidden states and positional information. That is the most balanced option because the model can consult live audio at every layer without letting the listening stream overwrite the speech stream at the input.

This is the design that works best. On the command-based interruption test, middle fusion stays near baseline speech quality and handles noisy interruptions well, with 4.05% WER in clean conditions, 4.51% WER in noise, and 97.38% F1 in noisy interruption detection. The point is not just that the numbers are good. The point is that the model can react without collapsing speech quality.

Late fusion: safe for speech, weaker for turn-taking

Late fusion waits until the output logits stage before mixing in the listening signal. That makes it gentler on speech generation, but it also means the interruption signal arrives very late in the decision process.

In clean settings that can look acceptable. Under noise, though, the model is more likely to confuse a random sound with a real human interruption or miss the interruption entirely. The paper shows that this late-stage decision hurts interruptibility more than middle fusion does.

So the fusion story is straightforward: earlier fusion gives you faster reactivity but more speech damage, later fusion preserves speech better but weakens take-over handling, and middle fusion balances the two.

How the paper tests real turn-taking

The evaluation is built around two versions of the same basic question: can the model stop speaking when it should, and keep speaking when it should not?

In the command-based setting, the interruption cue is a fixed keyword. That is the easier version of the problem, because the model only has to recognize one known trigger. In the voice-based setting, the interrupting speaker can say a variety of words, which makes the task much closer to real conversation.

The harder setting matters because it exposes the real failure mode of duplex systems: a live audio stream is messy. There is background noise, unfamiliar voices, and no guarantee that the interrupting speaker has been seen before. In that setting, the middle-fusion model still works, but its interruption scores drop. The paper reports 5.33% WER in clean voice-based tests and 8.50% WER with noise, with noisy interruption F1 falling to 85.15%.

That result is useful because it keeps the story honest. LSLM is not magically solving arbitrary speaker takeover. It is showing that a duplex model can handle the problem much better than a turn-based one, while still revealing where the remaining difficulty lives.

Why pretraining both halves still helps

The ablation study points to a sensible conclusion: the best version is the one that loads both pretrained components and keeps training them. That makes intuitive sense. The speaking backbone already knows how to generate speech tokens, and the listening encoder already knows how to read audio. Continuing training lets them learn the duplex handshake together instead of forcing one side to stay frozen.

The paper also suggests why freezing can be weaker. A listening encoder that was pretrained on cleaner or less varied audio may not be ready for the mixture of speech and noise that real turn-taking brings. Fine-tuning gives it a chance to adapt to that harder distribution.

What still breaks

LSLM is a strong first step, but it is not the final answer to real-time speech interaction. Noise and unseen speakers still reduce interruption precision and recall. Voice-based interruption is harder than fixed-command interruption. And the system still lacks speaker-following and audiovisual guidance, both of which would help it decide who is actually trying to take the floor.

Those limits point to the next set of problems rather than weakening the paper. They make it clear that the hard part is not only speech generation. It is deciding, in real time, whether a new audio event deserves to interrupt the current turn.

What to remember

LSLM is easiest to remember as a speaker with an always-on ear.

The speaking branch is a token-based decoder-only TTS system. The listening branch is a streaming SSL encoder that turns live audio into decoder-ready features. The IRQ token turns interruption into a normal token decision. And middle fusion is the sweet spot because it lets the model listen without corrupting its own speech.

That is the core idea of the paper: to make a speech model truly interruptible, the model itself has to keep listening while it speaks.
