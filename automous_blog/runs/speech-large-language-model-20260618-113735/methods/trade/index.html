TRADE: Transducer-Augmented Decoder for Speech Large Language Models

TRADE is built for a very specific mismatch. A speech LLM can produce fluent text, but its decoder is still label-synchronous: it chooses the next token from text history, not from frame-level timing. That means it does not naturally know when to emit a word, when to wait for more audio, or when a sentence has really ended.

The paper’s answer is to split the job in two. A transducer becomes the clock, deciding when the model may read or write. The LLM stays the linguist, deciding what the final wording, casing, and punctuation should look like. TRADE then ties those two parts to one shared encoder so they see the same acoustic evidence instead of drifting apart.

The easiest way to picture it

Here is the core architecture in one glance.

The important thing to notice is not just that there are two branches. It is that both branches start from the same speech encoder. The split happens after the audio has already been turned into a shared representation.

That design matters because it gives TRADE two different kinds of control over the same utterance:

  • The LLM path handles language quality. It sees audio embeddings plus text context and learns to produce the transcript under cross-entropy supervision.
  • The transducer path handles timing. It uses the same encoder, but its blank symbol acts like a read gate, so it can decide whether to advance the acoustic frame or emit a token.

The paper also uses a streaming-friendly encoder recipe so this shared backbone works in both offline and streaming settings. The key trick is Copy-and-Append Data Augmentation, or CADA: each layer gets exactly one chunk of lookahead, but future context does not cascade through the whole network. Think of it as giving the encoder a small peek ahead without letting it cheat on the future.

Why TRADE needs two vocabularies

The next design choice is the one that makes the system practical. A transducer has to score candidate tokens at every frame, so a full 128K-token LLM vocabulary would make the lattice expensive very quickly. It would also force the transducer to carry tokens it should never emit on its own, such as punctuation, whitespace, and formatting symbols.

TRADE solves that by deriving a compact transducer vocabulary from the LLM vocabulary.

The idea is simple:

  • keep the original token IDs so the LLM embedding table and output head still make sense,
  • prune away tokens that are not acoustically realizable on the transducer side,
  • merge surface forms that have the same spoken realization, such as case variants or homophones,
  • leave non-verbalized tokens, like punctuation, for the LLM to recover at blank steps.

That gives you a much smaller transducer search space without throwing away the LLM’s ability to spell things properly. In other words, the transducer does not need to know whether the output should be OK or ok in advance. It only needs to know that the spoken word landed in that neighborhood, and the LLM can recover the surface form later.

This is why the paper keeps talking about “verbalized” and “non-verbalized” tokens. Verbalized tokens are the words the compact transducer can carry. Non-verbalized tokens are the punctuation and formatting that the LLM emits when the transducer says “read” instead of “write”.

When the two heads vote together

The collaboration between the transducer and the LLM is the most important part of TRADE. The transducer gives a frame-synchronous score, while the LLM gives a language score. Before the final decision, the LLM’s probability mass is collapsed over all surface forms that map to the same compact token, so spelling variants vote together instead of competing with each other.

The paper’s fusion rule can be read as a small formula card.

Purpose. Choose the next non-blank token by combining acoustic alignment from the transducer with linguistic preference from the LLM.

Intuition. The transducer says what the audio timing allows. The LLM says which surface form sounds right in context. TRADE picks the token that both sides support.

Formula.

hat{c} is the selected token. c is a candidate compact-token ID. V_trans is the compact transducer vocabulary. varnothing is the blank symbol, which means “do not emit yet, just read more audio.” w is the fusion weight. p^{trans}_c is the transducer probability for token c. tilde{p}^{LLM}_c is the LLM probability mass after homophone or surface-form variants have been merged into one compact token.

Read-aloud meaning. Pick the non-blank token whose weighted combination of transducer confidence and LLM confidence is highest.

Why it matters. This is what lets TRADE keep frame-synchronous timing without losing the LLM’s ability to recover casing, punctuation, and other surface details that the compact transducer vocabulary cannot represent by itself.

You can think of this as a two-expert vote. The transducer is the expert on when a word is allowed to appear. The LLM is the expert on how that word should look. The fusion rule lets the two experts agree in the same token space instead of forcing one to dominate the other.

The paper also uses the blank step to recover non-verbalized tokens. On a read step, the LLM can emit punctuation before the acoustic frame advances. That is why TRADE can produce punctuation timestamps instead of leaving punctuation as a post-processing cleanup job.

Training so streaming is not a special case

TRADE only works well because the training loop is shaped to match the inference loop. If you train a model on full utterances and then ask it to run chunk by chunk, the decoder state at inference time will not look like the decoder state it saw during training. The paper avoids that mismatch in two ways.

First, it trains with two losses at once. The LLM path learns with cross-entropy over the transcript. The transducer path learns with a pruned RNNT loss, which keeps the large vocabulary tractable by focusing on the most likely lattice arcs. So one branch learns to generate the right text, while the other learns to align that text with audio frames.

Second, it uses dynamic chunk training. Instead of fixing one chunk size, TRADE randomly samples from a range of chunk sizes during training, from low-latency settings up to full-context settings. That means the same checkpoint learns to behave sensibly across a whole latency spectrum instead of being overfit to just one operating point.

The paper also stops gradients from the transducer back into the LLM during streaming training. That sounds like a small implementation detail, but it is what keeps memory under control. Without it, the transducer backward pass would need to retain a separate LLM activation graph for every chunk. With gradient stopping, the hidden states passed to the joint network are detached, so peak memory stays close to offline training instead of growing with utterance length.

There is one more subtle point here: the LLM hidden state used by the transducer is taken from the position immediately before the verbalized token, which may sometimes be a non-verbalized token like punctuation. That is a neat way of making punctuation and timing cooperate instead of treating them as separate cleanup passes.

Keeping memory bounded when audio gets long

Streaming is not only about latency. Long-form ASR also needs memory to stay bounded as the recording gets longer. TRADE does that with Localized Decoder Audio Attention, or LDAA.

LDAA limits the LLM’s audio context to a sliding window of recent audio embeddings. The decoder only sees a fixed amount of left context, the current chunk, and one lookahead chunk, so the cache does not grow with the utterance length. The result is that the model can keep decoding for a long time without dragging the entire history along for the ride.

The paper chooses a 5 second left-context window, and that choice is not arbitrary. The support-span analysis shows that the 99th percentile of the acoustic evidence needed to commit to a token is 3.28 seconds, so 5 seconds leaves a comfortable margin. In plain terms, the model usually does not need to look further back than that to make a good decision.

For very long recordings, TRADE also caps the text side. It only conditions on the most recent 20 generated tokens, and a small repetition filter catches short n-gram loops before they turn into runaway repetition or hallucination. That is how the same checkpoint can move from short utterances to hours-long audio without external segmentation.

What decoding actually looks like

At runtime, the decoding loop is straightforward once the pieces are in place.

  1. Audio arrives in chunks.
  2. The shared encoder turns each chunk into audio features.
  3. The transducer decides whether to read more audio or emit a token.
  4. If it emits, TRADE uses the fusion rule to choose the best compact token.
  5. If it is on a blank step, the LLM can recover punctuation or other non-verbalized tokens before the frame advances.
  6. At each chunk boundary, the LLM is re-prefilled from the prompt, the recent audio window, and the current partial transcript.

Offline decoding is just the special case where the whole utterance is treated like one big chunk. That is why the paper can claim a single checkpoint works across offline, streaming, and long-form modes. The mode changes at inference time; the architecture does not have to change.

Why the paper matters

The experimental story is mostly there to confirm that the design is doing real work, not just looking clever on paper. The same checkpoint reaches 6.71% average WER on the Open ASR Leaderboard offline, 8.40% WER at 960 ms streaming, 3.64% WER on TED-LIUM, and 10.88% on Earnings-22 without external segmentation. It also gets punctuation timestamps that help end-of-utterance detection when they are fused with voice activity detection.

The ablations tell the same story in a quieter way. The fusion weight is fairly flat over a wide range, which suggests the transducer and LLM really are complementary. The 20K compact vocabulary is the best default, which means the vocabulary pruning is not just a compression trick; it is part of what keeps the transducer tractable while still preserving useful surface forms.

What to remember

If you only keep one picture in your head, make it this:

  • The transducer is the clock.
  • The LLM is the linguist.
  • The shared encoder gives them the same acoustic evidence.
  • The compact vocabulary keeps the transducer manageable.
  • LDAA and chunk-synchronized training make the same checkpoint usable for offline, streaming, and long-form ASR.

The main tradeoff is real. TRADE buys better timing control and bounded memory, but it adds architectural complexity, still has a streaming-versus-offline WER gap at small chunks, and was only evaluated in English. It is also compute-heavy because it sits on top of a 1B backbone.

So the memorable lesson is not “use a transducer because transducers are good.” It is simpler than that: if a speech model must both recognize speech and stream in real time, separate timing from wording. Let one part decide when to speak, and let the other part decide what to say.
